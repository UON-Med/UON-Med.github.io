Vitamin K deficiency bleeding of the newborn

Abstract

Vitamin K deficiency bleeding of the newborn (VKDB) is a bleeding disorder that arises from decreased vitamin K levels. This decrease can be caused by different factors: low levels of vitamin K in breast milk, underdeveloped intestinal flora (which produces vitamin K), limited liver storage capacity, and poor placental passage of vitamin K. Depending on when the bleeding occurs, VKDB is categorized as early-onset (within 24 hours after birth), classic (within 4 weeks), or late-onset (between 2–8 months). Bleeding can occur in any area of the body, including the head. The disease is rare in industrialized countries, where most children receive a vitamin K shot at birth. Treatment is focused on managing the bleeding with, e.g., transfusions and restoring bleeding homeostasis by administering vitamin K.

Epidemiology

  • Without prophylaxis: affects 0.25–2% of newborns

Epidemiological data refers to the US, unless otherwise specified.

Etiology

The underlying cause is always a deficiency of vitamin K, which can be due to various factors:

  • Exclusive breastfeeding: low vitamin K levels in breast milk; (most important in late-onset VKDB)
  • Low liver storage capacity
  • Poor placental passage of vitamin K
  • Vitamin K deficiency in the mother (e.g., because of anticonvulsant therapy; most important in early-onset VKDB; maternal malnutrition)
  • Underdeveloped intestinal flora (which produces vitamin K), e.g., due to premature birth
  • Chronic diarrhea of the newborn
  • Long-term antibiotic treatment in newborns
  • Cholestatic diseases (e.g., biliary atresia)

Clinical features

  • Early-onset: within 24 hours after birth; intracranial bleeding common
  • Classic: within 4 weeks after birth; intracranial bleeding rare
  • Late-onset: between 2–8 months after birth; intracranial bleeding common

References:[2][1]

Diagnostics

Coagulation studies:

  • ↑ Prothrombin time (PT)
  • Normal or ↑ activated partial thromboplastin time (PTT)
  • ↓ Factors II, VII, IX, and X

References:[1]

Treatment

  • Transfusions as necessary
  • Administration of vitamin K

References:[1]

Prevention

In the US, all newborns receive intramuscular vitamin K (0.5–1 mg) at birth.

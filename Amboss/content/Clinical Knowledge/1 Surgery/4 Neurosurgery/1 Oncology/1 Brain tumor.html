Brain tumors

Abstract

Brain tumors are masses of abnormal cells within the brain. These tumors may be primary or metastatic, and benign or malignant. Common tumors in children are pilocytic astrocytomas, meningiomas, medulloblastomas, ependymomas, and craniopharyngiomas. Adults most often present with glioblastoma multiforme, meningiomas, hemangioblastomas, schwannomas, oligodendrogliomas, and pituitary adenomas. Clinical features and radiological findings vary according to the type, location, and onset of the tumor. Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) is the primary diagnostic method. Complete remission is only possible with removal of the entire tumor. Malignant tumors usually require additional treatment with radiotherapy and/or chemotherapy. The prognosis can be estimated postoperatively via histological differentiation of the tumor using the World Health Organization (WHO) grading system (grades I–IV).

Astrocytomas, meningiomas, and neuromas are discussed in separate learning cards.

Epidemiology

  • Sex: ♂ > ♀ (except meningiomas)
  • Age
    • Children
      • Brain tumors account for ∼ 20% of cancer cases and are the primary cause of cancer deaths in children in the US.
      • Most common primary brain tumors: astrocytoma, medulloblastoma
    • Adults
      • Brain tumors account for ∼ 2% of cancer cases
      • Most common primary brain tumors: glioblastoma multiforme, meningioma
  • ∼ 70% of brain tumors are metastases, while ∼ 30% are primary brain tumors.
  • ∼ 40% of primary brain tumors are benign.

General clinical features of brain tumors

Symptoms depend on the type and location of the tumor (see “Differential diagnoses of brain tumors” below). Onset is usually insidious.

  • Generalized symptoms
    • Headaches (initially present in ∼ ⅓ of cases)
    • Behavioral changes and changes in personality
    • Symptoms of elevated intracranial pressure and brain herniation
  • Lateralized symptoms
    • Seizures and focal motor deficits
    • Aphasia
    • Sensory deficits (e.g., visual field defects)

Treatment

  • Surgical resection, radiotherapy and chemotherapy are the common treatment options (either alone or in combination).
  • Management of complications
    • Cerebral edema and ICP management
    • Prevention of recurring seizures (antiepileptic drugs)

Metal toxicity

Abstract

Exposure to toxic amounts of heavy metals such as mercury, lead, chrome, and cadmium can be harmful to the human body, potentially causing both acute symptoms (e.g., local irritation, gastroenteritis, and pneumonia) and long‑term effects (e.g., abnormal physical development, cancer, damage to central nervous system, and kidney). Toxic metals have many industrial purposes and therefore represent occupational hazards for a number of professions. Industrial pollution with heavy metals can affect the wider population through the contamination of food (e.g., mercury in fish) and water (e.g., lead, arsenic).

General considerations

  • Chronic exposure to heavy metals generally manifests first as CNS and kidney damage.
  • For chronic diseases resulting from the inhalation of metal dust, see pneumoconiosis.

Arsenic

  • Sources of exposure
    • Metal smelting; , pesticides, herbicides, contaminated water (esp. in developing countries), ore mining, semiconductor production, paint and glass manufacturing
  • Pathophysiology: has direct toxic effects on endothelial cells; induces oxidative stress, and disrupts ATP production
  • Clinical features
    • Acute toxicity
      • Nausea and vomiting
      • Abdominal pain
      • Tachycardia, QT prolongation
      • Garlic-like odor on the breath
      • CNS (coma, seizures)
    • Chronic toxicity
      • Carcinogenic
        • Lung cancer
        • Skin cancer (Bowen's disease, basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma)
        • Hepatic angiosarcomas
      • Arsenical keratosis: hyperkeratosis (especially on the soles and palms)
      • White bands across the nails (Mees lines)
      • Peripheral polyneuropathy
  • Diagnosis: urinalysis
  • Treatment
    • Consider gastric lavage, activated carbon in acute toxicity
    • Antidote: dimercaprol, succimer (in children)

References:[2][3][4][5]

Lead

  • Sources of exposure
    • Battery manufacturing, metallurgy, corrosion inhibition
    • Gun range
    • Tableware containing lead
    • Drinking water (from lead plumbing or contaminated sources )
    • Antique or imported toys painted with lead-containing paint
  • Action: heme synthesis disruption via inhibition of aminolevulinate dehydratase
  • Clinical features
    • Acute
      • Classic symptoms:
        • Severe abdominal pain (lead colic)
        • Black-blue line on the gums (lead line or Burton line)
        • Paralysis of the radial or peroneal nerve (wrist/foot drop)
        • Symptoms of anemia
      • Constipation
      • CNS manifestations: headache, fatigue, muscle weakness, paresthesias, delirium, seizures
    • Chronic
      • Many children are asymptomatic and should be assessed for blood lead levels if they have risk factors (see screening in high-risk pediatric populations below).
      • Symptoms of acute toxicity, plus
        • Hypertension
        • Neurological symptoms
          • Polyneuropathy
          • Encephalopathy
          • Exposure to lead in the womb and during childhood causes neurodevelopmental and growth disorders.
        • Kidneys: lead nephropathy, renal cell carcinoma
        • Reproductive organs: miscarriages, infertility
  • Diagnosis
    • Blood lead level (BLL) measurement
    • Basophilic stippling of erythrocytes on peripheral blood smear
    • Microcytic, hypochromic anemia
    • Imaging (not routinely indicated): lead flecks on abdominal x-ray, lead lines on long-bone x-rays
    • Inspection of the patient's environment for lead sources to prevent further exposure
  • Screening in high-risk pediatric populations
    • Living in older housing under renovation or with peeling paint
    • Sibling or playmate with lead poisoning
    • Recent immigrant, refugee, or foreign adoptee
    • History of pica
  • Treatment
    • Decrease exposure: professional lead paint abatement, dust reduction, minimization of contact with bare soil
    • Nutrition: Calcium, iron, zinc, and vitamins C and D-rich diet
    • Chelation therapy indications:
      • Oral succimer with/without intravenous or intramuscular CaNa2EDTA (Calcium disodium ethylenediamine tetraacetic acid) and D-penicillamine
        • Children with BLL ≥ 45 μg/dl
        • Asymptomatic adults with BLL ≥ 80 μg/dl
        • Symptomatic adults with BLL ≥ 50 μg/dl
      • IM dimercaprol (British antilewisite, BAL) with/without CaNa2EDTA
        • Patients with lead encephalopathy
        • BLL ≥ 70 μg/dl

Cadmium

  • Sources of exposure
    • Food, batteries, metallurgy, plastics and paint manufacturing, cigarette smoke
  • Clinical presentation
    • Acute toxicity: irritation of the airways (pulmonary edema, interstitial pneumonia)
    • Chronic toxicity
      • Kidney damage (tubulointerstitial nephritis), renal cell carcinoma
      • Lung disease (emphysema, carcinoma)
      • Anosmia
      • Osteoporosis/osteomalacia
      • Itai‑itai disease: chronic cadmium toxicity with kidney failure and osteomalacia (with aching joints and bones)
  • Diagnosis
    • Acute toxicity: ↑ blood cadmium
    • Chronic toxicity
      • ↑ Urine cadmium
      • Beta‑2 microglobulin in urine
  • Treatment: no known antidote

References:[21][22][23]

Iron

  • Definition: chemical element often used as supplement in pediatrics and prenatal care and in the management of anemia
  • Epidemiology: toxicity is common in pediatric care (ingestion of red iron tablets mistaken for candy)
  • Effect: corrosive and toxic effects on cells
  • Clinical features
    • Initially hemorrhagic gastroenteritis, shock symptoms
    • In case patient survives: symptom-free interval after 6 hours
    • Causes dark-colored stools
    • Later: risk of renal and liver failure, unconsciousness, and respiratory depression
  • Diagnostics: anion gap metabolic acidosis
  • Antidote: deferoxamine

References:[24]

Mercury

  • Sources of exposure
    • Organometallic methylmercury in fish
    • Inorganic, metallic mercury in thermometers, amalgam (dental fillings), pyrotechnics
  • Clinical features
    • Acute toxicity
      • Gastroenteritis (bloody diarrhea)
      • Kidney failure
      • Inflammation of the airways (from inhalation exposure)
    • Chronic toxicity (Minamata disease)
      • Glomerulonephritis (albuminuria)
      • CNS disorders: ataxia, erethism mercuralis (abnormal irritability) and tremor mercurialis
      • Polyneuropathy
      • Bluish‑violet discoloration of the margins of the gums
      • Teratogenicity
  • Diagnosis
    • Inorganic mercury in urine
    • Organic mercury in blood
  • Treatment
    • D‑penicillamine
  • Antidote
    • 2,3‑dimercaptosuccinic acid
    • Dimercaprol

References:[25][26][27][28][29]

Thallium

  • Sources of exposure
    • Glass industry
    • Rat poison
  • Clinical features
    • Hair loss
    • Polyneuropathy (“burning feet”)
    • Memory disorders
    • White bands across the nails (Mees's lines)
  • Diagnosis: present in blood and/or urine

Manganese

  • Sources of exposure: metallurgy, welding
  • Clinical features
    • Manganese pneumonia = fibrinous pneumonia
    • Manganism = parkinsonism (see secondary parkinsonism)
  • Manganese is an essential trace element required for health and well-being, but the body's demand is typically met with a normal diet.

Nickel

  • Sources of exposure: alloys (e.g., in coins, jewelry)
  • Clinical features
    • Acute toxicity: contact dermatitis
    • Chronic toxicity
      • Squamous‑cell carcinoma of the nose or paranasal sinuses
      • Lung cancer

Chromium

  • Sources of exposure
    • Galvanization (chrome plating), paint and glass manufacturing, tanning leather, building materials (potassium dichromate in cement → “cement eczema” is an occupational hazard among construction workers)
  • Action
    • Corrosive
    • Allergenic
  • Clinical features
    • Acute toxicity
      • Ulceration of the nasal septum from contact with contaminated fingers → danger of perforation
      • Contact dermatitis
    • Chronic toxicity
      • Welder's lung
      • Lung cancer
  • Diagnosis: urine or blood

Platinum

  • Sources of exposure
    • Jewelry
    • Catalytic converters
  • Effects
    • Obstructive lung diseases (immediate reaction allergy)
    • Nephrotoxicity

Vanadium (vanadium pentoxide)

  • Exposure:
    • Metallurgy, alloys
  • Consequences
    • Acute
      • Mucous membrane irritation
      • Bronchitis
      • Bronchopneumonia
    • Chronic
      • Welder's lung
      • Pulmonary fibrosis
      • Blackish‑green discoloration of the tongue
  • Diagnosis: present in blood and/or urine

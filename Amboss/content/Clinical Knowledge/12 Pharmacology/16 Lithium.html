Lithium

Abstract

Lithium is a psychiatric medication used primarily as first-line therapy for bipolar affective disorders. It is also indicated in treatment-resistant depression (TRD) and reduces the risk of suicide in depressive patients. The specific mechanism by which lithium acts to stabilize mood is still not definitively known. It is thought to be due to the inhibition of the phosphoinositide cascade causing a decrease in cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP) levels. Short-term side effects of lithium are predominantly gastrointestinal, while long-term side effects involve neurological symptoms. Lithium therapy has a very narrow therapeutic index; frequent monitoring is therefore required to prevent toxicity.

Lithium intoxication

  • Etiology: : serum lithium levels > 1.5 mEq/L / 1.5â2.0 mmol/L
    • Overdosage due to frequent intake
    • Renal impairment
    • Low sodium diet or increased perspiration
    • Diuretic therapy (e.g., loop or thiazide diuretics)
  • Pathophysiology: : impaired concentration of urine (due to lithium nephropathy; ) + uncompensated fluid losses (due to confusion or disabling comorbidities) â severe dehydration â lithium toxicity
  • Clinical features
    • Lithium intoxication can be explained under three categories, as follows
      • Acute toxicity: : seen in patients with a low tissue level of lithium (not on regular therapy), who have acutely taken a large dose
      • Acute on chronic toxicity: : seen in patients who are on chronic lithium therapy with a low tissue level, but have taken a larger dose acutely
      • Chronic toxicity: : seen in patients with a high tissue level of the drug (long term treatment) with recent high doses, or the introduction of new therapy that exacerbates lithium effects
    • System-specific description of features
      • Neurological (earliest and predominant feature): dysarthria; , vertigo, ataxia, nystagmus (especially downbeat nystagmus), dysdiadochokinesia, tremor, somnolence, delirium.
      • Neuromuscular: fine tremors, muscle twitches (fasciculations)
      • Gastrointestinal: vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain
      • Psychiatric: apathy, psychomotor impairment
      • Others : dysrhythmias, renal failure with oliguria, myxedema coma
  • Treatment
    • Symptomatic therapy
      • Correct fluid and electrolyte balance
      • Ventilatory support, if required
      • Treat seizures and/or dysrhythmias
    • In severe intoxication
      • IV fluid therapy: 0.9% NaCl solution (possibly in combination with forced diuresis)
      • Hemodialysis
        • Indications
          • Serum lithium concentration > 4.0 mEq/L
          • Kidney dysfunction
          • Altered mental status; , seizures, and/or life-threatening arrhythmias
          • Expected time required to reduce serum lithium concentration to < 1.0 mEq/L is < 36 hours
        • Hemodialysis; should be continued until serum lithium; concentration is < 1.0 mEq/L or for a mininum of 6 hours (if serum lithium concentration cannot be measured)

Indications

  • First-line therapy for bipolar affective disorder
    • Prevention of acute manic episodes in bipolar disorders
    • Prophylaxis for depression in bipolar disorders
  • Lithium augmentation in TRD
  • Combination with antidepressant in suicidal patients as prophylaxis to prevent recurrence
  • SIADH

References:[1][2][3]

Contraindications

  • Absolute contraindications
    • Advanced renal failure
    • Severe cardiovascular disease
  • Relative contraindications
    • Low-sodium diet, or in instances of sodium deprivation (fever, diarrhea , vomiting, diuretic therapy)
    • Hypothyroidism
    • Addison's disease
    • Drugs that decrease lithium excretion
      • NSAIDs (with the exception of low-dose acetylsalicylic acid [ASA])
      • ACE inhibitors
      • Diuretics

If prophylaxis of a bipolar disorder is needed in a case with an absolute contraindication against lithium, anticonvulsants (e.g., lamotrigine) can be used as an alternative drug!

Special patient groups

  • Pregnancy
    • First trimester intake: Ebstein anomaly (and other congenital defects like septal dysgenesis)
    • Second and third trimester intake: goiter

Before prescribing lithium in young women, evaluate thyroid function, renal function, and human chorionic gonadotropin levels to rule out pregnancy!

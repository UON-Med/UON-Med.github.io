Congenital adrenal hyperplasia

Abstract

Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH) comprises a group of autosomal recessive defects in enzymes that are responsible for cortisol, aldosterone, and, in very rare cases, androgen synthesis. All forms of CAH are characterized by low levels of cortisol, high levels of ACTH, and adrenal hyperplasia. The exact clinical manifestations depend on the enzyme defect. The most common form of CAH, which is caused by a deficiency of 21Î²-hydroxylase, presents with hypotension, ambiguous genitalia, and virilization (in the female genotype), and/or precocious puberty (in both males and females). It is further characterized by hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, and metabolic acidosis. Increased levels of 17-hydroxyprogesterone confirm the diagnosis of 21Î²-hydroxylase deficiency. All newborn infants in the United States are screened for 21Î²-hydroxylase deficiency by measuring 17-hydroxyprogesterone in a blood sample obtained from a heel prick. Treatment of CAH involves lifelong glucocorticoid replacement therapy. Patients with a 21Î²-hydroxylase deficiency also require mineralocorticoid replacement with fludrocortisone. Certain rare forms of CAH (e.g., 11Î²-hydroxylase and 17Î±-hydroxylase deficiencies) present with symptoms of mineralocorticoid excess (e.g., hypertension) and therefore require spironolactone (aldosterone receptor inhibitor) in addition to glucocorticoid replacement. Patients with gender identity issues may benefit from counseling. Surgical reconstruction of the genitals may be considered in cases of ambiguous genitalia. Complications of CAH include severe hypoglycemia, adrenal insufficiency, and/or a failure to thrive.

Treatment

  • For all forms: lifelong glucocorticoid replacement therapy
    • Hydrocortisone in neonates and children
    • Prednisolone or dexamethasone in adolescents and adults
  • 21Î²-hydroxylase deficiency: lifelong fludrocortisone therapy (aldosterone substitution)
  • 11Î²-hydroxylase deficiency:
    • Spironolactone to block mineralocorticoid receptors
    • Reduce dietary sodium intake
  • 17Î±-hydroxylase deficiency
    • Spironolactone to block mineralocorticoid receptors
    • Reduce dietary sodium intake
    • Estrogen replacement therapy for female genotype; may be commenced in early puberty
  • Counseling may be considered for potential gender identity issues
  • Genital reconstruction surgery may be considered in children with ambiguous genitalia.

The dose of glucocorticoids must be increased during periods of stress (e.g., sepsis)!

Prenatal diagnosis and treatment of CAH

  • Prenatal testing is recommended in mothers who have previously given birth to a child with 21Î²-hydroxylase deficiency.
    • Chorionic villus sampling
    • â 17Î±-hydroxyprogesterone in amniotic fluid
  • If a defect in 21Î²-hydroxylase was diagnosed prenatally:
    • Prophylactic treatment of the mother with dexamethasone soon after conception
    • Dexamethasone therapy should be discontinued if the fetus has a male genotype (revealed by karyotyping).

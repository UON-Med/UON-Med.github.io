Clostridium difficile infection

Abstract

Content currently under development

Clostridium difficile (C. difficile) is a gram-positive rod-shaped bacillus that is commonly involved in antibiotic-associated diarrhea. As the bacterial spores are difficult to eradicate and easily transmitted (via fecal-oral transmission), the C. difficile infection rate is particularly high among hospitalized patients and residents in long-term care facilities. Colonization with C. difficile occurs following antibiotic treatment of other diseases, as the bacteria is particularly resistant to antibiotics. The resulting damage to the intestinal flora promotes infection, which may be accompanied by high fever, abdominal pain, and characteristically foul-smelling diarrhea. The most severe form of C. difficile infection is pseudomembranous colitis, which may lead to ileus, sepsis, and toxic megacolon. In most cases, however, colonization results in asymptomatic carriage rather than symptomatic infection. Diagnosis is usually made via detection of the C. difficile toxin in stool samples. If the toxin cannot be detected but an infection is clinically suspected, a colonoscopy may reveal typical pseudomembranes. C. difficile infections are treated with oral metronidazole and, in severe cases, oral vancomycin. Following diagnosis, strict adherence to hygiene measures and patient isolation is essential, especially in hospitals and other healthcare settings.

Epidemiology

Content currently under development

  • ∼ 3 million cases in the US per year, causing ∼ 14,000 deaths[1][2]
  • Hospital-acquired infections especially affect the elderly (> 65 years) [3]
  • Patients affected by community-acquired infections are typically younger (average age 50 years).
  • The incidence, severity, and likelihood of treatment-resistant C. difficile colitis are rising, both in healthcare-associated and community-associated settings.

Epidemiological data refers to the US, unless otherwise specified.

Etiology

Content currently under development

C. difficile infection typically requires a combination of factors: fecal-oral contamination, colonization after the intestinal flora are compromised, and pathogenic toxin release.

  • Pathogen: Clostridium difficile[4]
    • Gram-positive rod-shaped bacillus, obligate anaerobe, toxigenic organism
    • Forms environmentally resistant spores (capable of withstanding heat and acid)
    • Highly contagious
  • Route of infection: fecal-oral transmission of ubiquitous bacteria[4]
    • Hospital-acquired infections: transmission via contaminated hands of healthcare workers or patients, contaminated surfaces/furniture, and medical equipment (see "Prevention" below)
  • Community-acquired infections
    • Emergence of severe cases in individuals without typical risk factors (antibiotic treatment, hospitalization)
  • Antibiotic treatment [5][4]
    • Recent or ongoing treatment of unrelated diseases (e.g., ear, nose, throat infections; pneumonia)
      •
    • Common triggers; : clindamycin; , cephalosporins, fluoroquinolones, and less commonly, penicillin[1][5]
  • Other risk factors
    • Advanced age
    • Gastric acid suppression (e.g., with proton pump inhibitors) or bypass (e.g., enteral feeding)
    • Severe illnesses
    • Immune suppression
    • Inflammatory bowel disease

Colonization with C. difficile results in asymptomatic carriage more often than manifest disease, as symptomatic infection requires the presence of further risk factors. In both cases, individuals test positive for the toxin or the pathogen itself!

Clinical features

Content currently under development

Symptoms of C. difficile-associated diarrhea (CDAD) usually develop during antibiotic treatment or 2–10 days following its initiation; however, 25–40% of cases may manifest as late as 10 weeks following treatment.

  • Watery diarrhea, characteristically foul-smelling
    • Mild disease presents with ≥ 3 stools/day whereas patients with fulminant colitis may have up to 20 stools per day
    • May have traces of mucus or occult blood
    • Hematochezia or melena are rare
  • Cramping abdominal pain, nausea, anorexia
  • Fever and dehydration (especially in severe cases)
  • Fulminant colitis: abdominal distention and severe hypovolemia
  • Recurrent disease: reoccurrence of symptoms following end of treatment (10–40% of cases)

Diagnostics

Content currently under development

The patient history and clinical presentation are usually strong indicators of the diagnosis, which is then confirmed with identification of the pathogen or corresponding toxins in stool. Further diagnostics, such as blood tests or imaging, may be used to assess the severity of disease or the presence of complications.

History[1]

  • Treatment with antibiotics in the last 3 months
  • Recent hospitalization

Stool tests

  • Screening tests (performed together on liquid stool samples) [6]
    • Enzyme immunoassay (EIA) for glutamate dehydrogenase antigen[6] (good sensitivity)
    • EIA for C. difficile toxins A and B[6] (good specificity)
  • Confirmatory tests (performed on liquid stool samples)
    • Bacterial culture of C. difficile [1][2][6]
    • PCR for C. difficile genes[6]

Blood tests

  • Leukocytosis [1][6][8][7][9]
  • ↑ Serum creatinine (possible kidney injury caused by dehydration)[8][9]
  • ↓ Serum albumin[8][7]
  • Electrolyte imbalance, particularly hypokalemia (caused by severe diarrhea)[1]
  • ↑ Lactate levels[1][7]

Imaging studies

  • Abdominal x-ray/CT scan: detection of toxic megacolon, abscesses, or perforation[6][7]
  • Colonoscopy (or sigmoidoscopy)[6]
    • Only indicated if C.difficile is suspected, but not detected in stool samples
    • Approach cautiously (↑ risk of perforation)
    • Findings
      • Erythematous and friable mucosa
      • Typical pseudomembranes, often only individual findings; widespread lesions in severe cases (pseudomembranous colitis)

Treatment

Content currently under development

General measures[1][7]

  • Discontinue the precipitating antibiotic
  • Fluid replacement
  • Avoid antidiarrheals (e.g., loperamide)
  • Avoid or cease treatment with proton pump inhibitors (PPI) [4]

Medical therapy

  • If clinical suspicion for CDAD is high, empiric antibiotic treatment can be initiated without waiting for laboratory confirmation of C. difficile.[7]
  • Mild to moderate cases
    • Discontinuing the antibiotic causing symptoms may suffice[1]
    • 1st-line: oral metronidazole[1][8][7]
    • 2nd-line: oral vancomycin (if metronidazole fails)[8][7][9]
    • In cases of gastrointestinal dysfunction (e.g., ileus, megacolon): IV metronidazole plus vancomycin per rectum (enema)
  • Severe cases: 1st-line: oral vancomycin [8][10][7][9]
    • 2nd-line: fidaxomicin or rifaximin

C. difficile infection is one of the rare indications for oral administration of vancomycin![10]

Fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT)
  • Indication:
    • Failure of several (different) antibiotic regimens
    • Recurrent C.difficile infections without achieving permanent remission (rescue therapy)
  • Implementation: Sterile normal saline solution is added to donor stool and blended to a smooth, liquid consistency.
  • Administration
    • Enema
    • Colonoscopy
    • Nasogastric or jejunal tube

Complications

Content currently under development

  • Toxic megacolon[11]
  • Paralytic ileus[6]
  • Peritonitis, perforation, abscess formation, sepsis[6] : may necessitate surgery (subtotal colectomy and terminal ileostomy)

Prevention

Content currently under development

  • Detection: C. difficile toxin stool test for at-risk patients with recent onset of diarrhea
  • Isolation
    • Single-bed room with designated bathroom facilities (up to 2 days after symptoms subside)
    • Cohort isolation is possible if control measures are implemented.
  • Control measures
    • Personal protective equipment/contact precautions: gloves and protective gown (change after each patient); a mask is not necessary
    • Hand hygiene (applies to caregivers, patients, and contact persons): thorough washing with soap and water
      • C. difficile spores are resistant to alcoholic disinfectants.
    • Consistent disinfection of possibly contaminated surfaces with sporicidal oxidants, such as peracetic acid or sodium hypochlorite
      • Autoclaving is also sporicidal and can be used to sterilize larger equipment

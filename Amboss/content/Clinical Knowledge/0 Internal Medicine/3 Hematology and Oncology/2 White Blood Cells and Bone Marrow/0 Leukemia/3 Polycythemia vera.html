Polycythemia vera

Abstract

Polycythemia vera (PV) is a chronic myeloproliferative neoplasm that is characterized by an erythropoietin (EPO)-independent, irreversible increase in erythrocyte, granulocyte, and platelet counts. The elevated blood cell mass results in hyperviscosity, which is associated with slow blood flow, increased risk of thrombosis, and poor oxygenation. The disorder typically has a chronic, insidious course with a long intial asymptomatic period. Clinical features include facial flushing, pruritus, erythromelalgia, headaches, and dizziness. Severe complications such as thrombotic events (e.g., stroke, Budd-Chiari syndrome) may occur. Since PV is an incurable disorder, the primary goals of management involve controlling RBC mass and reducing the risk of thrombotic complications. Periodic phlebotomy reduces the amount of circulating cells and is therefore the treatment of choice.

Pathophysiology

  • The JAK2 (Janus kinase 2) gene codes for a tyrosine kinase in hematopoietic progenitor cells, which is essential for the regulation of erythropoiesis, thrombopoiesis (megakaryopoiesis), and granulopoiesis.
  • 95% of PV patients have a mutation in the JAK2 gene → ↑ tyrosine kinase activity → uncontrolled, EPO-independent proliferation of the myeloid cell lines → ↑ blood cell mass (erythrocytosis, thrombocytosis, and granulocytosis) → hyperviscosity + slow blood flow → ↑ risk of thrombosis and poor oxygenation.
    • The JAK2 V617F mutation is the most common mutation.

Clinical features

  • Often asymptomatic (incidental finding in routine blood tests!)
  • Constitutional symptoms: weight loss, fatigue, sweating
  • Hyperviscosity syndrome (triad of mucosal bleeding, neurological symptoms, and visual changes)
  • Plethora
    • Facial flushing
    • Cyanotic lips
  • Pruritus: Itching typically worsens when the skin comes into contact with warm water (aquagenic pruritus).
  • Unspecific neurological symptoms : dizziness, headache, visual disturbances, tinnitus
  • Hypertension
  • Splenomegaly : associated with early satiety and abdominal discomfort, less commonly hepatomegaly
  • Peptic ulcer disease
  • Symptoms of thrombotic and hemorrhagic complications (see “Complications” below)

PV is associated with an increased risk of thrombosis and bleeding!

Diagnostics

Laboratory findings[2][7]

  • ↑ Hb/Hct, ↑ RBCs
  • ↑ Platelets (> 450,000/μl)
  • ↑ Leukocytes (> 12,000/μl)
  • ↑ Uric acid, ↑ LDH
  • ↑ Leukocyte alkaline phosphatase
  • ↓ Serum iron levels
  • ↓ ESR
  • ↓ EPO
  • Arterial O2 saturation: normal

Diagnostic criteria[8]

For the diagnosis to be established, both major criteria or one major plus at least two minor criteria must be fulfilled.

  • Major criteria
    1. Evidence of increased RBCs
      • Men: Hb > 18.5 g/dL
      • Women: Hb> 16.5 g/dL
      • Hb or Hct > 99th percentile
    2. Evidence of a genetic mutation in the JAK2 gene
  • Minor Criteria
    1. Bone marrow: hypercellularity (increased erythropoiesis, granulopoiesis, and megakaryopoiesis) in fine needle aspiration cytology
    2. ↓ EPO levels
    3. Detection of erythroid colony formation in vitro

Treatment

  • Phlebotomy: Periodic phlebotomy temporarily reduces cell counts and hyperviscosity.
    • 250–500 mL in intervals of 2–3 days until a HCT of ≤ 0.45 is established[10]
      • The need for application of phlebotomy depends on HCT levels.
        • Iron deficiency is intentionally induced.
        • Iron substitution is only indicated if the patient displays obvious symptoms.
        • Alternative: erythrocytapheresis
        • Contraindications
          • Anemia
          • Hypotension and dehydration
          • Acute infections and acute emergencies
  • Antiplatelet prophylaxis: aspirin
  • Cytoreductive therapy: decide on an individual basis; recommended if risk factors are present
    • Hydroxyurea
    • Interferon alpha
    • JAK2 inhibitor: ruxolitinib

If platelet count is > 1 million/μl, platelet function is very likely to be impaired, and the risk of bleeding is significantly increased. Aspirin should be discontinued!

Complications

Polycythemia vera is a life-threatening disease due to the numerous complications associated with it.

Thrombotic complications

  • Venous thrombosis
    • Renal vein thrombosis
    • Mesenteric venous thrombosis
    • Portal vein obstruction
    • Splenic vein thrombosis
    • Deep vein thrombosis
    • Pulmonary embolism
    • Budd-Chiari syndrome
  • Arterial thrombosis
    • Stroke
    • Peripheral arterial emboli
    • Myocardial infarction (MI)

Hemorrhagic complications

  • Petechiae
  • Epistaxis
  • Bleeding gums

Late stages

  • Acute myeloid leukemia (AML), myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS): Additional genetic mutations may cause polycythemia vera to transition to other hematological diseases.
  • Primary myelofibrosis: fibrotic transformation of the bone marrow

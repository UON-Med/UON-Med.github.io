Beta blockers (Beta antagonists)

Abstract

Beta blockers are a group of drugs that inhibit the sympathetic activation of β-adrenergic receptors. Cardioselective blockers (e.g., atenolol, bisoprolol) primarily block β1 receptors in the heart, causing decreased heart rate and cardiac contractility, slower AVN conduction, and decreased cardiac workload. Nonselective β blockers (e.g., pindolol, propranolol) inhibit all β receptors and may cause bronchoconstriction, peripheral vasoconstriction, and metabolic imbalances (e.g., hyperglycemia, hypertriglyceridemia) in addition to cardiac effects. Cardioselective β blockers have a lower side effect profile and are preferred in the management of coronary heart disease, compensated heart failure, acute coronary syndrome, and in certain types of arrhythmias. Propranolol, a nonselective β blocker, is the first-line drug in the management of essential tremor, portal hypertension, migraine prophylaxis, and thyroid storm. Beta blockers are contraindicated in patients with symptomatic bradycardia, AV block, decompensated heart failure, and asthma. Initiation and cessation of β blocker therapy should always be gradual to avoid side effects or symptoms of withdrawal (rebound tachycardia, hypertension, acute cardiac death).

Specific effects
  • Block β1, β2, and β3 receptors
  • Cause bronchoconstriction: may exacerbate asthma/COPD
  • Cause vasoconstriction: avoid in patients with peripheral vascular disease (PVD)
  • Can cause hypo- and hyperglycemia
  • Selectively bind to and block β1 receptors, which are primarily found in the heart
  • Do not cause bronchoconstriction or vasoconstriction
  • Do not interfere with glycogenolysis; safe in diabetics
  • Cardioselectivity is dose-dependent
  • Potent vasodilators
    • Due to their α blocking action; vasodilation → ↓ peripheral vascular resistance, ↓ preload and afterload, and improved renal blood flow
  • Can cause orthostatic hypotension
  • Improve endothelial function and vascular remodeling
  • β blockers with intrinsic sympathomimetic activity
    • Bind to and stimulate the β-adrenergic receptor themselves (agonistic effect), while competitively inhibiting the binding of epinephrine and norepinephrine to β-adrenergic receptors (antagonistic effect)
    • Cause less bradycardia (due to their mild agonistic action)
    • Cause less peripheral vasoconstriction
    • Have a favorable effect on lipid profile (esp. pindolol and acebutolol): preferred in patients with metabolic syndrome
    • Are not recommended in patients with angina, ischemic heart disease or tachyarrhythmias

Beta blockers competitively bind to and block β-adrenergic receptors, thereby inhibiting sympathetic (adrenergic and/or noradrenergic) stimulation of β receptors.

Beta blocker withdrawal

  • Caused by sudden termination of β blockers
  • Clinical features: : tachycardia, tachyarrhythmia, hypertension, acute coronary syndrome, sudden cardiac death
  • Prevention: : taper dose over 7–10 days before discontinuing

Beta blocker overdose

  • Clinical features: : bradycardia/bradyarrhythmia, cardiogenic shock (hypotension; cold, clammy extremities), hypoglycemia, hyperkalemia, wheezing (bronchoconstriction), neurological symptoms (seizure, delirium, coma)
  • Treatment
    • Secure airway
    • Correct hypotension: IV fluids and vasopressors (epinephrine)
    • Correct bradycardia: IV atropine
    • Treat cardiogenic shock: IV glucagon ; IV calcium salts (improves cardiac contractility)
    • Correct hypoglycemia: IV high-dose insulin with glucose
    • Prevent further absorption of β blocker: activated charcoal/gastric lavage ; IV lipid emulsions (esp. useful in lipophilic β blocker overdose)
    • Poisoning with hydrophilic β blockers (e.g., atenolol, nadolol) may require hemodialysis for removal of the drug from circulation.

Indications

Cardiovascular indications

  • Hypertension The European Society of Hypertension guidelines approve of beta blockers as a first line antihypertensive in non-black patients < 60 years.
  • Coronary artery disease
    • Acute myocardial infarction: β blockers should be initiated early in all patients (without contraindications) and continued long-term if tolerated.
    • Angina pectoris: first-line treatment for stable angina pectoris in addition to ACE inhibitors or angiotensin-receptor blockers
    • Heart failure: as an adjunct with ACE-I/ARB and spironolactone (cardioselective β blockers are preferred)
    • Antiarrhythmic agents: all patients with atrial flutter/fibrillation, PSVT, VT, and premature ventricular contractions

Specific indications for propranolol

  • Essential tremor
  • Migraine prophylaxis
  • Portal hypertension
  • Hyperthyroidism and thyroid storm

Miscellaneous

  • Hypertensive crises; (e.g., malignant hypertension): IV labetalol (rapid onset of action)
  • Glaucoma: topical β blockers (timolol, betaxolol)

Beta blockers should be introduced gradually with slow increases in dosage, and slowly tapered off and stopped when their use is no longer needed!

Contraindications

Absolute contraindications Relative contraindications
  • Symptomatic bradycardia
  • Sick sinus syndrome (without a pacemaker); heart block greater than first-degree
  • Cardiogenic shock and hypotension
  • Pheochromocytoma
  • Decompensated heart failure
  • Asthma
  • Combination with calcium channel blockers (diltiazem or verapamil): can precipitate AV block
  • Psoriasis
  • Raynaud's phenomenon, peripheral artery occlusive disease
  • Pregnancy (atenolol is absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy)

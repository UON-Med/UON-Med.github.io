Antiarrhythmic drugs

Abstract

Content currently under development

Antiarrhythmic drugs are classified into 5 classes according to their electrophysiological effect on the myocardium. The original classification system, which was developed by Vaughn Williams in 1970, consisted of four classes. The fifth class was added to accommodate new antiarrhythmic drugs developed since 1970. Antiarrhythmic drugs are used to restore sinus rhythm in patients presenting with cardiac arrhythmias, as well as to prevent recurrent arrhythmias. Antiarrhythmic drugs do not improve the survival of patients with non-life-threatening arrhythmias, and may increase mortality, particularly in patients with structural heart disease. They are associated with severe adverse effects, primarily due to the proarrhythmic effect they exert on the myocardium. Intravenous application should, therefore, be monitored with ECG. This card provides an overview of the classification system. Several of the drug classes also have other medical uses. Details regarding these drugs, namely beta blockers, calcium channel blockers, amiodarone, and lidocaine, can be found in the corresponding learning cards.

Classes of antiarrhythmic drugs

Class Drug group Main mechanism of action Examples Use
Class IA
  • Sodium channel blocker
  • Prolong action potential duration (APD)
  • Slow conduction velocity
  • Quinidine
  • Procainamide
  • Disopyramide
  • Paroxysmal supraventricular tachycardia (PSVT)
  • Atrial fibrillation (AFib)
  • Atrial flutter
  • Ventricular tachycardia (VT)
Class IB
  • Sodium channel blocker
  • Shorten APD
  • No effect on conduction velocity
  • Lidocaine
  • Mexiletine
  • VT
Class IC
  • Sodium channel blocker
  • Mild increase in APD
  • Slow conduction velocity
  • Flecainide
  • Propafenone
  • PSVT
  • AFib (cardioversion): Structural heart disease must be ruled out prior to use!
  • Atrial flutter
Class II
  • Beta blocker
  • Inhibit adrenergic effect → negative dromotropic effect (slows conduction velocity in the AV node)
  • Metoprolol
  • Esmolol
  • Propranolol
  • AFib (frequency control)
  • Atrial flutter
  • PSVT
  • Premature ventricular contractions
  • VT
Class III
  • Potassium channel blocker
  • Prolonged repolarization of the cardiac action potential
  • Amiodarone
  • Sotalol
  • Dronedarone
  • Bretylium
  • Ibutilide, dofetilide
  • AFib (cardioversion and long-term rhythm control)
  • Atrial flutter
  • VT (not bretylium); see also advanced cardiac life support)
  • Sotalol: supraventricular arrhythmias and ventricular arrhythmias
  • Ibutilide, dofetilide: AFib (cardioversion and maintenance of sinus rhythm after successful cardioversion)
Class IV
  • Calcium channel blocker
  • Slow conduction velocity (in the AV node)
  • Verapamil
  • Diltiazem
  • AFib (frequency control)
  • Atrial flutter
  • PSVT
  • Multifocal atrial tachycardia
Class V
  • Variable mechanisms
  • See “Other antiarrhythmic drugs” below for details.
  • Adenosine
  • Digoxin
  • Magnesium sulfate
  • See “Other antiarrhythmic drugs” below for details.

All antiarrhythmic drugs are also potentially proarrhythmic! Administer under ECG monitoring!

Class I antiarrhythmics are contraindicated in patients with a history of structural heart defects, MI, and/or CHF because they can exacerbate the cardiac condition, and increase the mortality and cardiac arrest rate!

Other antiarrhythmic drugs

Content currently under development

Adenosine (drug)

  • Mechanism of action: transient AV node block
    • Potassium channel activation → ↑ potassium efflux → hyperpolarization → ↓ calcium influx → decrease in intracellular cAMP → acute termination of supraventricular tachycardia
  • Administration:
    • Rapid bolus IV (very short half-life: < 10 seconds)
    • May be administered repeatedly if previous dose was unsuccessful
    • Warn patients of unpleasant feeling that can follow administration. They may be feel a sense of impending doom.
  • Indications:
    • Drug of choice for narrow QRS tachycardia (paroxysmal supraventricular tachycardia: AVNRT and orthodromic AVRT)
    • Diagnostic value: exposing underlying AFib in supraventricular tachyarrhythmias
    • Adenosine also has strong physiologic effects on the kidney, particularly constricting the afferent arteriole and triggering profound natriuresis
    • Pharmacological stress test in myocardial perfusion scintigraphy
  • Adverse Effects:
    • Chest pain, flushing, hypotension, bronchospasm
    • Sense of impending doom
    • Asystole (rare)
  • Contraindications
    • Pre-excitation syndrome: antidromic AVRT including Wolf-Parkinson-White syndrome
    • AV block
    • Asthma

Digoxin

  • Mechanism of action: Inhibition of Na+/K+-ATPases → higher intracellular Na+ concentration → reduced efficacy of Na+/Ca2+ exchangers → higher intracellular Ca2+ concentration → increased contractility, decreased heart rate
  • Indications: tachyarrhythmias (e.g., AFib)
  • See cardiac glycosides for details.

Magnesium sulfate

  • Mechanism of action: decreases calcium influx → prevents early afterdepolarizations
  • Indications:
    • Torsades de pointes
    • Eclampsia
    • Constipation
    • Tocolysis

Adaptive immune system

Abstract

Content currently under development

The human body is host to innumerable microorganisms, including pathogens, and natural processes like eating and breathing expose it to a millions more external ones at all times. To protect the body from disease, humans and other animals possess a complex immune system that involves a variety of structures and processes. Mechanical barriers, i.e., the skin and mucous membranes, and chemical barriers such as the gastric acid represent the body's first line of defense against pathogens. If these fail and pathogens manage to enter the tissue or blood stream, these are attacked by white blood cells, which are categorized by their physical and functional characteristics as lymphocytes (comprising B cells, various subtypes of T cells, and natural killer cells), neutrophils, eosinophils, basophils, and monocytes.
Immunity against pathogens can be innate or acquired. Innate immunity is immediate, but nonspecific, and operates based on inherited cellular receptors that respond to broad pathogen-related patterns and common threat signals. Acquired immunity (adaptive immunity), on the other hand, develops after initial exposure to a pathogen and is highly specific to that pathogen. This specificity allows for a more effective immune response that can achieve clearance of the pathogen. Immune memory is a mechanism of the adaptive immune system that triggers a more rapid and extensive immune response to a pathogen upon subsequent exposure. Acquired immunity can be conferred via vaccination, which induces immunity through selective exposure to antigens that have been rendered innocuous.
Disorders of the immune system include autoimmune conditions, which are caused by abnormal immune responses to the body's own tissue, and immunodeficiency conditions, in which a weakened immune system leaves body vulnerable to pathogens and frequent infection.

Overview

Unlike the innate immune system, the acquired immune system becomes active several days after pathogen invasion and is typically more specific in its response. For adaptive immunity to be conferred, antigens (peptide fragments) are first presented on the surface of antigen-presenting cells (e.g., macrophages, dendritic cells); these in turn trigger activation and differentiation of lymphocytes (e.g., T cells and B cells). Another important aspect of the adaptive immune system is memory. In reinfection with a previously encountered pathogen, the acquired immune system can recognize the pathogen and mount an immediate response.

The cells of the acquired immune system (B cells, T cells) are dependent on external stimulation!

T cells [1] [2]

T cells (T lymphocytes) originate in the bone marrow and undergo maturation and differentiation in the thymus (hence “T cells”). The thymus is a primary lymphatic organ and grows during childhood after which it begins to atrophy (thymic involution). In young children, thoracic x-ray normally shows a prominent thymic shadow.

  • Differentiated T cells express specific T-cell receptors that allow them to recognize antigens presented by MHC molecules on the surface of antigen-presenting cells (e.g., macrophages).
  • General T cell markers: CD3, CD28, TCR
  • Surface proteins determine T cell function.
    • CD4+ T helper cells; : support B cells; and non-specific immune cells
      • Th1 cells: fight intracellular pathogens
        • Activate macrophages
        • Activate cytotoxic T cells
      • Th2 cells: fight extracellular parasites
        • Stimulate eosinophils → production of IgE
      • Th17 cells: fight extracellular pathogens
        • Stimulate neutrophils
      • TFHcells: support B cells in lymphoid follicles
        • Stimulate B cell isotype switching and antibody production in the follicles
      • Treg cells: regulate T cell activity, inhibit autoimmunity
        • Promote immune self-tolerance
          • Prevent formation of autoantibodies
    • CD8+ cytotoxic T cells: recognize and kill virus-infected or neoplastic cells

T cell subtypes

All T cells carry specific membrane-bound marker proteins that distinguish them from other lymphocytes. These general T cell markers are CD3, CD28 (which binds to B7 during T cell activation) and the T cell receptor (TCR). The two major T cell types are the cytotoxic T cells (CD8+) and T helper cells (CD4+, CD40L+, CXCR4/CCR5+). Subpopulations within the CD4+ subset have been identified by the cytokines they secrete or their surface markers (the following list is not exhaustive).

CD8 proteins on the surface of cytotoxic T cells interact with MHC I receptors, while CD4 proteins on the surface of T helper cells interact with MHC II receptors.

Rule of 8: MHC I x CD 8 = 8. MHC II x CD 4 = 8.

B cells [2]

B cells (B lymphocytes) originate in the bone marrow and migrate to secondary lymphatic organs (e.g., lymph nodes), where they differentiate to mature, naive B cells. Mature B cells circulate in between the blood and secondary lymphatic organs until they are activated by CD4+ T helper cells in response to an antigen. After activation, B cells undergo affinity maturation and differentiate into plasma cells that produce and secrete antibodies (see immunoglobulins).

Acquired immunity activation pathways

  1. Direct cell lysis or induction of apoptosis via perforin and proteases from cytotoxic T cells (CD8+)
    • Activated via antigen presentation by MHC class I receptors
  2. Cellular‑mediated type 1 response via Th1 cells
    • Interaction between Th1 cells with macrophages and antigen presenting cells (APCs) → stimulation of Th1 cells to produce cytokines (including IFN‑γ, IL‑2, and TNF‑α) → cytokines stimulate macrophages (positive feedback) and CD8+ cytotoxic T cells
    • Function: immune response to intracellular pathogens (viruses, intracellular bacteria)
      • Clinical relevance: tuberculosis in HIV infection
    • An exaggerated type 1 response can lead to autoimmune disease.
    • Cytokines inhibit the type 2 response (negative feedback) and activate the type 1 response (positive feedback).
  3. B cell activation and humoral type 2 response via Th2 cells
    • The humoral type 2 response involves the output of B lymphocytes, which recognize and internalize antigens via membrane‑bound immunoglobulins (IgD or IgM) → presentation via MHC class II receptors on the cell surface causes an interaction between Th2 cells; and B lymphocytes →; T cell‑dependent activation of B cells (plasma cells) ; → immunoglobulin production
    • Function: immune response to extracellular pathogens (bacteria, parasites)
    • The type 1 response is inhibited by suppressing macrophage activation (negative feedback)
  4. Class switching
    • B cell class switching occurs via two signaling mechanisms:
      1. Activation: Antigen bound to MHC II molecule binds to T cell receptor on the surface of helper T cells.
      2. Class switching: CD40 membrane receptor on the B cell binds to CD40 ligand (CD40L) on the surface of CD4+ T cell → cytokines released that determine immunoglobulin class switching
    • This interaction is essential for class switching of B cells and immunoglobulin secretion (e.g., IgM → IgA, IgG, IgE).
  5. T cell‑independent activation of B cells
    • Polysaccharide antigens can activate B cells and induce antibody production without stimulation by helper T cells.
    • Only leads to the development of IgM; no Ig class-switching occurs without T helper cells

Overview: immunoglobulins (antibodies)

Antibodies are divided into two functional parts, the Fab region and the Fc region. This classification can be identified by the application of two enzymes: papain and pepsin (see image below).

  • Fc region
    • Contains the constant region
    • Determines the isotype (e.g., IgA, IgG, IgM)
    • Binds complement (IgG, IgM), and macrophages (IgG)
  • Fab region
    • Contains the variable/hypervariable region
    • Formed by light chains and heavy chains
      • The heavy chain also forms part of the Fc region
    • Recognizes and binds to antigens
    • Determines the idiotype, which is specific for one antigen only

Immunoglobulin properties

  • Specificity
    • Requires antigens
    • Occurs by somatic hypermutation and affinity maturation, which takes place in the variable region
    • Class switching (e.g., IgA, IgM, IgG), which takes place in the constant region
  • Diversity
    • Does not require antigens
    • Random recombination
      • Light chain → VJ genes
      • Heavy chain V(D)J genes
    • Random addition of nucleotides to the DNA
      • By deoxynucleotidyl transferase (TdT)
    • Recombination of light chains with heavy chains

Autoimmunity

Content currently under development

  • Definition: Autoimmunity refers to an immune reaction against the body's own cells that occurs as a result of a loss of immunological tolerance.
  • Presumed pathogenesis: Autoreactive B lymphocytes are physiologically eliminated in the bone marrow, spleen, or lymph nodes. T lymphocytes that attack the body's own cells are either sorted out in the thymus or perish in peripheral lymphoid tissues (e.g, lymph nodes, adenoids, Peyer's patches) due to a lack of stimulation. If the selection mechanisms fail, this results in the immune cells attacking the body's own cells, which leads to autoimmune inflammation.
  • Epidemiology: Women have a disproportionately higher incidence of autoimmune diseases.
  • Causes
    • Mostly idiopathic
    • Sometimes elicited by a previous infection (e.g., Guillain‑Barré syndrome, rheumatic fever). The underlying pathomechanism is molecular mimicry.
    • Examples of a genetic predisposition
      • HLA-B8, e.g., myasthenia gravis, graves disease, Addison disease
      • HLA‑B27 e.g., ankylosing spondylitis, reactive arthritis, psoriatic arthritis, ulcerative colitis
      • HLA‑DR4 e.g., rheumatoid arthritis, type 1 diabetes, pemphigus vulgaris, addison disease
      • HLD-DR3 e.g., type 1 diabetes, SLE, Hashimoto's thyroiditis, Graves' disease
      • HLA-D2 e.g., SLE, goodpasture syndrome, multiple sclerosis
      • HLA‑DQ2/DQ8 e.g., gluten‑sensitive enteropathy
      • HLA-DR5 e.g.,Hashimoto thyoiditis
      • HLA-A3, e.g., Hemochromatosis
  • Consequences
    • The presence of autoreactive B lymphocytes causes the production of irregular antibodies, which can trigger various diseases. It can also be used as a diagnostic tool (see the table below). In T-cell‑mediated autoimmune reactions, there are usually no detectable specific antibodies (e.g., in multiple sclerosis).

Immune deficiency

Content currently under development

  • Epidemiology: Individual deficiencies are rare (an exception is selective IgA deficiency)
    • Estimate: ∼ 1–2/1,000 patients are immunodeficient.
  • There are over 50 different forms of immunodeficiency, with the most frequent being:
    • Innate
      • Fanconi anemia
      • Selective IgA deficiency
      • Wiskott‑Aldrich syndrome
      • Ataxia telangiectatica
      • DiGeorge syndrome
    • Acquired
      • HIV/AIDS
      • Iatrogenic immune suppression
  • See the learning card on congenital immunodeficiency disorders for more information.
  • Clinical findings: The main symptom of a primary immunodeficiency is a pathological susceptibility to infection. The type of susceptibility is characterized by the invading pathogen, localization, course, severity, and number of infections. Not all immune defects are clinically apparent.
Immune deficiency Disease example

Increased susceptibility to infection for:

Antibody deficiency
  • Bruton agammaglobulinemia
  • Variable immunodeficiency syndrome (common variable immunodeficiency, or CVID)
  • Bacterial infections: particularly Staphylococcus, Streptococcus, Haemophilus influenza
  • Enteroviruses

Complement defect
  • Hereditary angioedema
  • Bacterial infections: particularly Pneumococcus and Meningococcus

Granulocyte defect
  • Septic granulomatosis
  • Chemotherapy
  • Bacterial infections: particularly pyogenic gram-positive bacteria (Streptococcus, Staphylococcus, Enterococcus spp.)
  • Pseudomonas
  • Fungal infections: particularly Candida, Aspergillus

Defective cellular immunity
  • HIV
  • Viral infections
  • Fungal infections
  • Protozoal infections
  • Opportunistic infections

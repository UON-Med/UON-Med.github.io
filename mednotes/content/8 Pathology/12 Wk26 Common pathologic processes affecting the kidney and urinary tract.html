List the common causes of chronic kidney disease 

Risk factors for CKD include low birth weight, hypertension, childhood obesity, diabetes, autoimmune disease, age, family history, previous acute renal injury, and structural abnormalities of the urinary tract.  

 

Common causes of CKD: 

-Diabetic nephropathy 

-Glomerulonephritis 

-Hypertension, leading to vascular kidney disease, ischemic kidney disease 

-Autosomal dominant polycystic kidney disease 

-Other cystic and tubulointerstitial nephropathy 

 

Chronic disease leads to chronic kidney failure, which causes: 

-Hyperglycemia 

-Hypertension 

-Nephrotoxin 

-Hyperlipidemia  

 

Continued renal insult causes irreversible sclerosis and decreased renal mass and nephron function. This causes decreased glomerular filtration rate and causes more problems.  

-Electrolytes: High K+ and PO43-, low Ca2+ 

-Urine: nocturia, hematuria, proteinuria  

-Metabolic: acidosis, uremia 

-Systemic: hypertension, anemia, dyspnea, edema 

-GIT: anorexia, nausea, vomiting 

-Pruritus, restless legs 

 

Describe examples of renal pathology in systemic disease, specifically diabetes and hypertension 

 

Diabetes 

Hyperglycemia is the main cause of kidney disease in diabetic patients. High glucose levels in blood increases the amount of work done by the kidney in reabsorbing glucose through active transport mechanisms from the tubules. Over time, this leads to structural changes to the kidneys. However, other mechanisms are also in play. Soluble factors such as advanced glycation end products (AGE products) and growth factors can also damage the kidneys. Hemodynamic alterations in renal microcirculation can be regulated by further soluble factors such as through the vasoconstrictors angiotensin II and endothelin, and the vasodilator NO. Growth factors lead to both structural glomerulus changes and cytokine-mediated immune responses.  

 

Hypertension 

Hypertension  causes decreased glomerular blood flow (through atherosclerosis), which leads to both kidney damage and renin secretion. This renin then furthers hypertension, hence a worsening loop of events. 

 

In healthy individuals, when blood pressure is momentarily elevated the kidneys will undergo increased fluid excretion through regulation by natriuretic factor. However, atherosclerotic hypertension reduces nephron blood flow and long term ischemic events cause kidney failure. (Renal ischemia leads to tubular necrosis.) 

 

 

Describe the common pathological mechanisms and patterns of injury to the kidney in both acute and chronic kidney injury (excluding cancer) 

 

Acute renal failure 

Acute renal pathologies can be split into prerenal, intrarenal and postrenal.  

 

Prerenal: 

This usually refers to renal ischemia and hence tubular necrosis. Blood loss can be due to trauma, dehydration, cardiovascular event, septic shock (hypotension), renal artery stenosis, portal hypertension.  

 

Intrarenal:  

This refers to diseases which affect the nephron directly. Glomerular diseases involving acute glomerulonephritis, medullary disease eg acute pyelonephritis or interstitial nephritis, immune hypersensitivity reactions, vasculitis, and poisoning are examples.  

 

Postrenal: 

This refers to obstruction of the flow of urine at the level of the ureters, urinary bladder or urethra. If one ureter is blocked, the other kidney can compensate.  

 

Intrarenal medullary disorders: 

Pyelonephritis involves infection of the kidney, with infections originating either downstream or upstream. UTIs are a common cause. Presentation with tachycardia, sepsis and hypotension is expected.  

 

Tubulointerstitial nephritis involves damage to tubules by high levels of blood-borne substances eg therapeutic drugs. An acute inflammatory process causes white-cell damage and impedes tubule function. Drugs associated with kidney toxicity and hypersensitivity reactions include antibiotics, analgesics, immunosuppressive agents, psychotropic agents, and organic solvents.  

 

Intrarenal glomerular disorders: 

Glomerulonephritis is the inflammation of the glomerulus with reduced GFR which leads to decreased urine volume.

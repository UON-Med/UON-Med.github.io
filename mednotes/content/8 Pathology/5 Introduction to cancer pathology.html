Define and differentiate tumor and cancer 

A tumor or neoplasm is an abnormal mass of tissue which may be solid or fluid-filled.  

Cancer is a disease in which abnormal cells divide uncontrollably and destroy body tissue. Abnormal cancer cell growth has the potential to invade or spread to other parts of the body. 

Tumors can be non-cancerous (benign), pre-cancerous (pre-malignant), or malignant (cancerous).  

 

Define and differentiate the following terms: hyperplasia, metaplasia, dysplasia, neoplasia, malignant/benign and carcinoma in situ 

Hyperplasia- the enlargement of an organ or tissue caused by an increase in reproduction rate of its cells, often an initial stage in the development of cancer 

Metaplasia- a change of cells to a form that does not normally occur in the tissue in which it is found. Metaplasia is the process of the reversible substitution of a distinct kind of cell with another mature cell of the similar distinct kind. If the stimulus responsible for this transformation is removed, metaplasia can be reversed as tissues instantly get back to the normal course of growth and prototype of differentiation.  

Dysplasia- an irregularity that hinders cell maturation within a particular tissue. There is an increased growth of immature cells with a simultaneous reduction in growth of mature cells. Cellular uniformity and tissue architecture are lost. Dysplasia is carcinogenic and still sometimes reversible. 

Neoplasia- further progress of dysplasia to an irreversible, carcinogenic state. In cancer, a naturel development of metaplasia to dysplasia to neoplasia occurs. 

 

Note: anaplasia describes poor differentiation of cells. When assessing the differentiation of a tumor, the evidence of normal function eg production of keratin, bile, mucin, hormones etc, suggests a low grade tumor (anaplastic tumor). If differentiation is seen, the tumor is of a higher grade. Ultimately however, stage (how much the tumor has spread) is more important than grade (how much the tumor has differentiated).  

 

Benign tumor- incapable of invading neighboring tissue (metastasize), and are thus by definition non-cancerous 

Malignant tumor- cancerous tumor, capable of metastasizing.  

Carcinoma in situ- group of abnormal cells that have thus far remained in the place where they first formed, and have not spread. These abnormal cells may become cancer and spread to nearby normal tissue.  

 

 

Explain the principles underlying the nomenclature of tumors and define the following terms: hamartoma, carcinoma, sarcoma, teratoma, lymphoma, leukemia, melanoma, glioma 

-Carcinoma: malignant tumor derived from epithelium 

-Leukemia and lymphoma: tumors of white blood cells. Leukemia is a malignant tumor of primitive bone-marrow derived cells, which circulate the blood stream. Leukemia involves a disruption in the normal growth and development of nonlymphocytic (myeloid) blood cells. Lymphoma is a malignant tumor of lymphocytes, proliferating usually in lymph nodes. Lymphoma involves a disruption in the normal growth and development of lymphoid cells, and is also known as lymph node dysplasia. Leukemia is the main cause of cancer death in people aged 1-34 years. 

-Teratoma: a tumor derived from germ cells, which has the potential to develop in tumors of all three germ cell layers- ectoderm, mesoderm, endoderm. These are made up of various bodily tissues. They are usually found in the gonads. In men they are malignant; in women they are usually benign.  

-Hamartoma: localised overgrowth of cells and tissues native to the organ. Cells are mature but architecturally abnormal. This is common in children, and should stop growing as they do.  

-Sarcoma: a malignant tumor derived from connective tissue or mesenchymal stem cells 

-Glioma: neoplasia of glial tissue (brain) 

-Melanoma: tumor of melanocytes

Benign tumour (Adenoma, Papilloma of surface epithelium) 

-No direct threat to life, and do not invade surrounding tissue 

-Well differentiated, with slow growth and normal mitosis, are encapsulated 

Benign tumours are often non-fatal unless found it is found in a dangerous place (meninges, pituitary area), secretes something dangerous eg excess insulin produced due to insulinoma, gets infected eg in the bladder, bleeds, ruptures, or becomes torted (eg benign ovarian cyst infarct). 

 

Malignant tumour (Carcinoma) 

-Invade surrounding tissue, no capsule, poorly to well differentiated, rapid growth and abnormal mitosis 

-Can undergo metastasis 

 

The more malignant a tumour is, the more undifferentiated (anaplastic) it is. They may also have defects in differentiation allowing a cell that was one type eg nerve cell to acquire the characteristics of another cell type eg muscle cell. These are pleomorphic cells, and such cells of varying sizes and types can compromise a tumour cell.

Pattern of hyperplasia to dysplasia to neoplasia 

Hyperplasia involves an increase in the number of cells in an organ. Metaplasia then occurs where one differentiated/adult cell type is replaced with another. Dysplasia involves the large presence of abnormal cell types and abnormal growth patterns in a tissue. The proliferation of dysplasia produces a neoplasm, which leads to a carcinoma in situ. 

 

Briefly describe the concept of differentiation and its relation to cancer outcome 

More malignant cancers are less differentiated.  

 

If there is evidence of normal functioning still present in a tumour eg production of keratin, bile, hormones etc, it is unlikely to be high-grade. If there is no evidence of this, it is anaplastic.  

 

List the main pathways by which tumors metastasise and explain the factors which determine the sites of metastases 

Malignant cells invade into lymphatics, blood vessels and cavities. Lymphatics allow the spread to lymph nodes and is the frequent route of carcinoma spread. Blood vessels allow spread through capillaries to the common sites: lung, liver, bone, brain. The cells can also spread into coelomic spaces. 

 

Describe the mechanisms involved in invasion and metastases 

In order for metastasis, cells require certain characteristics such as the ability to grow unanchored, the ability to create finger-like projections to reach between other cells or into the lymphatic/blood system, and the capacity to produce proteins and enzymes to facilitate the breakdown of barriers to movement (eg stroma).  

 

The first step of metastasis involves detachment from neighbouring cells. This occurs due to a change in the expression of surface proteins (fibronectin and cadherin mainly) which reduce the connection between adjacent cells and render the cell unable to associate with neighbouring cells. The tumour then expresses receptors eg laminin receptors to bind to basement membrane. Secretion of certain enzymes degrades the protein and collagen of the extracellular matrix and allows penetration of pseudopodia through interstitial connective tissue layers. The cell then pulls itself through the gap and into the vessels using its pseudopodia, to be carried to other parts of the body.

Describe the distinguish grading and staging of cancer 

Classification of tumours is done by: 

1, tissue and cell source 

2, primary or secondary tumour (T) 

3, regional lymph node involvement (N) 

4, metastasis (M) 

 

The grade of a tumour describes its degree of differentiation, and the stage of a tumour describes how far it has spread. Tumours of a higher grade are more anaplastic. 

 

Stage 1: Confined to organ of origin 

Stage 2: Locally invasive 

Stage 3: Spread to lymph nodes 

Stage 4: Spread to distant sites

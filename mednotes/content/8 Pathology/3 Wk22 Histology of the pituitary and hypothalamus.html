Pituitary 

The pituitary gland is divided into the adenohypophysis and neurohypophysis. 

The adenohypophysis is further divided into the pars distalis, pars intermedia, and pars tuberalis. The neurohypophysis is subdivided into the infundibulum and neural lobe. 

 

Adenohypophysis 

This contains cells classified as chromophils (acidophils [alpha cells] and basophils [beta cells]), and chromophobes. Cords between these cells anastamose, and are separated from sinusoidal capillaries by a small amount of irregular tissue. Chromophil granules stain well with eosin or hematoxylin, whereas chromophobes do not.  

 

Neurohypophysis 

This contains unmyelinated axons from the hypothalamic-hypophyseal tract, blood vasculature, and pituicytes. Within the neural lobe, Herring bodies (neurosecretory bodies) may also be seen. These are large accumulations of neurosecretory material and degradative products.  

 

Control by hypothalamus 

The hypothalamus is a major link between the nervous and endocrine systems, integrating signals for appropriate response. The hypothalamus secretes several hormones and controls glandular secretions, helping regulate heart rate, metabolic rate, water/solute balance.  

-Hypothalamus secretes regulatory hormones through portal vein to the anterior pituitary lobe, controlling its activity. Hormones then secreted by the anterior pituitary control other endocrine organs.  

-Hypothalamus directly produces ADH and Oxytocin through posterior pituitary lobe.  

-Control of sympathetic output to adrenal medullae, resultantly controlling the secretion of epinephrine and norepinephrine. 

 

Neurohypophysis: hormones synthesised in neurosecretory cell bodies (ADH and Oxytocin in different nuclei) within the hypothalamus travel down the axons to be stored in secretory vesicles in the neuronal terminals of the posterior pituitary. These are released into the bloodstream upon excitation of hypothalamic neurons 

 

Adenohypophysis: activated hypothalamic neurons release hormones into the hypophyseal portal system. These either inhibit or promote the release of hormones synthesised by secretory cells in the anterior pituitary. Pituitary hormones then promote the release of specific hormones in "subordinate" endocrine glands/end organs.

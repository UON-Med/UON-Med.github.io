Describe the mechanical consequences of obesity on the body (reflux, osteoarthritis, sleep apnea, increased cardiac work, etc) 

Cardiac 

Buildup of LDLs and VLDLs in the bloodstream leads to atherosclerosis and high blood pressure. This leads to increased cardiac work, ventricular hypertrophy, and eventually heart disease.  

 

Reflux 

Increased consumption of food expands the stomach, and an excessive distention of the stomach will pull part of the lower esophageal sphincter toward the stomach. Repeated occurrences like this will cause a shortening in the breadth of the muscle band, hence weakening the sphincter. Increased relaxation of the sphincter allows gastric juices to reflux into the esophagus.  

 

Osteoarthritis 

Obesity-linked osteoarthritis is multifactorial.  

-Structural joint damage from increased weight 

-Decreased muscle strength due to fatty acid buildup and decreased exercise forces joint to take more weight and damage 

-Metabolic factors such as secretion of pro-inflammatory signals which can lead to destruction and remodeling of the joint. Cartilage is often eroded.  

 

Obstructive sleep apnea 

Excess weight and obesity can lead to changes in the soft tissue of the mouth and throat, and during sleep, relaxation of the throat and tongue muscles causes a blockage of the upper airway. Patients experience repeated stops in breathing for 10 seconds or more, causing sleepers to awake.  

 

Describe the consequences of obesity in adipose, muscle, and liver on the cellular level 

Adipose 

In the presence of excess energy supply, adipose tissue expands due to cellular hypertrophy and hyperplasia. The vascular infrastructure may not keep pace with rapid tissue expansion and this leads to local hypoxia and fibrosis. This leads to necrotic tissue attracting macrophages, with an increase in inflammation. (Adiposopathy) This leads to chronic changes to FFA metabolism and an increase in FFA release.  

 

Non-adipose 

Excessive release of FFAs into blood allows FFAs to accumulate in non-adipose tissue such as muscle, the liver, the pancreas, or the heart. Lipids are stored and accumulated as droplets in close proximity to the mitochondria, which aren't conventionally geared toward this function. By-products of cytosolic fatty acid accumulation lead to insulin resistance, organ dysfunction and organ failure.  

 

Describe the consequences of obesity on lipid profiles and the risk of atherosclerosis 

Obese individuals are more likely to have higher total cholesterol, LDL, triglyceride, and lower HDL. They are likely to have insulin resistance and suffer heart disease.  

 

Briefly describe the two types of diabetes mellitus 

Type 1 diabetes: insulin-dependent, no insulin is produced by the patient (10%) 

Patients are often young with rapid onset of the condition. Patients often lose weight and are ketosis-prone. 

 

Type 2 diabetes: insulin resistant, with decreased insulin sensitivity. (90%) 

Patients are often middle-aged with slow onset of the condition. Patients are not prone to ketosis and are often overweight with strong family history.  

 

Individuals with insulin resistance present with hyperglycemia and hypertriglyceridemia. Early stages of insulin resistance may involve the expansion of beta cell mass and enhancement of beta cell function, in response to the increased demand for insulin. This leads to hyperinsulinemia.  

 

In susceptible pancreatic beta cells from genetic risk, the systemic increase in glucose and FFAs can result in glucolipotoxicity which damages beta cells and leads to decreased mass, function and insulin secretion. In this manner, a patient progresses from normal to impaired glucose tolerance to type 2 diabetes. 

 

Note: HbA1c > 6.5% indicates diagnosis of diabetes (glycated hemoglobin) 

 

Briefly describe the link between obesity and type 2 diabetes 

Obese individuals often consume more energy than required. Excessive triglycerides and glucose in the bloodstream lead to increased pancreatic beta cell function. However, this can eventually lead to insulin resistance and thus the progress toward type 2 diabetes begins.  

 

Briefly describe the link between obesity and inflammation  

Adipocytes are capable of releasing hormones, and obesity is associated with the following pro-inflammatory agents: 

-Tumor necrosis factor 

-IL-6 

-Monocyte chemoattractant protein 1 

-Cytokine-inducible nitric oxide synthase 

-Matrix metalloproteinases 

-Lipocalin 

-Plasminogen activator inhibitor 1 

-Transcription factor NF-kB 

 

The activation of macrophages also occurs.

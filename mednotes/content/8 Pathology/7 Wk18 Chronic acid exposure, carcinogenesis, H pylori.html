Describe common causes of inflammation at the esophagus, stomach, and duodenum 

Esophagitis 

Esophagitis is inflammation of the esophagus that may lead to permanent damage of the structure. Most common causes of this are: 

-Gastro-esophageal reflux disease (GERD) 

-Hiatus hernia 

-NSAIDs, antibiotics, steroids 

-Alcohol 

-Infection 

-Radiotherapy 

-Allergy 

 

The transient involuntary reflux of gastric juices into the esophagus is considered normal in healthy humans. However, if symptoms such as heartburn and regurgitation (reflux symptoms) appear and impair the quality of life and place patients at risk of complication, it is regarded as a dysfunction ie GERD.  

 

 

 

Complications of GERD include esophagitis with ulceration, scarring, and cancer.  

 

Stomach 

Inflammation of the stomach can be caused by: 

-Long term use of aspirin, ibuprofen, and other medications 

-Alcohol 

-H pylori 

-Diabetes, kidney failure 

-Bile reflux into stomach 

-Immunosuppression 

 

H pylori is a gram negative bacteria, with several acid-resistance mechanisms such as the release of urease to catalyse urea hydrolysis into buffering ammonia. It is microaerophilic (requires low oxygen and is slow-growing). It is the main risk factor for peptic ulcer, gastric adenocarcinoma, and gastric mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue lymphoma (MALT) 

 

Duodenum 

Inflammation of the duodenum can be caused by: 

-H pylori, Giardia lambia, etc 

-NSAIDs, corticosteroids 

-Alcohol, cocaine, tobacco 

-Crohn's disease  

-Autoimmune 

-Radiotherapy  

 

Crohn's disease (inflammatory bowel disease) is a long term inflammatory condition that is likely caused by inappropriate immune response to gastrointestinal content. It may occur anymore along the digestive tract but is most common in the ileum, with inflammation occurring in all 4 layers of the intestinal walls (mucosa, submucosa, muscularis externa, adventitia/serosa). 

 

Define ulcer and briefly describe the general pathologic mechanisms that cause ulceration 

 

Peptic ulcer disease describes ulcers in the stomach and duodenum. A peptic ulcer is characterised by a mucosal erosive injury of about 5mm or more in depth, exposing underlying smooth muscle, blood vessels and sensory nerves to the gastrointestinal contents. Mucosal damage occurs either due to an aggressive action of gastric juices, or a weakened mucosal protection. Factors linked strongly to peptic ulcer disease are chronic NSAID use and H pylori infection.  

 

Describe the pathologic consequences of chronic exposure of tissues to acid and compare the effects on stomach, esophagus and oral cavity (teeth) 

 

Oral cavity 

Dental enamel consists primarily of a calcium phosphate mineral that is insoluble in alkaline, but soluble in acid. Hence in GERD the reflux of HCl from the stomach can cause tooth erosion. Note the significance of vomiting in bringing up acid, especially if recurrent such as in bulimia nervosa where patients purge. 

 

Esophagus 

Chronic acid exposure causes GERD, inflammation, Barret's esophagitis (pre-malignant), esophageal cancer. 

 

Stomach 

The stomach possesses a mucosal defense system including the mucus-bicarbonate phospholipid layer, surface epithelial cells producing mucus and providing ion transport, and an elaborate microvascular system providing HCO3- to neutralise acid.  

 

Chronic acid secretion however can cause peptic ulcers and gastric malignancies.  

 

Briefly describe the epidemiology and natural history of H pylori infection and discuss the response of the stomach to chronic infection 

H pylori is a gram-negative bacteria. It has colonised 50% of the world's population over the past 100000 years. Following chronic infection of the stomach, gastric mucosal atrophy occurs, with the destruction of glands. There is therefore a lower acidity of gastric juices, increasing certain chemical factors which ultimately leads to metaplasia and then neoplastic formation.  

 

List the common cancers occurring in the stomach and esophagus and briefly describe their epidemiology 

Esophagus 

These are extremely lethal. Almost all esophageal cancers are squamous cell carcinomas (most common), or adenocarcinomas. Squamous cell carcinoma is caused by excess alcohol consumption, cigarette smoke, toxins, dietary insufficiencies, mucosal damage from physical agents eg radiation, heat (hot food). Adenocarcinoma is caused by chronic GORD, cigarettes, obesity, Barrett's esophagus, and is more common in males.  

 

Clinical presentation: dysphagia, weight loss 

 

Stomach 

Around 85% are adenocarcinomas, with 15% being lymphomas, GI stromal tumors, leiomyosarcoma. Gastric adenocarcinomas are separated into diffuse type (no cell cohesion, cells infiltrate and thicken stomach wall without discreet mass formation, most common in younger patients, increases gastric wall distensibility, poor prognosis), and intestinal type (cohesive neoplastic cells forming gland-like tubular structure, frequently ulcerative, often preceded by prolonged precancerous process eg H pylori). Stomach cancers are caused by long term exposure to nitrates, H Pylori, gastric ulcers, adenomatous polyps, hypertrophy of gastric rugal folds, and is more common in blood group O patients.  

 

Clinical presentation: dysphagia, anorexia, vomiting, slight nausea, upper abdominal pain, asymptomatic in initial stages 

 

 

List features on endoscopy that would distinguish benign vs malignant stomach cancer 

Benign cancers appear to have intact mucosa to the edge, a smooth mound with tapering edges, projects beyond expected lumen, folds extent into the crater, depth > width, sharp contour, Hampton line on X-ray.  

 

Malignant cancers appear within the lumen of the stomach. Width > depth, eccentric, nodular, rolled, irregular, shouldered edges, carmen meniscus sign

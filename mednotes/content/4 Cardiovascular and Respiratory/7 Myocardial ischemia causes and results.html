Define and differentiate between primary and secondary hypertension 

Primary hypertension (90-95% of adult cases) is the result of environmental or genetic factors, whereas secondary hypertension has multiple etiologies (causes), including renal, vascular, endocrine- related causes.  

 

Describe the impact of hypertension on the vasculature 

-High blood pressure can cause the arteries and arterioles to lose their elasticity and become more firm, with the onset of arteriosclerosis and arteriolosclerosis.  

-An aneurysm can also result due to a weak spot in the inner arterial wall 

-A thrombus can reduce O2 supply to cells, which will lead to mitochondrial death, acidosis, cellular death etc. 

 

Describe the pathogenesis of atherosclerosis and atheroma 

Atheroma is caused by endothelial damage and dysfunction, resulting from the high blood pressure. High blood pressure stimulates the accumulation and oxidation of LDL-C in the vessel wall. Monocytes migrate from outside the cell into the sub-endothelial intima an transform into macrophages, which accumulate lipids and form the lipid core of the atherosclerotic plaque. Thrombotic and inflammatory responses will produce inflammatory mediators and cytokines. Cytokines induce the migration of smooth muscle cells from the media to the intima, and then stimulate their proliferation. These factors also induce the deposition of extracellular molecules such as elastin and collagen, which leads to plaque expansion and formation of the fibrous cap.  

 

Plaque rupture is caused by the breakage of this fibrous cap, leading to further thrombus (clot) formation and the release of inflammatory mediators. Continued luminal narrowing occurs. A more drastic outcome may include an arterial occlusion leading to myocardial infarction, ischemic stroke or critical ischemia in peripheral tissues.  

 

Define thrombus and briefly describe its causes and potential consequences 

A thrombus is a blood clot, intended to prevent excessive blood flow and bleeding. A thrombus can form if a vessel's inner lining is damaged, leading to inflammatory responses as described in atherosclerosis. A thrombus can also form if blood flow is sluggish or slow, or if the blood is thicker than normal. (Eg inherited conditions like factor V Leiden) Hormone therapy or birth control pills also increase the risk of clotting.  

 

A thrombus can lead to further blockage of blood flow, leading to ischemia. A thrombosis eg Deep Vein Thrombosis can also result, which presents with pain, swelling in extremities, tenderness, and warm skin. An embolism can also result. 

 

Define emboli and briefly describe its causes and potential consequences 

An embolus is a particle that moves through the blood vessels and proceeds to lodge in a vessel and become an embolism (blocking the vessel). Embolisms result from blood clots, and examples include: 

-Pulmonary embolism: an embolism travels through the heart and into the lungs where it lodges in an artery (typically where the artery forks), and blocks blood flow. This can be fatal if large amounts of blood tissue are deprived of blood and die.  

-Heart attack: If a clot is lodged in one of the heart's coronary arteries, severe angina will occur and possibly lead to death 

-Stroke: a blood clot  

 

Define aneurysm 

An aneurysm is a weakness in a blood vessel in the brain that balloons and fills with blood 

 

Describe the response of a cell to hypoxic/ischemic stress (revise cell response to injury) 

-Oxidative phosphorylation is not possible 

-Anaerobic respiration begins, producing lactic acid which lowers pH inside the cell 

-Denaturing of proteins occurs due to low pH 

-Na+/K+ ATPase stops working, cell starts to swell due to influx of sodium and osmosis of water 

-Swelling of rough endoplasmic reticulum causes ribosomes to detach, which ceases protein synthesis 

-Calcium pump stops working, leakage of calcium into cell can activate proteases (which digest proteins), endonucleases (which digest DNA), and phospholipase (which digest phospholipids in the cell membrane) 

-Calcium causes small molecules such as cytochrome C to leak out of the mitochondria; cytochrome C causes cell apoptosis 

 

-The resupply of oxygen will usually reverse such damage if it occurs early enough 

Note that cell injury has a variety of causes, including O2 depravation, toxins, infection, physical trauma 

 

Describe the consequences of ischemia on myocardial cell function 

-Ischemia compromises both the supply of oxygen and metabolic substrates eg glucose to end organ tissues 

-Lactic acid buildup and eventual acidosis 

-Contraction and relaxation of the heart muscle requires ATP, which becomes depleted in ischemia 

-Systolic dysfunction (loss of inotropy) and diastolic dysfunction (impaired relaxation and reduced compliance) 

 

Describe the potential benefit of rapid restoration of blood flow 

-Rapid restoration of blood flow will bring oxygen to the cells, possibly reversing much of the damage that has been done 

-This will also help remove waste products such as acid to bring pH levels back to normal 

-ADP can be converted into ATP if oxygen is present; if oxygen is not available again quickly the ADP can diffuse away. If this happens, ATP cannot be regenerated. 

 

Briefly describe how ischemia causes pain 

During myocardial ischemia, adenosine is released in large amounts into the interstitial space. Arachidonic acid can also be released into the blood from adipose tissue, which is converted by COX-2 to give inflammation and pain-mediating prostaglandins. Lactic acid buildup from anaerobic respiration also activates pain receptors. 

 

Define and differentiate angina, ischemia, infarction and necrosis 

Angina: pain caused by lack of blood supply to the heart 

Ischemia: lack of blood supply to a certain part of the body 

Infarction: blockage of a blood vessel with partially necrosed muscle wall 

Necrosis: unprogrammed cell death due to autolysis, releasing harmful cell components that can damage other cells and cause inflammation. This involves the death of most cells in an organ or tissue, and is due to injury, disease, or failure of blood supply.  

 

Briefly describe the morphologic changes seen in ischemia 

Necrotic cells and their organelles are characteristically swollen. There is early membrane damage with eventual loss of plasma membrane integrity and leakage of cytosol into extracellular space. Blebs form, but organelles are not located in the blebs. (As opposed to in apoptosis, where some organelles are located in the blebs) 

 

Differentiate necrosis and apoptosis 

Apoptotic cells are shrunken and develop blebs containing dense cytoplasm. Membrane is not lost until much later stages. The cytoplasm becomes divided to form small apoptotic bodies containing still-functional organelles and nuclear debris. These fragments are engulfed by phagocytes. This is a normal, physiological process involving programmed cell death, is an energy-dependent process and is not associated with inflammation. Few cells in a tissue undergo apoptosis at any given time. 

 

Necrosis involves the breakdown of the organelles and leakage of cellular components into the interstitial space. This usually involves injury, disease or the failure of blood supply. Most of the cells in a tissue will undergo necrosis together, as the leakage of chemicals is often harmful to surrounding cells. Necrosis is an immune-dependent process, and is associated with inflammation.

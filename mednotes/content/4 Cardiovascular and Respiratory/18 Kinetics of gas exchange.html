Briefly describe the factors that affect the diffusion of gases between alveolar gas and pulmonary capillary blood 

-Membrane thickness, rate of diffusion is inversely proportional to membrane thickness  

-Surface area of membrane, rate of diffusion is directly proportional to surface area  

-Diffusion coefficient of gas (diffusiveness specific to each gas which is inversely related to molecular weight, directly proportional to solubility of the gas in the fluid and tissue of the respiratory membrane) 

-Partial pressure (concentration) gradient 

 

 

 

 

Diffusing capacity 

The diffusing capacity of the respiratory membrane (DL) is defined as the volume of gas that diffuses through the respiratory membrane each minute per mmHg of partial pressure difference. The diffusing capacity is directly proportional to surface area and the diffusion coefficient for the gas, and inversely proportional to diffusion distance (membrane thickness).  

 

Briefly describe the kinetics of oxygen transfer from the alveolus to capillary and the concept of capillary reserve time (ie the portion of the erythrocyte transit time in which no further diffusion of oxygen occurs) 

PO2 in the alveolar air is always at around 104mmHg, as this is air supplied by the bronchi, independent of blood supply. The PO2 of blood arriving at the pulmonary capillary (from pulmonary artery) is normally around 40mmHg. This large concentration gradient allows O2 to diffuse rapidly across the respiratory membrane. O2 diffusion into the pulmonary capillaries is so efficient that at resting levels of cardiac output, diffusion has gone almost to completion by the time the blood has travelled only 1/3 the length of the pulmonary capillaries (ie diffusion gone to completion by 0.25 seconds of 0.75 second total capillary transit time). The remaining 0.5 seconds are referred to as the capillary reserve time. The same processes apply for CO2 diffusion, with net diffusion occurring in the opposite direction.  

 

Thus, blood can flow three times as quickly through the pulmonary capillary during exercise and still be properly oxygenated. 

 

The partial pressure gradient across the respiratory membrane for CO2 is only around 5mmHg however. However, CO2 is 20 times more soluble in plasma than O2, and thus at any concentration gradient CO2 diffuses 20 times as readily as O2.  

 

Briefly describe the alveolar gas equation and the clinical significance of the A-a gradient 

The alveolar gas equation is used to measure the arterial PO2 (PAO2). Alveolar PO2 and PCO2 cannot be practically measured as ventilation is not uniform in different parts of the lungs in many respiratory disorders. The equation is used to examine the alveolar-arterial (A-a) gradient, which is impaired in a range of respiratory disorders and is thus a useful clinical quantity.  

 

 

 

Estimates of PAO2 and PACO2 can be produced, by letting PACO2 = PCO2 and then using the equation.  

 

Define and contrast the following terms: anatomic dead space, physiologic dead space, wasted (dead space) ventilation, total minute ventilation, and alveolar minute ventilation 

A person needs to inhale around 150ml before any fresh air gets into the alveoli for gas exchange, and this must be taken into account when determining how much fresh air gets into the alveoli per minute. This 150ml is called the anatomic dead space. 

Damaged lungs will often have ventilated, but under-perfused alveoli incapable of gas exchange. These under-perfused lung units cause the alveolar dead space. Note: normally, alveolar dead space is zero. 

The physiologic dead space is the addition of the anatomic and alveolar dead spaces. 

Wasted (dead space) ventilation is Physiologic dead space x Respiratory rate. 

Total minute ventilation is Tidal volume x Respiratory rate 

Alveolar minute ventilation is (Tidal volume - Anatomic dead space) x Respiratory rate 

 

Briefly describe how the ventilation/perfusion ratio of an alveolar-capillary lung unit determines the PO2 and PCO2 of the blood emerging from that lung unit 

VA/Q denotes the ventilation/perfusion ratio, and is used to examine a specific lung unit. Optimal respiratory gas exchange occurs when VA/Q = 1. If the alveoli examined have no ventilation, then the regional VA/Q = 0. If the alveoli examined have no perfusion, regional VA/Q = infinity.  

 

In asthma, there will be a combination of low and normal VA/Q values, primarily due to impaired regional ventilation due to airway mucus and inflammation.  

In COPD, there will be low VA/Q values in some regions due to airway mucus, inflammation and distorted bronchioles, but also high VA/Q values in some regions of adequate ventilation but damaged alveolar walls (and hence inhibited perfusion).  

 

High VA/Q leads to high PO2 and low PCO2, whilst low VA/Q leads to low PO2 and high PCO2.  

 

Briefly describe the vertical distribution of ventilation and perfusion in the healthy lung and the effect of ventilation/perfusion (V/Q) ratio in each region 

As opposed to the supine position, in the upright position, both regional ventilation and perfusion increase as you go down the lungs. However, the increase in perfusion is greater. Hence, VA/Q is abnormally high at the top of the lung and abnormally low at the bottom of the lung. The effects on ventilation and perfusion are both due to gravity: 

-The lowest regions of the lungs (base of lungs in upright position) are partly deflated and compressed by the weight of the lungs. When partly deflated, they are more compliant. Hence, a given pleural pressure change during inspiration will inflate the lower, more compliant lung regions to a greater degree than the less compliant upper lung regions. 

-Gravity progressively increases the pulmonary blood pressure the lower the vessel is below the heart. Thus, perfusion is greater toward the bottom of the lungs.  

 

These regional differences are much less apparent when lying down. 

 

Briefly describe the airway and vascular control mechanisms that help maintain a normal ventilation/perfusion ratio 

The primary mechanism is the vasoconstriction of small pulmonary blood vessels when the local PO2 decreases (hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction, opposite to systemic hypoxic vasodilation). Pulmonary vasoconstriction is useful in diverting blood flow toward better ventilated alveoli, thus maximising the delivery of oxygen to the systemic supply. Note, however, that PO2 in the pulmonary arterial blood is inherently low, and thus pulmonary vasoconstriction is not induced by low PO2 in the blood. This has thus led to speculation that a receptor on the adventitial (outer) surface of pulmonary arteries senses alveolar PO2 and initiates vasoconstriction.  

 

When there is reduced alveolar ventilation and excessive perfusion (low VA/Q), PO2 reduces and PCO2 increases. This leads to pulmonary vasoconstriction and reduced perfusion to keep VA/Q = 1. 

When there is reduced perfusion and excessive alveolar ventilation (high VA/Q), PO2 increases and PCO2 decreases. This leads to pulmonary vasodilation and increased perfusion to keep VA/Q = 1.   

 

Define right-to-left shunts, anatomic and physiologic shunts, and physiologic dead space (wasted ventilation) and briefly describe the consequences of each for pulmonary gas exchange 

Right-to-left shunting refers to systemic venous blood bypassing ventilated parts of the lungs (or bypassing the lungs altogether), which then mixes with oxygenated blood to be delivered to systemic arteries. Physiologic shunt refers to the total amount of shunted blood flow per minute. Physiologic shunt includes: 

-Normal anatomical shunt, eg deoxygenated blood from bronchial circulation passes into pulmonary veins to mix with oxygenated blood, and also right-to-left shunt of deoxygenated blood in thebesian veins directly into the left atrium 

-Abnormal anatomical shunt, due to pathological causes eg congenital heart defects 

-A small amount of right-to-left shunt also normally occurs as a result of a reduced VA/Q ratio in gravity-dependent lung units in the lower parts of the lungs 

-Right-to-left shunt increases in scenarios of low VA/Q, eg collapsed lung due to pneumothorax, COPD 

 

Note that the solubility of CO2 is greater than that of O2.

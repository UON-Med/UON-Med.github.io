Briefly describe the response of the myocardium to pressure overload (prolonged hypertension and/or outflow obstruction)  

Hypertrophic growth of cardiomyocytes is the main mechanism by which the heart reduces left-ventricular wall stress imposed by pressure overload. Besides hypertrophy, other alterations of the cardiomyocyte include an increased rate of apoptotic cell death, fibrosis, and changes to the coronary circulation. These increase the risk of cardiovascular outcomes. Note that left-sided heart failure leads to pulmonary hypertension, which leads to right-sided heart failure.  

 

Fibrosis 

The proliferation of fibroblasts and resultant accumulation of collagen type I and type III fibers within the interstitium and around blood vessels leads to systolic and diastolic left-ventricular dysfunction, diminished coronary flow reserve, and ventricular arrhythmias.  

 

Changes to coronary circulation 

Hyperplasia and hypertrophy of intramyocardial arteries and a decrease in arteriolar and capillary density occurs. This predisposes the heart to inadequate myocardial perfusion.  

 

Outflow obstruction 

Hypertrophy of the interventricular septum can lead to outflow obstruction. Furthermore, aortic stenosis directly causes outflow obstruction and leads to further hypertrophy.  

 

Other complications of hypertrophy 

-Arrhythmias 

-Endocarditis 

-Atrial fibrillation and mural thrombus formation 

-Sudden death 

 

Define and contrast hypertrophy and hyperplasia 

Hypertrophy: increased length of myocytes 

Note: concentric hypertrophy is the addition of sarcomeres to myocytes in parallel leading to a thicker cell, and eccentric hypertrophy is the addition of sarcomeres in series leading to a longer cell 

Hyperplasia: increased number of myocytes  

 

Briefly describe the negative consequences of ventricular hypertrophy on ventricular function 

-Systolic and diastolic dysfunction 

-Diminished coronary flow reserve 

-Ventricular arrhythmias  

-Outflow obstruction by anterior movement of mitral valve toward the interventricular septum 

-Outflow obstruction by enlarged septum 

 

Briefly describe how hypertension and cardiac hypertrophy increase susceptibility to ischemia 

-Hypertension exacerbates the progression of arteriosclerosis by increasing the risk of endothelial damage and thus the formation of a thrombus from plaque, leading to ischemia 

-Excessive hypertrophy leads to decreased cardiac output and thus ischemia 

 

Briefly describe the pathological mechanisms affecting cardiac valves (overview level)  

-High blood pressure leading to ventricular hypertrophy stretches and distorts the valves (mitral and tricuspid) 

-Atherosclerosis in the aorta leads to distortion 

-Fibrotic tissue 

-Rheumatic fever: heart valves may be damaged or scarred when the body attempts to clear the infection 

-Infective endocarditis 

-Non-infective endocarditis caused by trauma can obstruct the valves 

 

Briefly describe the consequences of heart valve dysfunction (stenosis and regurgitation), on changes to the heart chambers (dilation or hypertrophy), on cardiac output, and impact on venous pressure  

-Aortic and pulmonary stenosis lead to ventricular hypertrophy, decrease cardiac output, eventual increased venous pressure 

-Aortic and pulmonary regurgitation lead to ventricular dilation (eccentric hypertrophy), decrease cardiac output, and increase pulmonary and systemic pressure respectively. 

 

-Mitral and tricuspid stenosis lead to atrial hypertrophy, reduces cardiac output, and increases venous pressure (first increasing atrial pressure) 

-Mitral and tricuspid regurgitation lead to dilated atria, reduce cardiac output, increase pulmonary and systemic venous pressure respectively 

 

 

Briefly describe the pathological changes observed in edema 

-Ventricular failure leads to an increase in venous pressure, resulting in higher hydrostatic pressure in the peripheral capillaries. This results in edema.  

-Proinflammatory mediators can result in leaky vessels, causing edema 

-Blocked lymphatic ducts 

-Reduced plasma oncotic pressure due to liver dysfunction (lack of protein production) or renal failure (excess protein excretion) 

 

Compare the consequences of edema at different sites (lung vs periphery) 

Peripheral edema: nerve damage, tissue damage 

Pulmonary edema: hypoxia, cyanosis, heart disease, alveolar damage (macrophages etc may flood alveoli to cause damage)

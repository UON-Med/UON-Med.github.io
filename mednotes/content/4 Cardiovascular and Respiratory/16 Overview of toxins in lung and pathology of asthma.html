List the substances with potential respiratory toxicity that are most commonly encountered in Australia 

-Carbon 

-Sulfate compounds 

-Dust 

-Tobacco smoke 

 

Briefly describe the anatomic/functional reasons that lungs are particularly susceptible to toxic exposures 

-Lungs directly bring toxins into body via inhalation 

-Depending on solubility of the toxic gas inhaled, it may be able to move into the blood and be spread around the body 

-Large surface area of lungs allow toxins to be absorbed 

 

Briefly describe the range of toxic effects produced in the lungs (asthma, COPD, fibrosis, cancer) 

-Asthma: involves hypersensitivity and bronchospasm, with inflammation of the airway 

-Chronic bronchitis: mucus hypersecretion from inflamed bronchial tubes, causing difficulty breathing 

-Emphysema: irreversible enlargement of airspaces due to rupture of inner walls of alveoli. This reduces surface area and traps old air due to malfunctioning alveoli, resulting in difficulty breathing 

-Bronchiectasis: permanent dilation of bronchi and bronchioles from damage to airway walls due to infection, cystic fibrosis etc. which causes difficulty removing mucus. Mucus builds up and creates an environment in which bacteria can grow, leading to lung infections.  

-Cancer can cause a blockage of an airway 

 

Describe the triggers and pathological mechanisms which cause reversible and irreversible decreases in airway diameter (inflammation, secretions, bronchoconstriction, airway remodeling, foreign body, cancer) 

Reversible 

-Asthma: inflammation (bronchoconstriction) due to histamines causes airway remodeling, is reversible 

-Foreign body 

Irreversible 

-Chronic bronchitis: hypersecretion of mucus, from inflamed bronchial tubes (bronchoconstriction) 

-Emphysema 

-Bronchiectasis 

-Cancer 

 

Briefly describe the pathologic changes observed in chronic asthma 

-Some narrowing of airway is present continuously in chronic asthma; airway quite normal between acute asthma attacks 

-Episodic bronchoconstriction 

-Increased airway sensitivity to stimuli, resulting in inflammation of bronchial walls and an increase in mucus secretion 

-Inflammation due to an increase in vascular permeability and thus leaky vessels (fluids leaking into extracellular space) 

 

Briefly describe how chronic inflammation can lead to enhanced sensitivity to non-allergic triggers in asthma (cold, exercise, etc.) 

Chronic inflammation elicits a hyper-sensitive immune system, which therefore increases responses to non-allergic triggers.

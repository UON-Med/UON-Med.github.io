Describe the control of the respiratory drive (general principles, central control, central and peripheral chemoreceptors) 

Respiratory muscles are somatic skeletal, and are innervated by spinal motor neurons whose axons form phrenic nerves for the diaphragm and intercostal nerves for the intercostal muscles. Spinal respiratory motoneurons receive their excitatory drive from the respiratory centre which consists of several groups of neurons in the medulla oblongata and the intercostal muscles. The respiratory centre receives sensory feedback from central chemoreceptors in the medulla and peripheral chemoreceptors. Descending input from higher brain centers also occurs.  

 

 

Brainstem respiratory centre 

This is a group of neurons in the medulla, and is the pacesetting respiratory centre where the neural respiratory rhythm is generated. It excites the inspiratory muscles and sets a resting rhythm of 12-15 inspiratory neural bursts per minute. This rhythm steadily increases for about 2 seconds then ceases abruptly for about 3 seconds during the expiratory phase.   

 

Chemoreceptor input into the respiratory centre 

Central chemoreceptors located below the ventral surface of the medulla are activated by an increase in pressure of CO2, or a decrease in pH in the cerebrospinal fluid. The chemosensitive area in the medulla is highly sensitive to changes in CO2 and acidity, as CO2 easily crosses the blood-brain barrier as a gas to produce H+.  

 

Peripheral chemoreceptors are located in the walls of the aortic arch and carotid bifurcation, and are activated by a decrease in pressure of O2 (less so by increased CO2 pressure) in the blood. Stimulation of these chemoreceptors causes increases in minute ventilation by increasing both the respiratory rate and depth. (Minute ventilation = tidal volume x respiratory rate) Peripheral chemoreceptors are located bilaterally in the carotid bodies adjacent to the carotid arteries, and send neural signals to the respiratory centre via the Herring's nerves and the glossopharyngeal nerves. Some peripheral chemoreceptors are located bilaterally in the aortic bodies adjacent to the aortic arch, and send neural signals to the respiratory centre via the vagus. Compared to the chemosensitive area, the peripheral chemoreceptor effects on respiratory drive by CO2 and H+ are weak but rapid. (Chemoreceptors respond very quickly whereas it takes longer for changes in the pressure of oxygen and H+ content to reach the brain).  

 

Other important factors that impact ventilation 

-Voluntary control in vocalisation, breath-holding 

-Reflexes mediated by lung and airway receptors eg irritant and cough receptors in the airways which cause reflex coughing and sneezing 

-Neural afferent signals from somatic muscle and tendon proprioceptors eg immediate increase in ventilation at the onset of exercise 

-General anesthesia 

-Sleep 

 

Describe the relationship between conscious and unconscious control of respiration 

The homeostatic control of breathing is carried out automatically. However, respiratory muscles are somatic and are therefore under voluntary control.  

 

Briefly describe the fundamentals of consciousness and the effect of altered respiration on consciousness.  

Consciousness is a subjective experience and not a physical event. There are different levels of consciousness, and these are clinically assessed in terms of degree of orientation to time, place, person, and degree of response to stimuli.  

 

Describe the physiologic response to airway obstruction 

The obstruction leads to hypoxaemia/hypercapnia, which leads to an increase in the respiratory drive. An increased force is generated by the primary inspiratory muscles (diaphragm and external intercostals). Accessory inspiratory muscles are recruited. Expiratory muscles (normally silent) are recruited to help drive expiratory airflow.

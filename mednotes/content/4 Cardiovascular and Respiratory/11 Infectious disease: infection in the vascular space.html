Using risk factors for valve infection, describe general principles that increase risk of infection in the vascular space (stagnation, flow abnormality, foreign body) 

 

Risk factors for endocarditis include prosthetic heart valves, congenital heart defects, a history of endocarditis, damaged heart valves from hypertension, atherosclerosis, fibrosis, rheumatic fever etc, and intravenous drug use. Other risk factors include cyanotic congenital heart defect (open fossa ovalis, lack of aortic/pulmonary split), endocardial cushion defects (poorly formed walls between chambers), lesions, patent ductus arteriosus, and septal defect.  

 

The stagnation of blood leads to thrombus formation. As thrombi attach to heart valves, blood flow is impeded. As blood flow decreases, the circulation of white blood cells decreases and thus the risk for infection increases.  

 

Flow abnormality/turbulence such as atherosclerosis or aneurysms result in damaged endothelium. Atherosclerosis of the heart valves will thus increase risk of infection. Cholesterol also leads to calcification by combining calcium, fats, and other substances to form plaque. This stiffens the valves and further increases its susceptibility to damage. This results in a greater risk of infection. 

 

The introduction of foreign bodies such as prosthetic implants carry the risk of infection by introducing bacteria into the vascular space. Furthermore, foreign bodies cause bacteria elsewhere in the body to migrate toward it, leading to infection. A malfunctioning implant or other foreign body capable of creating clots will also increase the risk of infection.  

 

Briefly describe common mechanisms that introduce bacteria to the vascular space 

Day to day- efficiently removed by host defenses without chemotherapy or transplant- immunosuppression 

-Tooth cleaning 

-Nose blowing 

-Translocation from the bowel flora 

 

Invasive infections 

Localised skin or mucosal site (respiratory etc) entry can cause either localised or systemic infection. Bacteria colonising the throat leading to minor pharyngitis can invade directly into the bloodstream. Streptococcus pneumonia causing lobar pneumonia can have a secondary spread into the bloodstream.  

 

Healthcare-associated 

-IV lines (entry of skin flora, bacteria from a contaminated catheter hub, bacteria from the infused fluid)  

-Urinary catheters 

-Operative wounds 

-Any invading bacteria when immunosuppressed by transplant or radiation 

Prevention: 1- assess whether a device is needed for a patient and look for suitable alternatives eg oral therapy, 2- antiseptic management during procedures, 3- early device removal 

 

Briefly describe the challenges of identifying bacterial infection in the blood samples 

-Diagnosis of sepsis is difficult as the presence of inflammation as a result of other underlying diseases will produce the same signs as sepsis 

-The use of antibiotics to treat patients will cause microbial cultures to come out negative, impeding the diagnosis of sepsis if it exists 

-Furthermore, culture-dependent diagnosis of infection is slow, and biomarkers are needed to provide a more rapid means of diagnosing sepsis 

-Complexity of sepsis response results in combinations of biomarkers needed, many of which have not yet been found useful 

 

Optimising sepsis detection 

-Careful asepsis during collection 

-Optimised sensitivity, taking into account the lower blood concentration of bacteria in adults as compared to children 

 

Describe the epidemiology of Staphylococcus aureus colonisation and infection 

Staph aureus is a gram-positive pathogen, causing a wide range of infections. They can infect people of all ages, at all body sites, and are spread both through the community and through healthcare facilities.  

 

Staph aureus colonises the skin and mucosal surfaces, and is carried by many healthy individuals.  

 

Risk factors 

Diabetes, dialysis, IV drug use, liver dysfunction, HIV, recurring Staph aureus skin infections 

 

Persistent carriers are at risk of endogenous (from internal cause) infection due to the carried strain post-surgery. Attempts to "decolonise" the carrier patient prior to surgery using nasal ointment are quite successful at reducing wound infection rate.

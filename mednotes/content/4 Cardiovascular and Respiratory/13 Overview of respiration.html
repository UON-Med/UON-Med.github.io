Describe the process of diffusion of gases at the level of the capillary (ie role of concentration gradient, membranes/barriers that must be crossed) 

 

The prime function of capillaries is to allow effective exchange of nutrients (including oxygen) from the blood plasma into the interstitial fluid bathing the body's cells, and metabolic waste (including CO2) from this fluid into the blood plasma. Capillaries have a thin wall consisting of only and endothelium, made of a single layer of endothelial cells and a basal membrane.  

 

The systemic capillary wall is all the separates the systemic blood from the interstitial fluid.

The mechanics of breathing 

Describe the mechanical processes involved in normal breathing.  

Airflow is produced by pressure differences between the alveolar and atmospheric air, with air moving from high pressure to low pressure. A decrease in pressure in the lungs will cause air to move into the lungs. Inspiration is active, involving the contraction of the diaphragm (the principal inspiratory muscle) to lengthen the chest cavity, or by elevation of the ribs by external intercostals to both lengthen the cavity and increase the anterior-posterior diameter of the chest.  

 

Expiration is passive in healthy people, and is driven by elastic recoil of the stretched lung and chest wall. Expiratory muscles can also contract to contribute, if this is insufficient.  

 

How do alveolar and intrapleural pressures change during the respiratory cycle?  

When inspiration occurs, intrapleural and alveolar pressures decrease as the chest wall is pulled away from the lungs. Hence, when expiration occurs, the chest wall pushing against the lungs causes the intrapleural and alveolar pressures to increase.  

 

Describe the methods of maintaining a clear upper airway (including gag and cough reflexes and the function of the epiglottis) 

-Reflex closure of the epiglottis over the trachea acts as a trap door to ensure that non-gaseous material is propelled into the esophagus during swallowing. If inappropriate material bypasses the epiglottis, the cough reflex comes into action which involves the stimulation of general irritant sensors in the larynx or irritant receptors in the lower airways.  

 

Cough receptors on the posterior wall of the trachea, pharynx and at the carina of the trachea send impulses down the internal laryngeal nerve which stems from the Vagus nerve to the medulla oblongata. The cerebral cortex and medulla then transmit relevant signals via the vagus and superior laryngeal nerves to the glottis, external intercostals, diaphragm, and other major inspiratory/expiratory muscles.  

 

Cough mechanism: Diaphragm innervated by phrenic nerve and external intercostal muscles contract, creating a negative pressure around the lung causes air to rush into the lung. The glottis closes and the larynx shuts. The abdominal muscles and other expiratory  muscles contract, increasing pressure in the lung. The glottis opens and air is coughed out. 

 

Inappropriate stimulation of pharyngeal receptors can also evoke the gag reflex which can forcibly eject the material out of the throat by vomiting.  

 

Describe the physical qualities of lung tissue that affect breathing/lung function (compliance, elasticity and surface tension) 

 

Lung compliance is a measure of its distensibility (ease with which lungs can be stretched or inflated). Stiffness is define is impedance to stretch or inflation. Lung compliance at any lung volume is determined from pressure-volume curves of the lungs, and equals the gradient of the pressure-volume curve at this lung volume. Elasticity is how much the lung tissues can be stretched. Surface tension forces act where the air and liquid meet in the alveoli. Water is polar and therefore possesses cohesion forces between its molecules, tending to bead together. However, it possesses adhesion forces to multiple alveoli walls, therefore drawing these alveoli walls toward one another. Hence, alveoli reduce in overall size and increase lung stiffness (2/3 lung stiffness determined by this effect). The body secretes surfactants to reduce surface tension and hence the stiffening effect.

Briefly describe pulmonary surfactant and its role in pulmonary mechanics 

The pulmonary surfactant is a complex mixture of phospholipids and proteins. It is synthesised in alveolar epithelial cells, and forms a thin film at the air-liquid interface. Its purpose is to reduce the surface tension that tends to collapse the alveoli. Without surfactant, much higher pressures are needed to inflate the alveoli.

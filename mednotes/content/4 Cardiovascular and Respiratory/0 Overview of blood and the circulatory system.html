Briefly describe the components of the cardiovascular system and the path of blood flow through them. 

The main components of the circulatory system include: 

-Vessels (Arteries, veins, capillaries)  

-Blood 

-Heart 

-Control mechanisms including pump output and vessel diameter 

 

The human circulatory system includes the pulmonary and systemic circuits. The right ventricle pumps blood through the pulmonary circuit, whilst the left ventricle pumps blood through the systemic circuit. (Ventricles are below atriums) 

 

Functionally, blood vessels can be classified as: 

-Buffer vessels eg aorta and middle-sized arteries 

-Resistance vessels eg small-sized arteries and arterioles 

-Metabolic vessels eg capillaries 

-Capacitance vessels eg veins 

 

Microcirculation: arteries feed to arterioles, which feed to capillaries. Precapillary sphincters exist, but capillaries have no muscular cells in their walls. 

 

 

Briefly describe the roles of the circulatory system in normal bodily function. 

-Delivery of nutrients and oxygen to the tissues 

-Removal of metabolic waste and CO2 

-Delivery of hormones to the target organs 

-Water balance 

-Thermoregulation 

Overall, to provide adequate blood flow to individual organs; note that "adequate" may vary greatly depending on the functional state of an organ.  

 

List the components of blood and the general role of each. 

Blood composition: 

-Water 

-Erythrocyte cells (O2, CO2 transport) 

-Leucocyte cells (immunological defense) 

-Platelet cells (hemostasis, clotting of blood) 

-Proteins 

-Nutrients 

-Electrolytes  

 

Plasma is blood without cells, whilst serum is blood without cells and without clotting proteins 

 

How does hemostasis occur? 

Upon damage to the vascular wall, there is a vascular spasm, with contraction of vascular smooth muscle that is neurally-induced and initiated by local factors. A platelet plug is formed, and the blood clots. Eventually, the growth of fibrous tissue closes the rupture permanently.  

 

 

Describe the basic requirements for maintaining effective circulation (volume, vascular tone, and pump action).  

 

The heart has 4 chambers. The right side supplies the pulmonary circulation: 

-Right atrium receives systemic venous blood from the superior and inferior vena cavae 

-Right ventricle receives blood from the right atrium and pumps the blood to the lungs via the pulmonary trunk and arteries 

 

The left side supplies the systemic circulation: 

-Left atrium receives oxygenated blood from lungs via pulmonary veins 

-Left ventricle receives blood from the left atrium and pumps this to the body via the aorta and systemic arteries 

 

Pump action 

The contraction of cardiac muscle cells in the heart walls will increase the pressure in the cardiac chambers, which pumps the blood around.  

 

The heart contains 4 one-way valves which ensure that the blood flows in one direction through the heart and along the blood vessels. This includes the 2 atrioventricular valves between the atria and ventricles (tricuspid on the right, mitral on the left), and 2 semilunar valves (pulmonary on the right, aortic on the left). 

 

Vascular tone 

Vascular tone is the contraction/dilation of blood vessels to regulate flow. For example during heavy exercise, the precapillary sphincters in areas of low priority will contract, whilst those in the exercising muscles relax to allow greater blood flow.  

 

Blood volume 

The heart normally matches the venous input to the arterial output. Reduced blood volume can be due to blood loss or severe dehydration.  

 

Homeostatic control of blood pressure 

Sensors: baroreceptors 

-Controlling mechanisms: neural mechanisms, hormones and other circulating chemicals, and locally produced hormones 

 

Executing mechanisms: Heart as pump for heart rate and stroke volume, and vascular tone 

 

Input from baroreceptors go through the medulla oblongata, then the hypothalamus. The output comes back through both and is sent to the cardiac and vascular nerves. (However, note that the medulla oblongata is capable of maintaining arterial pressure all on its own. The hypothalamus can be removed without damage to this function.) 

 

Note that there is no parasympathetic innervation of blood vessels. Hence, vascular tone is entirely controlled by the vascular sympathetic nerves, which cause the release of NA thereby causing muscle contraction. 

 

Describe the physical laws that determine flow and pressure, and relate to BP = CO x TPR. 

  

Stroke volume (blood volume ejected per cardia contraction x Heart rate = Cardiac output (ml/min). 

 

Blood pressure = Blood flow x Vascular resistance.  

Note: Vascular Conductance = 1/(vascular resistance).  

 

Diagram the normal hydrostatic pressures in each part of the vascular system (heart chambers to capillaries and back for both pulmonary and system circuits). 

 

-Left ventricle generates highest systole at 120 mmHg 

-Right ventricle rises to 15-30 mmHg during systole 

-Pressures in both ventricles fall close to zero during diastole, but is counteracted by elastic recoil.  

-Blood pressure progressively falls as blood moves along the vessels 

 

 

Relate the structure of each type of vessel to its function in the cardiovascular system  (role in regulation of blood pressure, gas exchange, venous return and capacitance) 

-Aorta: thick vessel wall with large amount of elastic fiber, provides elastic recoil during diastole after being stretched to prevent arterial pressure from falling too low 

-Muscular arteries: contain a large amount of vascular smooth muscle, can undergo strong vasoconstriction to stop hemorrhage and increase vascular resistance to raise arterial blood pressure 

-Arterioles: have a thin wall but retain vascular smooth muscle, and can undergo strong vasoconstriction to increase blood pressure and divert blood flow to areas of need 

-Capillaries: one-cell-thick wall, ideal or exchange of nutrients and waste (including gases) between the blood plasma and interstitial fluid. 

-Veins and venules: have thin collapsible walls with some smooth muscle. External pressure and vasoconstriction can help return significant amounts of pooled venous blood to the right heart, thereby increasing venous return and cardiac output.  

Note: arterioles and venules carry blood from and to the heart respectively.

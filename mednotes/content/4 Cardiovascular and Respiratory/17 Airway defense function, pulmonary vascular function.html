Briefly describe the specific defensive functions of respiratory epithelium, both in airways and in alveoli 

The respiratory epithelia are the contact point between the respiratory system and the external environment (non-sterile air which continuously flows through the airways and alveoli). Thus, the respiratory epithelia perform a number of important defensive functions against infection.  

 

Airway epithelia 

-Mucociliary escalator (cilia sweep foreign material trapped in mucus up toward the mouth and nose, and into esophagus) 

-Secretion of antibodies into the fluid lining the airway lumen (Most IgA in larger airways, mostly IgG in smaller bronchioles and alveoli) 

 

Alveolar epithelia 

-Alveolar macrophages that scout the alveolar lining by moving through the fluid between alveoli to phagocytose inhaled foreign material (there is no mucociliary escalator in the alveoli) 

 

Briefly describe the cough reflex, including anatomical triggers and the mechanism of cough 

Cough receptors on the posterior wall of the trachea, pharynx and at the carina of the trachea are very mechanically sensitive, and they send impulses down the internal laryngeal nerve which stems from the Vagus nerve to the medulla oblongata. The cerebral cortex and medulla then transmit relevant signals via the vagus and superior laryngeal nerves to the glottis, external intercostals, diaphragm, and other major inspiratory/expiratory muscles.  

 

Cough mechanism: Diaphragm innervated by phrenic nerve and external intercostal muscles contract, creating a negative pressure around the lung causes air to rush into the lung. The glottis closes and the larynx shuts. The abdominal muscles and other expiratory  muscles contract, increasing pressure in the lung. The glottis opens and air, mucus, and foreign material are coughed out. 

 

Cough reflexes also occur in other places eg ears, esophagus, stomach, etcetera.  

 

Note: cough receptors in the airway wall are very mechanically sensitive (especially in larynx and tracheal carina where they are easily stimulated by foreign objects or excessive mucus) and chemically sensitive (especially in the smaller bronchioles).  

There is no single class of cough receptors, but irritant receptors predominate. Note that there are also irritant receptors in the nose, stimulation of which can evoke the sneeze reflex. The sneeze reflex mechanism is very similar to the cough reflex, except the explosive blast of expiratory airflow out of the nose instead of the mouth. 

 

 

Briefly describe the process of mucociliary clearance 

Most of the airways are lined internally with ciliated pseudo-stratified epithelium containing mucus-producing goblet cells. The ciliated pseudo-stratified epithelial cells are the most common cells of the respiratory epithelium, each with around 300 contractile cilia on its luminal (apical) surface. The next most common cells are the goblet cells which produce a sticky mucus. Multicellular glands below the epithelium also exist, secreting a less viscous serous liquid. Inhaled particles and pathogens stick to the mucus and are swept up from the tracheobronchial tree by the beating cilia towards the pharynx to be swallowed. Cilia in the nasal passages and nasopharynx sweep mucus downwards to be swallowed. The mucus and serous fluid also help keep the airway epithelium moist.  

 

Chronic exposure to cigarette smoke results in an increase in goblet cell numbers and a reduction in the number of ciliated pseudo-stratified columnar cells in the respiratory epithelium. This results in more mucus, and a poorer ability to clear this mucus. Thus, frequent productive (producing mucus) coughing may be required to clear the excessive mucus from the airways.  

 

Contrast the systemic and pulmonary circulations with respect to pressures, resistance to blood flow, and response to hypoxia 

Pressures 

Systolic right ventricular pressure (25mmHg) is much lower than systolic left ventricular and aortic pressures (120mmHg). Diastolic pressure equals 0mmHg. Systolic pulmonary arterial pressures = systolic right ventricular pressure, whilst diastolic pulmonary arterial pressure = 8mmHg. (Compared to 80mmHg in aorta) Note: pulmonary arteries have some elastic recoil. 

Pulmonary capillary pressure normally equals 7mmHg.  

 

Resistance to blood flow 

The shorter length and somewhat wider internal diameter of the pulmonary vessels means that resistance to blood flow is much lower in pulmonary vessels than in systemic circulation. Total peripheral resistance is calculated as the pressure gradient along the entire systemic circulation (mean arterial pressure - vena cava pressure)/cardiac output. Similarly, pulmonary vascular resistance is calculated as (mean pulmonary arterial pressure - left atrial pressure)/cardiac output. The PVR is generally much less at 3 mmHg min / L than the TPR at 19 mmHg min/L.  

 

Hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction  

A reduction in alveolar pO2 (especially if <70 mmHg) results in pulmonary vasoconstriction, whilst hypoxia in the systemic vasculature causes systemic vasodilation.  

 

Briefly describe the concept of ventilation-perfusion matching in the lung, and the consequences of perfusing a non-ventilated lung 

In order for the lungs to effectively oxygenate and decarbonate the blood, the alveoli need to be both adequately ventilated and perfused. Ventilation-perfusion matching is thus necessary for effective gas exchange. If a lung is collapsed, it is effectively perfused but not effectively ventilated. As such, it does not bring oxygenated venous blood to the heart, and mixes with the oxygenated blood from the good lung to reduce systemic arterial pO2. However, hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction serves a useful function by diverting pulmonary blood flow to where it is useful, ie the good lung.  

 

Briefly describe the pulmonary receptors/reflexes that alter tidal volume and rate 

Important reflexes: 

-Chemoreflex: stimulates respiratory drive, mediated by central chemoreceptors located in the medulla, sensitive to CO2 and H+ (requires CO2 to diffuse across blood-brain barrier), and peripheral chemoreceptors located in the carotid and aortic bodies stimulated by decreased pO2 (weakly sensitive to CO2 and H+) 

-Reflexes mediated by lung and airway receptors eg irritant receptors in the airways which cause reflex coughing and sneezing, includes weak Hering-Breuer inflation reflex where lung inflation stimulates airway stretch receptors to inhibit inspiration 

-Neural afferent signals from somatic muscle and tendon proprioceptors (eg immediate increase in ventilation at the onset of exercise 

 

Briefly describe the response of the respiratory system to vigorous exercise (muscle recruitment, change in lung volumes, rate) 

 

Neurally-mediated changes (start and stop of exercise) in the respiratory drive are important in regulating ventilation during exercise, and produce rapid and potent effects. Chemical factors are important in bringing steady-state adjustments to ventilation to keep pO2, pCO2 and pH levels within normal ranges.  

 

Initial ventilatory response 

When the brain transmits motor impulses to contracting skeletal muscles during exercise, collateral nerve impulses are transmitted to the brain stem to excite the respiratory centre, anticipating the stimulation of ventilation. In addition, joint and muscle proprioceptors in the arms and legs are stimulated by body movements (can be passive) and send excitatory signals to the respiratory centre. These are neurally-mediated effects, producing immediate changes in minute ventilation and alveolar ventilation. At the onset of exercise, arterial pCO2 decreases and arterial pO2 and pH increase initially due to the anticipatory effect.  

 

Stead-state exercise ventilatory response 

During stead-state exercise, minute ventilation and alveolar ventilation increase linearly with respect to increasing vO2 (rate of O2 consumption). The close matching between this increased metabolic rate and increased ventilation in healthy individuals ensures that systemic arterial pO2, pCO2 and pH change very little.  

 

Note: at higher minute ventilation, the increased respiratory frequency contributes more than the increased tidal volume. 

Minute ventilation however will increase disproportionately to vO2 at exercise workloads above the lactate inflection point, because at this workload blood lactate levels begin to increase and the lactic acid's H+ stimulate the respiratory centre.

Interesting aspects of the coronary vessels 

-There are few anastomoses between the coronaries 

-Supply to the walls of the heart can vary, especially posteriorly, eg 70% posterior interventricular artery is right dominant (branches of right coronary artery), 10% is left dominant (branches off circumflex), and 20% are codominant. 

-Since blood flow across the myocardium is from the surface, through the perforating vessel, deep into the heart, perfusion pressure is lowest in sub-endocardial regions 

-Principal coronary vessels are outside the myocardium, but smaller branches are surrounded by cardiac muscle so that they are exposed to compressive forces during systole 

-High density of capillaries 

 

Briefly describe the role of pH and CO2 concentration in the control of tissue perfusion 

CO2 is dissolves in the blood to produce carbonic acid, lowering pH. This is able to cross the blood-brain barrier, where central chemoreceptors detect an increase in pCO2. This stimulates the respiratory drive and increases alveolar ventilation. Chemoreceptors located close to the baroreceptors of the carotid sinus and arch of aorta in carotid and aortic bodies detect changes in blood concentration of O2, CO2, and H+. These chemoreceptors will send impulses to the cardiovascular centre. A lowered pH  causes the cardiovascular centre to increases sympathetic stimulation to the heart, arterioles and veins, causing coronary vasodilation and increased blood pressure. 

 

Discuss how mechanical factors during the cardiac cycle affect coronary blood flow 

 

As per the mathematical equations, there will be small coronary flow back toward the ventricles during systole, and most of the coronary flow will occur during diastole. 

 

Clinical consequences of coronary anatomical and mechanical factors 

-Few anastomoses lead to high risk of cardiac occlusion (most vulnerable is the sub-endocardium with low perfusion pressure) 

Factors that compromise sub-endocardial blood supply include: 

-Excessive rise in systolic pressure  

-Excessive fall in diastolic pressure in aorta 

-Tachycardia 

-Respiratory sinus arrhythmia 

 

Describe the neural, hormonal and endothelial control mechanisms for coronary blood flow 

Neural: sympathetic nerves, and occasionally parasympathetic nerves 

Hormonal: Catecholamines (adrenaline), Angiotensin II (sympathetic), ADH (both excitatory and inhibitory) 

Endothelial: Nitrogen oxide, Prostaglandin, Endothelin 

 

Describe the process of vascular autoregulation in coronary arteries 

Hypoxia is the principal factor for autoregulation of coronary blood flow. Hypoxia leads to the breakdown of ATP in cardiac myocytes, leading to an increase in adenosine. This activates A2 receptors on vascular smooth muscle cells. This increases cAMP and PKA. The activation of K-ATP channels results in hyperpolarisation, therefore allowing relaxation. (CAMP is reduces activity in smooth muscle, as opposed to how it is in cardiac muscle.)

Describe the changes in myocardial oxygen demand and distribution of blood flow during exercise 

Coronary blood flow increases by 367% during exercise. Skin and skeletal muscle increase in demand, whilst, the digestive tract, kidneys, bone decrease in demand. The brain has no change.  

 

Why is the early relief of ischemia so important? 

The production of adenosine from the decomposition of ADP is needed for the activation of A2 receptors, to open potassium channels. Unfortunately, adenosine has the tendency to be washed away, and the reproduction of this adenosine is very slow. The removal of adenosine prevents the re-synthesis of ATP. Vasodilation does not occur, and ischemia continues.

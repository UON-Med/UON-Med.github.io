Briefly describe the contribution of atrial contraction to cardiac output 

-At rest, atrial contraction contributes 10% to ventricular filling, with passive flow from the veins sufficient. Atrial fibrillation may be asymptomatic at rest. 

-At high heart rates, when there's not enough time for passive filling, atria may contribute 30-40% via the atrial kick 

-Note that atrial contribution also depends on atrial contractibility, facilitated by cardiac sympathetic nerves and subject to alteration by medications 

 

Briefly describe the contribution of a well-coordinated ventricular contraction to cardiac output, and the consequence of uncoordinated contraction 

-There needs to be sufficient atrial to ventricular delay for appropriate filling of the ventricles 

-Coordinated contraction of ventricular muscle cells maximises systolic pressure to force all blood through circulation 

-Absence of tetany, so that the heart can fill up again 

 

Briefly describe the relationship between heart rate and stroke volume 

Stroke volume = blood volume ejected from each ventricle during a single cardiac contraction, 60-80ml 

Heart rate = beats per minute, 60-100 bpm 

Cardiac output = Stroke volume x Heart rate, 3.6-8L/min 

Both heart rate and stroke volume are usually either increased or decreased by the same neurotransmitter. 

 

Describe the physiologic processes occurring during exercise that lead to increased demand for cardiac output 

Exercising muscles require energy and produce metabolites. Thus, blood is needed to supply O2 and glucose, whilst removing CO2. 

 

Describe cardiovascular adjustments during exercise 

-Increase in Cardiac Output (Increased stroke volume and heart rate) 

-Increase in respiratory rate 

-Dilation of coronary arteries, skeletal muscle arteries, and skin 

-Vasoconstriction in digestive tract, kidneys, bone 

-Redistribution of blood flow (Increase in skin, heart, skeletal muscle; decrease in digestive tract, kidneys, bone; no change in brain) 

-Increase in Blood Pressure 

 

These changes occur through both extrinsic (neural control of heart rate and vasculature) and intrinsic (metabolic) mechanisms. 

 

Describe central and peripheral mechanisms involved in cardiovascular adjustments during exercise 

 

Central mechanisms 

Exercise Pressor Reflex (EPR) is a pressor reflex response triggered from contracting skeletal muscles. There are two components of EPR- mechanoreflex and metaboreflex. Mechanoreflex involves an immediate rise of arterial pressure initiated by mechanical deformation or stretch. Metaboreflex involves a delayed rise in arterial pressure following stimulation by muscle chemoreceptors by metabolites, with possible activators being lactate, K+, ATP, H+, prostaglandins etc. Both reflexes could be evoked by electrical stimulation of muscles or motor nerves. 

 

Descending neural influences from the higher brain centers activate somatomotor, respiratory and cardiovascular systems. For example, the baroreflex (control of vessels following changes in blood pressure) shifts in sensitivity following influences from the hypothalamic region. 

 

Note: the EPR may also increase Heart Rate. 

 

Peripheral mechanisms 

The increase in energy demand and production of metabolites leads to local vasodilation. 

 

Describe post-exercise hypotension and post-exercise heart rate recovery 

Post-exercise hypotension is initiated by muscle contraction, as it is present following the electrical stimulation of muscles or motor nerves. Mechanisms are poorly understood and may include peripheral humoral factors such as atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP), endothelium-derived relaxing factor (EDRF), endogenous opioids. Neural factors such as the downward resetting of the baroreflex and reduction in muscle sympathetic activity may also be involved. 

 

The fitter an individual, the faster their post-exercise heart rate recovery.

Briefly describe the common mechanisms causing heart failure 

-Hypertension or cholesterol buildup leading to atherosclerosis results in ventricular hypertrophy, clots and aneurysms 

-Cholesterol buildup and calcification at valves leads to changes in both atrial and ventricular walls 

-Fibrosis (after rheumatic fever or hypertrophy) 

-Infective endocarditis 

-Non-infective endocarditis caused by trauma eg catheter passed through right side of heart damaging tricuspid and pulmonary valves 

-Autoimmune diseases leading to pericardial effusion and hence tamponade 

 

 

Briefly describe the mechanisms that maintain cardiac output in the face of outflow obstruction 

-Sympathetic action through baroreceptors and Bainbridge reflex to cause vasodilation, increase heart rate and increase cardiac contractility 

-Frank-starling mechanism: increased preload increases contractility of ventricles 

-Hypertrophy 

 

Compare outflow obstruction to chronic hypertension in terms of effect on myocardium and end organs 

Outflow obstruction 

This is caused by valvular stenosis or a range of congenital heart defects such as a narrowing of the aorta, or an underdeveloped left ventricle. This will cause hypertrophy and fibrosis, damaging the myocardium. Stenosis results in reduced blood pressure in the periphery. This can lead to syncope, blurred vision, and a lack of concentration. Severe blockages can also lead to hypoxia and tissue damage.  

 

Hypertension 

Hypertension increases cardiac workload and results in hypertrophy. This leads toward decreased ventricular volume, fibrosis, decreased myocardial perfusion and can lead to outflow obstruction. Hypertension also leads to atherosclerosis and aneurysm, which increase risks of endocarditis and the formation of thrombi. Thrombi can cause fatal pulmonary embolisms or infarctions. Clots elsewhere in the body can result in tissue damage.  

 

Briefly describe how compensatory mechanisms in the setting of a failing ventricle can become counterproductive 

-Hypertrophy can lead to damage 

-Excess sympathetic action leads to tachycardia and arrhythmia 

-Reduced cardiac output due to a failing ventricle can cause a decrease in blood pressure, but RAAS and Bainbridge/Baroreceptors will increase blood pressure. This increase blood pressure places further stress on the ventricles and damages them further 

 

Briefly describe how increased cardiac work and ventricular hypertrophy increase the risk of myocardial infarction 

These result in increased blood pressure, and hence atherosclerosis and risks of infarction.  

 

Briefly describe the effect of valve regurgitation on cardiac function 

Regurgitation leads to dilated heart chambers. Contractility decreases, and cardiac output decreases. These also increase venous pressure, and can lead to edema. 

 

Briefly describe the general principles used to manage symptoms in chronic cardiac failure 

In hypertrophy, valve regurgitation and aortic/pulmonary stenosis, preload is increased and is therefore managed by a combination of preload-reducing drugs: diuretics, organic nitrates, and ACE inhibitors.  

 

In atherosclerosis/systemic hypertension and aortic/pulmonary stenosis, afterload is increased and is therefore managed by ACE inhibitors, Ca2+ channel blockers, and certain direct vasodilators (particular organic nitrates).  

 

Insufficient contractility eg eccentric hypertrophy can be managed with inotropic drugs: digoxin and beta-adrenergic partial agonists (dobutamine).  

 

Beta-blockers are also used to reduce blood pressure via vasodilation.

Symptoms and signs of chronic kidney disease 

 

 

Describe the consequences of loss of selectivity of the filtration membrane (ie leaky membrane) 

Glomerular damage may follow a range of causes such as immunological injury (autoimmunity), metabolic stress (eg diabetes), deposition of extraneous materials (eg amyloid). After the delicate filtration barrier is damaged, a sequence of pathological responses follows, including: 

-Leukocyte infiltration 

-Resident cell changes eg mesangial or podocyte cell proliferation 

-Extracellular matrix changes eg thickening of glomerular basal lamina 

-Crescent formation due to proliferating cells after blood and clot in Bowman's capsule 

-Clots 

-Glomerulosclerosis (scarring) 

 

Glomerular injury induces downstream injury responses and counter-regulatory adaptations leading to tubulo-interstitial scarring and inflammation. Tubular atrophy can occur, progressively leading to renal failure.  

 

 

 

Large proteins appear in the urine when diseases reduce the charge selectivity or cause the development of large pores. Hypoalbuminemia results when increased protein synthesis and decreased protein catabolism fails to compensate for urinary protein loss. The reduced serum oncotic pressure leads to edema and increased lymphatic flow. Interstitial proteins are returned through lymph to the blood, but this will also get depleted. Note that despite hypoalbuminemia, blood volume may increase due to increased Na+ retention.  

 

The loss of vitamin D through urine will decrease Ca2+ reuptake, and bone density may decrease.  

 

The loss of thyroxine through urine will decrease metabolic rate.  

 

Increased lipoprotein synthesis by the liver in response to hypoalbuminemia carries a risk for cardiovascular events. 

 

Describe the consequences of decreased permeability or surface area of the filtration membrane 

With decreased permeability and surface area of the filtration membrane, uremic retention solutes accumulate in the body due to insufficient filtration. These uremic retention solutes, if capable of exerting adverse biological effects, are termed "uremic toxins". Uremic toxins contribute to inflammation, immune dysfunction, vascular disease, platelet dysfunction, bleeding risk, gut dysbiosis, altered drug metabolism, and CKD progression through uncertain mechanisms.  

 

Describe the response of the kidney to decreasing delivery of filtrate to the tubules 

A decreased delivery of filtrate to the tubules will cause reduced delivery of Na+ and Cl- to the macula densa of the thick ascending loop of Henle. This causes vasodilation of the afferent arteriole through NO and prostaglandin production, and constriction of the efferent arteriole through RAAS (via renin release from juxtaglomerular cells). This increases the glomerular filtration rate.  

 

Describe the role of prostaglandins in maintaining GFR as renal function declines 

Arachidonic acid is converted to prostaglandins and then to various active forms for vasodilation. This increases GFR. The COX-1 pathway is always active and its actions are desirable, whereas the COX-2 pathway is stimulated by cytokines and causes excessive inflammation and damage. As glomeruli are destroyed, local vasodilation in the still-functional glomeruli shunts blood toward themselves.  

Note that the prostaglandins most important are PGE2 and PGI2 (prostacyclin). These increased renal blood flow, and furthermore PGE2 regulates Na+ and H2O reabsorption whilst PGI2 increases K+ secretion through the stimulation of renin production.  

 

Describe the role of the kidney in regulation of red cell mass via erythropoietin and angiotensin 

Erythropoietin (Epo) stimulates red cell production by the bone marrow. Epo-producing cells are interstitial cells found between tubules, which increase in number in response to hypoxia. Increased renin production in response to reduced filtration rate increases the production of angiotensin I and hence angiotensin II. Angiotensin II modulates Epo secretion and further stimulates bone marrow red cell production.  

 

The kidney may also be able to directly produce angiotensin II.

Describe the normal function of the bladder 

The bladder is a storage organ which receives urine from the kidney via the ureters. In abnormally-functioning bladders, pathologies can involve changes to the sensitivity of sensory nerves, structural changes to the sphincters, or structural changes to the pelvic floor muscles.  

 

The urothelium detects local chemicals (products of metabolism and immune cytokines) and releases chemicals in response to this to excite afferent nerves. However, these afferent nerves can also be excited by pro-inflammatory cytokines. Hence, inflammation caused by infection/irritation can change the sensitivity of these nerves, with an example being oversensitivity to stretch.  

 

Describe the role of each of the following in the maintenance of continence: detrusor muscle, internal and external sphincter, pelvic floor, autonomic and somatic nervous systems 

 

Detrusor muscle, internal + external sphincters 

Contraction of the detrusor muscle through M3 causes the outflow of urine into the urethra. Relaxation occurs through the beta-3 receptor, and internal sphincter contraction occurs through alpha-1. Thus, sympathetic action can stop urination. It must be noted that the external sphincter (skeletal muscle, nicotinic) can be contracted voluntarily to stop urination.  

 

Pelvic floor 

Contraction of the pelvic floor muscles lifts the internal organs, and sphincters cause tightening of the urethra. Relaxation of the pelvic floor muscles allows urination and defecation. 

 

Autonomic and somatic nervous systems 

When bladder volume is low, urinary outflow is inhibited as activity in the sympathetic pathway is greater than activity in the parasympathetic pathway. When filling causes the bladder to reach a critical volume, the increased sensory activity reaches a threshold beyond which it passes through the Barrington's nucleus and greatly increases sphincter relaxation.  

 

During the emptying process, spinal interneurons inhibit the sympathetic and somatic signals. Note: the Onuf's nucleus is connected to somatic motor neurons, and inhibition of the Onuf's nucleus causes relaxation of the external sphincter. 

 

Describe the function of the ureters and the prevention of backward flow of urine (reflux) 

The ureters supply urine from the kidneys to the bladder. The ureterovesical valves at the trigone prevent the backflow of urine.  

 

Describe micturition control in an infant and the development of voluntary control of micturition (urination) 

In neonates, a spinal reflex activates once the bladder is full to cause the contraction of the bladder wall smooth muscle and relaxation of sphincters around the urethra. After age 3-7 however, a powerful descending pathway from the brain develops to override and modulate the spinal reflex, hence voluntary control of urination.

Briefly describe the average daily energy intake and expenditure of a healthy adult 

The basal metabolic rate of a healthy adult is around 30 Cal/kg. For a 70kg man, this equates to around 2100 Cal or 8786 kJ. 8700kJ is the quoted average healthy daily energy intake. 

1 Cal or 1kcal = 4.2 kJ 

 

Gender, age, ambient temperature and various stressors such as infection impact the body's metabolic rate and energy needs.  

 

Average daily energy expenditure will be also around 8700kJ, if there are to be no significant changes to weight over time. 

 

The body stores enough glycogen to survive half a day (1050 Cal), enough protein (only half of protein can be mobilised as fuel source) to survive around 10 days (21000 Cal), and enough lipids to survive around 2 months (131 600 Cal).  

 

In terms of energy density, carbs < protein < alcohol < fat 

 

Changes to metabolic rate and energy needs 

-Digestion + absorption requires a 20-25% increase for 90 minutes from BMR 

-Storage: requires an elevation of 3-7% when storing glycogen/lipids, requires up to 25% when storing proteins (from diet) 

 

Briefly describe the range of factors that can cause variation in energy intake and expenditure including: 

-Regulation of appetite 

-Role of exercise and physical activity 

-Role of adipose tissue and adipokines 

 

Regulation of appetite 

Appetite is regulated by the arcuate nucleus of the hypothalamus. It receives downward regulatory signals from the forebrain and limbic system, and ascending regulatory signals from the GIT, pancreas, and fatty tissue. Two substances are produced at the arcuate nucleus: Neuropeptide Y (NPY, increases appetite) and POMC (pro-opiomelanocortin, decreases appetite). Signals received by the hypothalamus control the production of each peptide. NPY is a neurotransmitter, whilst POMC is a pro-hormone.  

 

Pleasure (from the ventral tegmental area and the Accumbens nucleus) increases NPY and inhibits POMC, whilst aversion (from the amygdala) increases POMC and inhibits NPY. Other signals include: 

 

Only Ghrelin increases appetite. 

 

 

Exercise and physical activity 

Physical stress usually decreases appetite during exercise, but increases appetite after this period due to the prior expenditure of energy.  

 

Role of adipose tissue and adipokines 

Leptin, insulin, and metabolic hormones related to fat stores control body weight over the long term through effects on feeding and energy expenditure, as shown above.  

 

Neuronal and hormonal signals directly from the GI tract mainly simply control hunger. Note that food has pleasurable qualities that drive appetite beyond metabolic needs. 

 

 

Overview of the role of the hypothalamus and pituitary in homeostasis and regulation, including portal circulation 

The hypothalamus initiates efferent control through both the endocrine system via the pituitary, and through the autonomic nervous system via sympathetic and parasympathetic nerves. It also controls neural centers in the lower brainstem for regulation of vital visceral function.  

 

The hypothalamus controls appetite by housing the arcuate nucleus, controls circadian rhythm, controls metabolic rate through thyroid hormones, and controls temperature. All of these factors impact energy intake and expenditure.  

 

Circadian rhythm 

The Suprachiasmatic Nucleus (SCN) of the hypothalamus involves the brain's biological clock, can be modulated by light, and outputs signals to the forebrain (attention, emotion), the hypothalamus (hormonal control, metabolism, orexin production), and to the brainstem (arousal, BP, HR). 

 

Adenohypophysis 

Activation of the hypothalamus causes secretion of releasing factors (often labelled RF or RH) into the hypophyseal portal vein, which activates relevant secretory cells in the adenohypophysis. This is released into blood for command of other endocrine glands or end-organs.  

 

Obesity due to hormonal dysregulation: 

-Hypothyroidism: Thyrotropin releasing hormone (TRH) from hypothalamus causes adenohypophysis to secrete TSH,  which causes the thyroid gland to secrete thyroid hormones. Hypothyroidism occurs from a malfunction of the thyroid (often due to iodine deficiency), which causes a deficiency of thyroid hormones in the bloodstream to control metabolism. As such, metabolic rate decreases.  

 

-Hypercortisolism: Corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRF) from hypothalamus causes adenohypophysis to secrete adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH). This causes the adrenal glands to secrete cortisol, which increases the metabolism of glucose/fats/proteins, resulting in high blood glucose and high insulin. The high glucose is then converted specifically into dangerous visceral fat. 

 

Temperature 

The warmer the body, the higher the metabolic rate. If exposed to cold temperatures, the body will expend further calories to maintain body temperature. Temperature is detected by thermosensitive ion channels externally on the skin, internally in the abdominal and thoracic cavity, and at the dorsal root ganglion. These channels cause firing at different frequencies depending on the temperature. (Higher the temperature, higher the frequency).

Describe the functional divisions of the nephron and the role of each 

Glomerulus: filtrate of plasma and solutes passes into the glomerular capsule 

Proximal tubule: drains the glomerular capsule, highly involved in reabsorption of ions 

Distal tubule: follows on from the proximal tubule, highly involved in influx of ions from blood (secretion) 

Juxtaglomerular apparatus: composed of macula densa (detects levels of Na+) and juxtaglomerular cells (secrete renin) 

Peritubular capillaries: provide nutrients to tubules and have high oncotic pressure to retrieve fluid 

 

Define glomerular filtration rate and the effect on it of vasoconstriction/vasodilation of the afferent and efferent arterioles 

Glomerular filtration rate is the amount of fluid passing through each unit of glomerular surface area per minute. In an average sized kidney, 125ml of filtrate passes per minute. Renal blood flow is around 1L per minute (out of cardiac output of 5L per minute).  

 

Constriction of afferent + dilation of efferent = decreased GFR 

Dilation of afferent + constriction of efferent = increased GFR

Sodium and urea 

Sodium is actively transported in order to produce an osmotic gradient capable of driving water reabsorption. Urea also contributes to osmolarity in the renal medullary interstitium, and can contribute to up to 50% of this osmolarity when maximally concentrated urine is formed. How?? 

 

Medullary gradient 

The medullary gradient allows for the passive diffusion of solutes back into the blood. This includes HCO3-, PO43-, SO42- etc.

Autoregulation 

Tubuloglomerular feedback: the macula densa measures GFR and signals the juxtaglomerular cells (using adenosine) to secrete renin as required to increase aldosterone levels.  

 

The myogenic response involves contraction/relaxation of the vascular smooth muscle in response to stretch-sensitive mechanoreceptors.

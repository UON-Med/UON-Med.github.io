Define digestion 

The digestive process is the enzymatic conversion of complex dietary substances to a form that can be absorbed in the intestines.  

 

Describe the overall function of the small and large bowel in digestion and absorption macro- and micronutrients 

Macronutrients 

Carbohydrates, proteins and fats are absorbed most in the duodenum, some in the jejunum, and a small amount in the ileum.  

 

Micronutrients 

Calcium, iron, folate have high absorption in the duodenum. There is some calcium absorption in jejunum and ileum. Cobalamin B12 is absorbed in the ileum with intrinsic factor. Bile acids are absorbed in primarily the ileum, with some absorbed in the jejunum and large bowel, and a very small amount absorbed in the duodenum.  

 

Describe how rates of absorption are affected by the macroscopic and microscopic architecture of the gut epithelium 

Absorption of carbohydrates 

Salivary and pancreatic (in small intestine) amylase digest starch and disaccharides into oligosaccharides and disaccharides. Brush-border enzymes in the small intestine then digest these into galactose, glucose, and fructose. Glucose and galactose are absorbed via cotransport (secondary active to Na+/K+ ATPase) with Na+ ions. Fructose passes via facilitated diffusion. All 3 are monosaccharides, and leave the epithelial cells via facilitated diffusion into capillary blood in the villi. 

 

Absorption of proteins 

Pepsin and HCl from the stomach breaks protein down to large polypeptides, pancreatic proteases in small intestine break these into small polypeptides and peptides, brush border enzymes break these into amino acids. Amino acids are absorbed by secondary active transport into the absorptive cells. Some peptidases continue to work inside the cell. They move to the opposite side (transcytosis) and leave the villus epithelial cell by facilitated diffusion, entering the capillary via intercellular clefts.   

 

Absorption of lipids 

Lingual (tongue) lipase from tongue glands begins hydrolysis of triglycerides, beginning in mouth and continuing to the stomach. However, this lipase is water soluble and thus its action is limited due to the hydrophobic nature of lipids. Pancreatic lipase also helps break a triglyceride into a fatty acid + monoglyceride. Large fat globules are emulsified (physically broken down into smaller droplets) by bile salts in the duodenum. After they are broken into fatty acid + monoglyceride, they attach to bile salts to form micelles made of all 3. Micelles ferry them to the intestinal mucosa, where the fatty acid and monoglyceride leaves the micelle. They diffuse into the epithelial cells, where they are repackaged with other lipoid substances and proteins to from chylomicrons. Chylomicrons undergo exocytosis, then enter lacteals (lymph).  

 

Absorption of nucleic acids 

Pancreatic ribo-and deoxyribonuclease, and brush border enzymes convert nucleic acids to pentose sugars, nitrogenous bases, and phosphate ions in the small intestine. Units enter intestinal cells by active transport via membrane carriers.  

 

Briefly describe the interaction between motility and absorption 

Decreased motility favors absorption. 

 

Describe the GI hormones that control the process of digestion 

 

Most importantly: 

-Cholecystokinin stimulates gallbladder contraction and intestinal motility, stimulates secretion of pancreatic enzymes 

-Gastrin stimulates the secretion of gastric acid, pepsinogen, intrinsic factor, increases gastric + intestinal motility, causes release of secretin 

-Secretin (S cells in small intestine only!!) stimulates pancreatic secretion of HCO3-, enzymes, reduces gastric and duodenal motility, inhibits gastrin release and gastric acid secretion 

-Somatostatin (D cells in stomach, duodenum and pancreas) inhibits secretion acid and enzyme secretion 

 

 

Describe the consequences to GIT function of malabsorption 

-Insufficient absorption can cause excessive osmotic pressure drawing water into the lumen, causing osmotic diarrhea 

-Nausea resulting in vomiting 

-Edema, ascites from loss of oncotic pressure due to protein malabsorption 

-Bleeding tendencies from vitamin K and other coagulating factor deficiencies 

-Malabsorption increases risk of infection

Describe the role of the oral cavity in digestion 

-Receives food from outside 

-Mechanical digestion through mastication of teeth and tongue 

-Mixing of food with saliva (enzymes in saliva begin the digestion process, mucus in saliva lubricates the food, forms a bolus and allows transfer to esophagus) 

-Gustation (tasting) 

 

5 flavors: sweet, sour, salty, bitter, umami 

Any taste can be detected at any area of the tongue, with dissolved food elements in saliva binding to sensory cells. Taste receptors send information to gustatory centers of the brain through cranial nerves VII, IX, X (facial, glossopharyngeal, vagus) 

 

Major salivary glands: 

-Sublingual gland 70% 

-Parotid gland 20% 

-Submandibular gland 10% 

 

There is one of each gland on each side. Saliva is secreted into the mouth by ducts present under the tongue (sublingual, submandibular) and near upper molars (parotid) 

 

Saliva contains: 

-Enzymes amylase (digestion of starch to glucose and maltose), lipase (triglycerides to diglycerides and free fatty acids) 

-Mucus (lubricates food, adheres food particles into a bolus for swallowing) 

-Leukocytes, lysozyme and secretory IgA for antimicrobial activity 

 

Autonomic nervous control 

-Sympathetic input creates more viscous saliva, facilitating respiration, sticking to surfaces and preventing desiccation  

-Parasympathetic input creates watery saliva to facilitate digestion 

 

Input from salivary nuclei in the brain; sights, sounds and memories; also activate salivation.  

 

Describe the mechanism of swallowing 

Oral phase - voluntary 

Food is moistened with saliva, with parasympathetic input. Mucus lubricates and prepares a bolus, with mechanical breakdown of food by the teeth. The lingual nerve of the tongue senses the state of the bolus; swallowing does not continue if it is too dry. A trough is then formed at the back of the tongue, with bolus forced to the back of the throat due to the downward slope. Lips contract to seal the oral cavity, and once bolus reaches the palatoglossal arch the pharyngeal phase is initiated by proprioceptors.  

 

Pharyngeal phase - involuntary 

The soft palate is elevated to close the nasopharynx, preventing bolus from entering nasal cavity. Pharyngeal peristalsis occurs. Elevation and closure of the larynx by the epiglottis and vocal cord muscles produces swallowing apnea, so food doesn't go into windpipe. Relaxation of the upper esophageal sphincter then occurs. Fast transit time of 40cm/s.  

 

Esophageal phase - involuntary 

Bolus enters esophagus, and peristalsis pushes bolus towards stomach. Lower esophageal sphincter relaxes for bolus to enter stomach. There is a slow transit time of about 5cm/s, 20s for bolus to completely transit the esophagus. Larynx and pharynx move to original position by elastic recoil.  

 

Briefly describe the mechanisms that contribute to propulsion of food down the esophagus 

Peristalsis- contraction behind bolus, relaxation in front of bolus by circular smooth muscle. Mucus lining and wet nature of food minimises friction with the lumen walls.  

 

Describe the normal functioning of the upper and lower esophageal sphincters 

Upper esophageal sphincter opens at end of pharyngeal phase for food to pass into the esophagus. It then closes. Lower esophageal sphincter opens at end of esophageal phase for food to pass into the stomach. It then closes. 

 

Describe the storage, digestion and motility roles of the stomach 

Storage 

Rugae and smooth muscles allow expansion of the stomach, with vagovagal reflex causing smooth muscle relaxation in response to swallowing of food. An empty stomach at 75ml can expand to an average full volume of 1.5L, to a max volume of 4L. Temporary storage of ingested food is in the stomach, with a typical meal taking about 1 hour to be emptied from the stomach.  

 

Digestion 

HCl denatures proteins, and activates pepsinogen (secreted by stomach) into pepsin. Pepsin hydrolyses amide bonds, digesting protein molecules. Gastric lipase hydrolyses lipid molecules and digests fat.  

 

Motility 

When stomach is full, peristaltic waves begin and cause contraction in all three muscle layers. Wave happens 3 times per minute. This causes churning of contents with gastric juice (mechanical digestion). Contraction causes contents to move from pylorus to antrum and back again. Contents become a watery, acidic mixture (chyme), with the pyloric sphincter allowing some chyme into the duodenum with each wave.  

 

Describe the secretions of the various gastric cell types and the role of each 

The mucosal surface of the stomach is covered in gastric pits, and below each of these 2-3 gastric glands connect to the base of each pit. Gastric glands are tubular structures containing a number of specialised cell types: parietal, chief, goblet and D and G enteroendocrine.  

 

-Parietal cells are found throughout the fundus, corpus and antrum. They secrete HCl.  

 

-Chief cells are found predominantly in the corpus, and secrete pepsinogen (a zymogen/inactive precursor to pepsin). When pepsinogen is secreted into the stomach, the acidic environment removes the masking amino acid from the active site on pepsin to then activate the pepsin enzyme. Pepsin hydrolyses protein residues in the chyme. 

 

-Goblet cells secrete alkaline mucus, and are found just inside gastric pits. This mucus protects the epithelium of the stomach from the acidic, proteolytic environment.  

 

-Enteroendocrine cells (D secreting somatostatin to inhibit acid secretion, G secreting gastrin to stimulate acid secretion) 

 

-Enterochromaffin-like cells (ECL) secreting histamine (stimulates acid secretion) 

 

Describe the mechanisms and regulation of acid secretion by the stomach 

 

ACH, histamine and gastrin all stimulate acid secretion. Somatostatin inhibits acid secretion.  

 

Cephalic phase 

In the cephalic phase, sights, smells, tastes activate the parasympathetic enteric neurons. ACH acts directly on parietal cells to increase acid secretion. Excitatory effects on G cells and ECLs cause gastrin and histamine to be secreted, increasing acid secretion.  

 

Gastric phase 

Food stretches the stomach, and neural reflexes release ACH from the enteric neurons to increase acid secretion. Food also releases peptides which stimulate G cells to release gastrin. Food also buffers pH, with increased pH reducing somatostatin production and hence increasing acid secretion.  

 

Intestinal phase 

Chyme in the duodenum activates a negative feedback mechanism to slow acid production. Neural reflexes prevent ACH release, decreasing acid secretion. Enterogastrones (hormones including cholecystokinin, secretin) secreted by the intestines (epithelial cells eg S cells for secretin) inhibit the ECLs and G cells to decrease acid secretion.  

 

Distinguish endocrine from paracrine and autocrine 

Endocrine involves a cell secreting a hormone which acts on distant cells via transport through bloodstream.  

Paracrine involves a cell secreting a molecule which acts on nearby cells. 

Autocrine involves a cell secreting a molecule which acts on itself.  

 

In the stomach: 

-Histamine acts as a paracrine signal 

-Gastrin and somatostatin act as both paracrine and endocrine signals 

 

Describe the mechanisms contributing to gastric mucosal defense 

-Alkaline mucus neutralises low pH over epithelial cells to protect these cells 

-High cellular turnover replaces damaged cells 

-Secretion of inactive precursors minimises damage to epithelial cells (eg HCl secreted as ions, pepsin secreted as pepsinogen) 

-Microbial growth prohibited due to acidic, proteolytic environment 

-Leukocytes are present in the gastric mucosa 

 

Note: 

Helicobacter pylori uses flagella to burrow into epithelial cells where pH is less acidic, then secretes urease to convert urea into CO2 and NH3, neutralises acid and creates neutral pH bubble around the bacteria. Urea is already found in small amounts in the stomach, as urea enters from the blood (from liver metabolism of protein). It also produces proteins which can block T-cell responses and induce epithelial apoptosis. It can also cause the loss of tight junctions and lead to chronic inflammation.  H Pylori furthermore prevents gastric acid inhibition and increases acid secretion whilst decreasing mucosal defense.  

 

Treatment with triple therapy: clarithromycin, amoxycillin, PPI 

 

Note: Fimbriae are used to attach to the GI wall. 

 

Briefly describe the roles of prostaglandins in the stomach and elsewhere 

Prostaglandins have a cytoprotective effect on epithelial GIT cells, by stimulating mucus and bicarbonate secretion, mucosal blood flow, and suppressing the diffusion of acid into the mucosa by reducing acid secretion.  

 

NSAIDs inhibit prostaglandin synthesis from arachidonic acid, and are thus associated with gastric erosions and ulcers.  

 

Elsewhere, it acts as a vasodilator, inhibits platelet aggregation, and sensitises spinal neurons to pain.

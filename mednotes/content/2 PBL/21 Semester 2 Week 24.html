Define constipation and briefly describe its common causes 

Primary constipation 

-Normal transit constipation involves normal colonic motility, and normal physical examination. There is difficulty passing stools due to hardened stools or irritable bowel syndrome. IBS-C involves more abdominal pain. 

-Slow transit constipation involves infrequent bowel movements and less urgency, and straining is involved in attempting to defecate. There is impaired colonic motility. This usually involves a bowel obstruction due to hernia, adhesion, volvulus, intussusception, or tumour. Alternatively, autonomic neuropathy can impair colonic motility, and is caused by diabetes, hypercalcemia/hyperparathyroidism, brain/spine damage or tumour.  

-Pelvic floor dysfunction  

 

Secondary constipation 

-Dehydration, diet 

-Systemic diseases eg hypercalcemia/hyperparathyroidism, hyperkalemia, diabetes; neurologic injury 

-Structural obstruction eg anal fissures, thrombosed hemorrhoids, colonic strictures, tumour, megarectum 

 

Briefly describe the potential consequences of constipation 

-Anal fissures from passing of hard stools 

-Fecal impaction, infection, damage to lumen 

-Hemorrhoids 

-Rectal prolapse 

-Malnourishment from avoiding certain foods 

 

Briefly describe the mechanism of action of common medications used by patients, pharmacists, and doctors to treat constipation 

-Bulk forming agent: fiber can be given long-term, and provide long-term benefits (as opposed to fast relief) 

-Emollient stool softeners: rapid relief, but effectiveness is lost with long-term administration 

-Rapidly acting lubricants and laxatives provide rapid relief, but long-term use causes habituation and toxicity 

-Prokinetic agent eg prucalopride antagonises 5-HT4 to increase colonic motility 

-Osmotic agent/prosecretory drug which stimulates intestinal fluid secretion 

 

Briefly describe the concept of 'red flag symptoms,' and apply this to history taking for GI problems 

Red-flag symptoms require immediate attention, but may not necessarily involve a medical emergency. These include: 

-Vomiting coffee-ground material: upper GI bleeding  

-Unexplained chest pain 

-Significant weight loss 

-Black tarry stools: upper GI bleeding 

 

Briefly describe the procedure and use of fecal occult blood test (FOBT) 

Stool samples are collected from each of two or three bowel movements. A smear is then examined under microscope for hidden (occult) blood. This test is used for screening of colon polyps or cancer. Inflammatory bowel disease or diverticulitis can also cause blood in stool. Anal fissures, hemorrhoids etc can be ruled out with examination.  

 

Briefly describe the common causes of macro- and microscopic blood in stool 

Macroscopic 

Anal fissures, hemorrhoids, inflammatory bowel disease, ulcer, diverticulitis 

 

Microscopic 

Colon polyps, cancer 

 

Define cachexia and briefly describe how cancer can result in weight loss 

Cachexia involves marked weight loss and muscle wastage. Cancer can cause decreased appetite, difficulty swallowing, nausea, depression, and therefore lead to weight loss. Tumour necrosis factor (TNF) is a multifunctional cytokine involved in carcinogenesis. TNF can be a tumour promoter, but can also kill cancer cells. TNF regulates energy balance by promoting lipid and protein mobilisation and by suppressing appetite, and thus its increased activity in cancer contributes to cachexia.  

 

Briefly describe the epidemiology of colon cancer 

Colorectal adenocarcinoma is responsible for nearly 10% of cancer deaths. Each year, approximately 600 000 deaths occur, with 1.2 million new cases. It is highest at 15% in USA, with high rates also found in Australia, New Zealand, and Europe. Incidence rates peak between 60 and 70 years of age.  

 

Briefly describe the risk factors of colon cancer 

-Age 

-Dietary factors such as low unabsorbable fiber, high refined sugars, high fat 

-Sedentary lifestyle 

-Family history of colon cancer 

-Inflammatory bowel disorders 

 

Briefly describe the common symptoms and presentation of colon cancer 

-May be screened for and detected before symptoms 

In advanced cases: 

-Rectal bleeding 

-Abdominal pain 

-Right sided lesions more likely to bleed and cause diarrhea, left sided lesions usually present as bowel obstruction 

-Weight loss

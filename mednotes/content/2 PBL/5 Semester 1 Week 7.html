Define cyanosis and briefly describe the interacting effects of oxygenation, hemoglobin concentration and blood flow on its appearance 

Cyanosis is a bluish appearance which results when approximately 5g/deciliter of deoxyhemoglobin builds up in the capillaries. Hence, increased oxygenation will reduce cyanosis whilst decreased oxygenation will increase cyanosis. Increased hemoglobin will increase cyanosis, and decreased hemoglobin will reduce cyanosis. Increased blood flow decreases cyanosis by refreshing the blood, whilst reduced blood flow increases cyanosis. 

 

Anemic patients can be hypoxemic (low O2 saturation) without presenting cyanosis, as they have less hemoglobin in the blood. 

 

Briefly describe and compare accuracy in assessing adequacy of oxygenation using clinical exam, pulse oximetry, and arterial blood gas 

Clinical exam: check respiratory rate, cyanosis, posturing, and usage of intercostal/accessory muscles. This provides a rough estimate of respiratory adequacy.  

Pulse oximetry: measures oxygen saturation at the peripheries (fingers), and is less accurate than ABG as it measures SpO2 which is an indirect measurement of oxygen saturation.  

Arterial blood gas: measures partial pressures of O2 and CO2, HCO3- concentration and pH. This measures SaO2 which is a direct measurement of arterial oxygen saturation. 

 

Differentiate pulse oximetry and arterial blood gas, in terms of method, uses, and exactly what is measured (ie oxygen saturation vs partial pressure) 

Pulse oximetry is non-invasive but is less accurate, measuring the oxygen saturation at the peripheries. 

An ABG is invasive but more accurate, measuring partial pressure to provide a more accurate value of SaO2.

-Difference between expiratory and inspiratory wheeze, and what they tell us 

Both refer to the phase during which wheezing is heard. Bronchiolar disease causes wheezing in the expiratory phase, as this form of wheezing indicates that the patient's peak expiratory flow rate is less than 50% of normal. Stiff stenosis caused by tumors, foreign bodies are scarring usually lead to wheezing heard in the inspiratory phase.  

 

The type of wheeze is also important. Diffuse processes that affect most parts of the lung will cause wheezing that is heard throughout the chest whereas localized processes eg an occlusion will produce wheezing in that location.  

 

An upper respiratory tract obstruction often causes a stridor (heard only in the expiratory phase).

-Periphery cyanosis and central cyanosis 

Cyanosis is the bluish or purplish discoloration of the skin or mucous membranes due to the tissues near the skin surface having low oxygen saturation.  

 

Peripheral cyanosis refers to cyanosis impacting the limbs, extremities and fingers, and can be caused due to exposure to cold, shock, congestive heart failure, peripheral vascular disease, which all constrict the blood vessels. Low oxygen states can also cause cyanosis, eg pneumonia, CO poisoning, cardiac tamponade, emphysema, pulmonary embolism.  

 

Central cyanosis refers to discoloration of the mucous membranes of the lips and tongue. This can be caused by intracranial hemorrhage, pneumonia, pulmonary embolism, heart failure, etc.  

 

-Epiglottitis 

This is the swelling of the epiglottis which leads to the blocking of air to the lungs. This can be caused by burns from hot liquids, trauma, infection, etc. This is treated by wearing an oxygen mask, intubation or needle tracheostomy, along with appropriate treatment for its cause.  

 

-Croup 

This is a viral infection which causes swelling of the larynx and trachea, leading to difficulty in breathing. A harsh and barking cough results. Steroids or nebulised adrenaline are used as treatment options.  

 

-Cough reflex 

Cough receptors on the posterior wall of the trachea, pharynx and at the carina of the trachea send impulses down the internal laryngeal nerve which stems from the Vagus nerve to the medulla oblongata. The cerebral cortex and medulla then transmit relevant signals via the vagus and superior laryngeal nerves to the glottis, external intercostals, diaphragm, and other major inspiratory/expiratory muscles.  

 

Cough mechanism: Diaphragm innervated by phrenic nerve and external intercostal muscles contract, creating a negative pressure around the lung causes air to rush into the lung. The glottis closes and the larynx shuts. The abdominal muscles and other expiratory  muscles contract, increasing pressure in the lung. The glottis opens and air is coughed out.

-Pulse oximetry 

This is a non-invasive method for measuring a patient's oxygen saturation. Pulse oximetry measures the percentage of hemoglobin that is loaded. The mechanism uses an electronic processor and a pair of small LEDs facing a photodiode through a translucent part of the patient's body, usually a fingertip or an earlobe.  

 

-Whooping cough 

This is caused by bacterial infection, with a major symptom the characteristic "whooping" sound on inhalation following a cough. Vomiting might come after bouts of coughing, and apnoea may occur in children as they stop breathing for periods of time. The disease is much more severe in babies less than 6 months of age as they are particularly prone to complications. These include hemorrhage, pneumonia, inflammation of the brain, coma, and death. This is treated by antibiotics. Immunisation is the most effective method at preventing this disease.

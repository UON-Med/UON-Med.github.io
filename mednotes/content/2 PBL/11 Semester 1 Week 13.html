Describe the symptoms and clinical assessment of both and peripheral edema 

Pulmonary edema 

Symptoms: 

-Extreme shortness of breath or difficulty breathing that worsens when lying down 

-Cough producing frothy sputum, tinged with blood 

-Chest pain if caused by heart disease 

-Rapid, irregular heart beat (palpitations) 

 

Long term: 

-Awakening at night with a breathless feeling that is relieved by sitting up 

-Swelling in lower extremities 

 

Clinical assessment: 

-Chest X-ray 

-Pulse oximetry 

-Blood tests to look for troponin and observe thyroid function, looking for possible causes of the pulmonary edema 

-ECG to observe heart 

-Echocardiogram to image heart if ECG is insufficient 

 

Peripheral edema 

Symptoms: 

-Swelling or puffiness of tissue directly under skin (hands, arms, feet, ankles, legs) 

-Skin that retains a dimple after being pressed for several seconds 

 

Clinical assessment: 

Causes of edema include congestive heart failure, liver cirrhosis which causes fluid buildup in abdomen and legs, kidney damage (loss of blood albumin) or disease (inadequate secretion of sodium), vein damage in legs (allows blood to pool in leg veins when one-way valves are compromised), and inadequate lymphatic drainage. Thus, a thorough history will be taken. Other assessment tools include: 

-ECG to monitor heart 

-Blood test for albumin, O2 saturation, troponin, thyroid function, etc 

-X-rays to look for clots and other symptoms of disease 

-UECs to look for excess sodium 

 

Compare and contrast peripheral and pulmonary edema in terms of location, pressures, drainage, and consequences 

Location 

Pulmonary: In the lungs 

Peripheral: lower extremities, hands and arms, abdominal cavity 

 

Pressures 

Peripheral: in right-sided heart failure, blood backs up into the venous circulation to cause an elevation in venous pressure. This increases capillary hydrostatic pressure and results in edema. (Right atrial mean pressure 2-6mmHg, right ventricle end-diastolic 5mmHg) 

Pulmonary: in left-sided heart failure, blood backs up into the pulmonary venous circulation to similarly result in elevated venous and thus capillary pressure. This leads to pulmonary edema. (Pulmonary capillary wedge mean 12-16mmHg, left ventricle end-diastolic 12-16mmHg) 

 

Define heart failure and describe the associated signs and symptoms of right and left failure 

Heart failure is the failure of the heart to pump blood with normal efficiency.  

 

Right heart failure leads to the congestion of peripheral tissues, and thus peripheral edema and ascites (fluid accumulation in abdominal cavity). Congestion of the GI tract leads to anorexia and GI distress. Congestion of the liver impairs liver function. The liver is responsible for producing angiotensinogen, crucial to the function of the RAAS system in elevating blood pressure. Other liver failure-related symptoms exist.  

 

Left heart failure leads to decreased cardiac output, resulting in intolerance to physical activity, and signs of decreased tissue perfusion. Pulmonary congestion also results, causing impaired gas exchange, cyanosis, and signs of hypoxia. Pulmonary edema symptoms also result. 

 

Briefly describe the clinical use of the JVP in assessment of heart failure 

An elevated JVP points toward heart failure, constrictive pericarditis, cardiac tamponade, and a few other problems. An examination of the JVP waveform can be used to diagnose further problems.  

 

Define cardiomyopathy and describe common causes. 

Cardiomyopathy is damage to the heart muscle, where it becomes enlarged, rigid or thick. This reduces the ability for the heart to pump and deliver blood to the rest of the body, possibly leading to heart failure.  

Genetic 

-Dilated cardiomyopathy: weakening the chamber walls of the heart through stretching, caused by toxic, metabolic or infectious agents 

-Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy: causes thickening of the heart's walls 

-Restrictive cardiomyopathy: heart is restricted from stretching properly, caused by amyloidosis, sarcoidosis 

-Arrhythmogenic right ventricular dysplasia: heart muscle of the right ventricle dies and is replaced by scar tissue 

Acquired 

-Ischemic cardiomyopathy: loss or weakening of heart muscle tissue due to ischemia 

 

Define fitness and describe the cardiovascular changes that occur with increasing fitness 

Fitness is a measure of an individual's health and ability to cope with physical activity. In increased fitness, the cardiovascular changes include: 

-Increased muscular thickness and strength of ventricles, and increased internal ventricular space to increase stroke volume 

-Reduced total peripheral resistance in arteries due to decreased congestion from cholesterol, which reduces blood pressure 

-Reduced resting heart rate due to increased stroke volume 

 

Demonstrate interpreting a CXR with cardiomegaly identification, the cardiothoracic ratio, and the heart chambers  

and major vessels that make up the cardiac silhouette 

Cardiomegaly is the enlargement of the heart. The cardiothoracic ratio is the ratio of maximal horizontal cardiac diameter to maximal horizontal thoracic diameter. A normal measurement is <0.5.  

 

Briefly describe the signs of fluid overload identifiable on a CXR 

-Airspace shadowing: due to alveolar edema 

-Blunt costophrenic angles: due to pleural effusions 

-Septal (Kerley B) lines: thickening of interlobular septa due to interstitial edema 

 

Briefly describe the imaging technique of the echocardiogram, and the cardiac parameters that can be measured 

An echocardiogram uses ultrasound to produce moving pictures of a heart. A transthoracic echo looks for the cause of abnormal heart sounds, an enlarged heart, unexplained chest pain/pressure, irregular heartbeats, or dyspnea. It checks the thickness and movement of the heart wall, examines the heart valves, measures the size and shape of the heart chambers, and determines ejection fraction. It can also be used to detect diseases and blood clots.  

 

A doppler echo is used to examine blood flow through the chambers, heart valves and blood vessels.

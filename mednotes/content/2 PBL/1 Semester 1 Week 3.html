Briefly describe the major events of puberty 

Boys 

  1. 1.No visible signs of development, hormonal changes 

  2. 2.Height increases, muscle and fat tissue increase, darkened aureole, scrotum + testicular growth, pubic hair growth 

  3. 3.Penis lengthens, increase pubic hair, shoulders broaden, voice deepens, facial hair develops 

  4. 4.Penis widens, testes continue growth, voice continues to deepen 

  5. 5.Full adult height 

 

Girls 

  1. 1.No visible signs of development, ovaries enlarge and hormone production begins 

  2. 2.Height and weight increase, curves begin developing, breast buds develop, pubic hair may appear 

  3. 3.Breast growth continues, pubic hair increases, whitish vaginal discharge may present, first menstrual period may begin 

  4. 4.Aureoles get even darker and rise above rest of breast, irregular ovulation may begin 

  5. 5.Adult height reached, regular ovulation 

 

Briefly describe the use of Tanner stages to monitor puberty 

The tanner scale provides 5 stages, with graphical charts, for clinicians to determine the stage of development of an adolescent. The charts look at male and female genitalia, male and female pubic hair, and female breasts.  

 

Define constitutional delay of growth and puberty 

The constitutional delay of growth and puberty involves a slower rate of growth at the onset of puberty. The major pubertal growth spurt occurs later than average, but normal adult height and sexual function will be reached. CDGP is a variant of normal growth, but may lead to psychological difficulties warranting treatment. 

 

Briefly describe the use of x-rays in diagnostics and identify key features on an x-ray, linking to tissue density 

A PA CXR is used for mobile patients that are able to stand. This can estimate cardiac size, and produces higher overall quality. An AP CXR is of much less quality, used for less mobile patients. It produces an exaggerated heart size.  

 

In taking the image, the patients is at the detector, between the detector and X-ray source. PA has the patient hugging the detector, whilst in AP the patient faces the opposite way. In AP, the scapulae produce significant obstruction to the image of the thorax.  

 

5 Shades of gray 

  1. 1.Air 

  2. 2.Fat 

  3. 3.Soft tissue or fluid 

  4. 4.Bone 

  5. 5.Metal 

Body parts are identified by examining the contact between different shades of gray. Fluid within soft tissue cannot be identified, as they are of the same shade of gray/density.  

 

Compare features, factors, and risks of x-rays to ultrasounds 

X-rays are cheap, easy to perform, but produce a small dose of radiation. A still image is produced. Ultrasound is user-dependent, cheap, and produces no radiation. A moving image that may be useful for certain examinations is produced.

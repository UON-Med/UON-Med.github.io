Describe the range of pathological effects associated with hypertension 

-Ventricular hypertrophy and decreased perfusion 

-Fibrosis 

-Outflow obstruction (hypertrophy of interventricular septum, anterior movement of mitral valve) 

-Atherosclerosis and thrombus formation through endothelial damage (stroke, aneurysm etc) 

-Edema due to increased hydrostatic pressure 

-Kidney damage and fibrosis 

-Retinopathy and optic nerve damage (neuropathy) 

 

Define pulse pressure and briefly describe its use in the evaluation of cardiac function 

Pulse pressure is the difference between systolic and diastolic pressures. Low pulse pressure often indicates poor heart function and low stroke volume, such as in congestive heart failure, aortic valve stenosis, pericardial tamponade, etc. High pulse pressure often indicates any valve regurgitation which results in a decreased diastolic pressure in circulation. Anemia and hyperthyroidism also increase pulse pressure. Stiff arteries, especially the aorta, possess less compliance and will also increase pulse pressure by increasing the systolic value.  

 

Briefly describe the heart sounds S3 and S4, in terms of location, timing, and clinical significance 

Both occur at the left ventricle, and are thus best heard at the apex of the heart.  

 

S3 is heard during passive left ventricular filling as blood strikes the over-compliant left ventricle. This is a sign of systolic failure, but can sometimes be a normal finding in children, pregnant women, and athletes. S3 is heard immediately after S2. 

 

S4 heard during active atrial contraction, when blood strikes a stiff, non-compliant left ventricle. This is almost always an abnormal finding, pointing toward diastolic failure and hypertrophy. S4 is heard immediately before S1.  

 

Both S3 and S4 are low-pitched, and are heard by placing the stethoscope bell lightly on the apex.  

 

Define cardiac murmur and briefly describe the factors that contribute to the formation of turbulent flow 

A cardiac murmur is a sound during the heartbeat cycle created by turbulent blood flow. Turbulent blood flow is caused by changes to the blood vessel which causes blood flow to no longer be "laminal". Atherosclerosis, abnormal arterial dilations (aneurysms) and heart valve damage contribute to turbulent flow.

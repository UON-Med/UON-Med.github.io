Demonstrate the taking of a focused history of symptoms of the lower urinary tract, including asking about and understanding the significance of frequency, urgency, dysuria, hematuria, nocturia and incontinence 

Frequency: indicates diabetes (excess production of urine) or interstitial cystitis (chronic pain of bladder) 

Urgency: caused by involuntary contractions of the bladder, caused by diuretic use, neural damage, or UTI 

Dysuria: UTI, obstruction (be aware of prostate problems leading to obstruction) 

Hematuria: UTI, trauma, malignancy, stones in kidney, bladder, ureter 

Nocturia: Decreased bladder storage due to infection, trauma, structural pathology 

Incontinence: UTI, neural damage, excess urine output, stool impaction 

 

Describe the clinical features and contributing factors of the four basic types of incontinence: urge, stress, overflow and functional 

Urge: overactivity of the detrusor muscle, characterised by an intense urge to urinate and leakage of urine in the absence of cough or other stressors 

Stress: leakage after stress of coughing or after rise in intra-abdominal pressure 

Overflow: complete obstruction leads to overflow incontinence. 

Functional: patient becomes aware of needing to pee, but is unable to reach toilet in time due to environmental obstructions 

 

Briefly describe the prevalence of incontinence in different age groups and genders 

Urinary incontinence affects around 13% of Australian men and 37% of Australian women. Fecal incontinence affects around 20% of Australian men and 13% of Australian women. Incontinence increases with age. 

 

Briefly describe the normal development of day and night continence in children and the common reasons for delay 

In children, bowel maturation typically comes before bladder maturation, due to their respective degrees of complexity.  

 

Causes of day wetting include overactive bladder (trouble storing urine), underactive bladder (overfill leakage), leakage due to conscious avoidance of toilet, or incomplete emptying of the bladder. Lack of neural development also plays a role.  

 

At night, children which experience enuresis (bed wetting) may have a delay in growth of bladder size, and thus an increase in bladder volume beyond this size forces the passing of urine.

Define and differentiate the clinical features of cystitis and pyelonephritis 

Cystitis is an infection of the bladder, and is the most common UTI. Cystitis occurs when bacteria travels up the urethra, infects the urine and infects the bladder lining. Cystitis generally isn't dangerous nor contagious, but can lead to a kidney infection if left untreated. Symptoms include dysuria, urinating urge with little urine, hematuria, and lower abdominal pain.  

 

Pyelonephritis is an infection of the kidney, and can be life-threatening. Bacteria usually reaches the kidney by ascending from the lower urinary tract, but can also reach the kidney via the bloodstream. Classical presentation involves fever, costovertebral angle pain, and nausea/vomiting.  

 

Briefly describe the concept of localization of infection, and the risk factors associated with spread of infection (eg cystitis to pyelonephritis to sepsis) 

An infection that is limited only to the local parenchyma of an organ is described as localised. However, it can spread through fluids to other parts of the body eg from bladder to kidney to cause pyelonephritis. If it spreads to blood, the systemic infection is known as sepsis.  

 

Briefly describe the use of bedside urinalysis in diagnosis of urinary tract infection and the significance of nitrites, leukocyte esterase, and blood 

Dipstick leukocyte esterase test: screens for pyuria (white blood cells in urine) 

Nitrites: screens for bacteria converting nitrates to nitrites, common in UTIs 

Blood: looks for blood in urine which can be caused by malignancy, stones 

 

Briefly describe the collection and interpretation of urine microscopy and culture 

Multiple methods are available for urine culture: 

-Clean catch (pee in cup) 

-Urethral catheterisation 

-Suprapubic aspiration (most accurate method) 

All methods attempt to retrieve uncontaminated urine.  

 

Briefly describe the methods and use of antibiotic susceptibility testing 

The agar and broth method: results are commonly reported as the minimal inhibitory concentration (MIC), which is the lowest concentration of a drug that inhibits the growth of an organism.  

 

The Kirby-Bauer method: involves placing small wafers of different antibiotics on a plate on which bacteria are growing. Bacteria are unable to grow around the drugs they are sensitive to.

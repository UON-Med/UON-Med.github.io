List the main functions of the kidney and the clinical manifestations that can result from loss of these functions 

The main functions of the kidney: 

-Excrete waste products of metabolism 

-Regulate concentration of salts 

-Hormonal release of erythropoietin, renin, prostaglandins, vitamin D metabolism 

 

Metabolic and filtration dysfunction 

 

With decreased permeability and surface area of the filtration membrane, uremic retention solutes accumulate in the body due to insufficient filtration. These uremic retention solutes, if capable of exerting adverse biological effects, are termed "uremic toxins". Uremic toxins contribute to inflammation, immune dysfunction, vascular disease, platelet dysfunction, bleeding risk, gut dysbiosis, altered drug metabolism, and CKD progression through uncertain mechanisms. 

 

An inability to excrete waste products is also problematic for certain drugs.  

 

Regulate concentration of salts 

Numerous transport channels throughout the nephron need to function in order to maintain safe salt concentrations in the body.  

 

Hormonal release 

Renal failure can cause anemia through decreased Epo production. Renal ischemia due to atherosclerosis or other vessel damage causes increased renin release, which further worsens hypertension. Prostaglandins can cause increased immune cell infiltration and damage. 

 

Define normal glomerular filtration rate 

Normal glomerular filtration rate in adults is >90ml/min/1.73m2. This is a measure of how much blood passes through the glomerulus each minute.

Define, compare and contrast nephritis and nephrosis 

Nephrotic syndrome: massive proteinuria, hypoalbuminemia, edema, hyperlipidemia.  

 

Nephritic syndrome: hematuria, red cell casts, oliguria (low urine output), azotemia, hypertension, some proteinuria. This is caused by increased leukocytic infiltration. 

 

Briefly describe the common causes and risk factors for CKD 

Risk factors for CKD include low birth weight, hypertension, childhood obesity, diabetes, autoimmune disease, age, family history, previous acute renal injury, and structural abnormalities of the urinary tract.  

 

Common causes of CKD: 

-Diabetic nephropathy 

-Glomerulonephritis 

-Hypertension, leading to vascular kidney disease, ischemic kidney disease 

-Autosomal dominant polycystic kidney disease 

-Other cystic and tubulointerstitial nephropathy 

 

Briefly describe who should be screened for CKD 

People at risk of CKD should be screened: 

-Patients with diabetes, hypertension, cardiovascular disease 

-Other chronic illnesses such as HIV, HepC 

-Malignancy 

-Nephrotoxic medication 

-Obese individuals

Briefly describe the methods used for quantification of protein in urine 

Albumin/creatinine ratio: creatinine is a good indicator for renal function and remains relatively constant. Hence, the ratio is used as a normal ratio indicates that high albumin may be simply due to high GFR.

Compare urea and creatinine and describe the features of creatinine that enable it to be used to estimate GFR 

Creatinine is produced by the breakdown of protein and is removed from blood via the glomerulus. Decreased GFR increases serum creatinine. 

 

Blood urea nitrogen levels also increase with protein breakdown. However, BUN fluctuates with dietary protein and nucleic acid metabolism.  

 

Briefly describe the calculation of eGFR and its limitations 

Estimated GFR is calculated based on serum creatinine levels through the MDRD formula. However, serum creatinine excretion declines in the elderly and is determined by a person's size and body mass. Hence, the MDRD formula is good for GFR > 60 but becomes less accurate for GFR < 60.

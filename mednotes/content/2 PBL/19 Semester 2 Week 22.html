Briefly describe the signs of hypoglycemia and the mechanisms causing them 

Symptoms of hypoglycemia can be classified as neurogenic (adrenergic) or neuroglycopenic. When blood glucose is low, sympathetic activation can cause sweating, tachycardia, anxiety, and shakiness. Neuroglycopenic (insufficient blood to brain) symptoms include weakness, dizziness, inappropriate behaviour similar to inebriation, confusion, blurred vision, and are capable of leading to coma and death.  

 

Briefly describe the clinical relevance of differentiating between subcutaneous and visceral fat 

Subcutaneous fat lies directly under the skin and is not necessarily hazardous to health. Visceral fat however surrounds the abdominal organs. Adipocytes secrete hormones and inflammatory mediators, interfering with bodily functions. Therefore, visceral fat is very harmful and increases risk for heart disease and diabetes. Note: we do have normal visceral fat eg around the heart.

BMI, distribution of weight (waist circumference) and extent of comorbidity are used to assess level of disease risk. Independence is also a necessary indicator of improvement.  

 

Briefly describe the use of "exercise as a vital sign" in evaluation of obesity 

For every day of the week that 30-60 mins of moderate intensity physical activity is undertaken, the likelihood of obesity decreases. However in order to become healthier, a healthy diet with a diverse mix of vegetables and healthy fats is required. 

 

Briefly describe the relevant history and examination to assess a patient for complications of obesity 

Medical and social history needs to be taken to reveal: 

-Diabetes, renal failure, alcoholism, hepatic failure, endocrine disease, surgery 

-CNS changes 

-Alcohol intake, nutritional deficiency 

-Weight reduction, nausea, vomiting 

-Fatigue, somnolence (drowsiness) 

 

This can provide information crucial for identification of complications. Complications include coronary heart disease, hypertension, gallstones, osteoarthritis and infertility.

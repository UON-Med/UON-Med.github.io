Describe the presentation and pathophysiology of common disorders of the thyroid  

Hypothyroidism 

This has a primary cause 90% of the time (iodine insufficiency, autoimmune, surgery, drugs, genetics), but may also have a secondary (~10%) cause from pituitary failure, or a rare tertiary cause from hypothalamus failure.  

 

Autoimmune pathophysiology: can be caused by the generation of anti-thyroid-peroxidase (anti-TPO) antibodies prevents TPO from oxidising iodide (a step in the synthesis of thyroid hormone), or the generation of antibodies targeting the sodium/iodide symporter (NIS) gene.  

 

Hyperthyroidism 

Again, this also has a primary cause 90% of the time, eg Grave's disease (autoimmune), too much iodine, synthetic T4 overdose, cancer, or thyroiditis. A secondary cause may be a pituitary adenoma.  

 

Grave's disease: production of thyroid-stimulating-immunoglobulins leading to hyperthyroidism and ophthalmopathy  

 

Briefly describe the physical signs associated with hyper- and hypothyroidism 

Hypothyroidism causes a decreased metabolic rate, weight gain, bradycardia, fatigue, cold intolerance, constipation.  

 

Hyperthyroidism results in an increased metabolic rate, weight loss, heart palpitations/heart failure due to high output, muscle weakness, heat intolerance, fatigue, diarrhea. 

 

Briefly describe the long-term consequences of thyroid hormone excess and deficiency including in pregnancy 

Excess 

High metabolic rate utilises body's energy stores. First the liver's glycogen stores are used to generate glucose upon stimulation by glucagon, distributing glucose to all organs. When this is depleted, gluconeogenesis occurs through alanine + lactate + glycerol with beta-oxidation of FFAs as an energy source. Certain intermediates are used to generate oxaloacetate, a precursor in gluconeogenesis. If oxaloacetate is depleted, it can no longer react with acetyl-CoA for the citric acid cycle and thus ketone bodies are produced from acetyl-CoA as a fuel source instead.  

 

This all causes weight loss, heart palpitations/heart failure, muscle weakness, heat intolerance, fatigue, diarrhea.  

 

During pregnancy 

Pregnancy will result in an increase in thyroid hormone production overall. Hyperthyroidism in pregnancy can cause the mother to develop toxemia (HBP, proteinuria, edema), thyroid storm, and early labor/stillbirths. The baby can suffer malformations, fetal tachycardia, and hyperthyroidism (note impact on airway). Anti-thyroid drugs for the mother can cross the placenta and cause hypothyroidism in the baby. 

 

Deficiency 

Decreased metabolism, weight gain, bradycardia, fatigue, cold intolerance, constipation all can result.  

 

During pregnancy 

Hypothyroidism can cause in the mother: anemia, myopathy, toxemia, postpartum hemorrhage, low birth weight, congestive heart failure. Thyroid hormone is essential for brain development in babies (reliant on mother for production of thyroid hormone for first 10 weeks). Hypothyroidism can cause impaired brain development in babies. Treatment for mother is as normal, and will not impact baby.  

 

Briefly describe the mechanism of action of carbimazole and propylthiouracil 

Carbimazole is a pro-drug, which is converted to active form methimazole. Methimazole inhibits TPO enzyme function in oxidising iodide. This reduces thyroid function. 

 

Propylthiouracil functions in the same way, but does not reverse hyperthyroidism as fast as methimazole. It has the potential for liver damage, and is only given when methimazole is inappropriate (eg thyroid storm).  

 

Briefly describe the interpretation of blood tests for thyroid hormone, TSH, and relevant antibody results 

 

Primary hypothyroidism: elevated TSH, low free T4 

Subclinical hypothyroidism: elevated TSH, normal free T4 

Secondary hypothyroidism: normal/low TSH, low free T4 

 

Hyperthyroidism results in decreased TSH levels and elevated free T4 levels in blood tests. 

 

Briefly describe thyroid scintigraphy, and its use in distinguishing between common causes of hyperthyroidism 

This is a radioactive scan, where a radioactive material is injected into the bloodstream and concentrated at the thyroid. A gamma camera is used to take images from several different angles. 

 

The thyroid scan can look for a cancer, determine its nature, look at the shape of the thyroid. 

 

A radioactive iodine uptake test can also be done with the thyroid scan, where the uptake of radioactive iodine is measured. Low uptake suggests thyroiditis, whilst high uptake suggests Grave's disease.

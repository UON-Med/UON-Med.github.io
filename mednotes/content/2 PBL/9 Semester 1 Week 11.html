Briefly describe and distinguish the clinical syndromes associated with inflammation/infection at various levels in the airway: rhinitis, sinusitis, pharyngitis, epiglottitis, laryngitis, croup, tracheitis, bronchitis, bronchiolitis and pneumonia 

-Rhinitis: irritation and inflammation of the mucus membrane in the nose, causing a stuffy nose, runny nose, sneezing. 

-Sinusitis: infection or inflammation of the sinuses, causing yellow or green mucus draining from the nose, facial pain, headache 

-Pharyngitis: inflammation of the pharynx, sore throat, other flu symptoms arise. Swollen lymph nodes may present. 

-Epiglottitis: inflammation of the epiglottis, life-threatening 

-Laryngitis: inflamed vocal cords, caused mainly by viruses by also by bacteria, irritants or overuse. A weakened voice, hoarse, dry throat and a dry cough may result. 

-Croup: viral infection of throat and windpipe that causes noisy breathing, and a harsh barking cough. There may also be stridor and difficulty breathing. 

-Tracheitis: bacterial infection of the trachea, leading to airway obstruction, with potential life-threatening complications unless treated quickly 

-Bronchitis: infection of the bronchi leading to airway obstruction with a productive cough 

-Bronchiolitis: bronchiole inflammation, common in babies under 6 months 

-Pneumonia: inflamed alveoli that made be filled with fluid, causing difficulty breathing, productive cough 

 

 

Briefly describe and distinguish patterns of pneumonia (lobar, broncho-, interstitial) 

-Lobar pneumonia involves the exudative inflammation (pneumonia) of an entire lobe.  

-Bronchopneumonia (lobular pneumonia) is caused when a peribronchiolar inflammation spreads through the pores of Kohn (inter-alveolar connections) to create consolidation throughout an entire secondary pulmonary lobule. Bronchopneumonia involves the inflammation of bronchi. 

-Interstitial pneumonia involves diffuse alveolar damage and subsequent fibrosis on the alveoli, in a bilateral, generally symmetric and diffuse fashion. 

 

Briefly describe how pneumonia can result in hypoxia and/or CO2 retention 

The filling of alveoli with fluid reduces the ability for gas exchange at the lungs, hence hypoxia and likely CO2 retention. 

 

Evaluate a blood gas for respiratory sufficiency 

The definition of respiratory failure is at below an arterial oxygen tension (PaO2) of 60mmHg with normal CO2 partial pressure (Type I hypoxemic). Hypercapnic failure is characterised by this, in addition to PaCO2 above 50mmHg.  

 

Briefly describe the full blood count changes in white cells, seen with bacterial infection vs viral infection 

Both infections usually cause an increase in total white blood cells. The white blood cell differential helps determine viral vs bacterial, with elevated neutrophils often pointing to bacteria and elevated lymphocytes suggesting viral.

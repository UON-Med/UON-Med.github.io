Define and briefly distinguish the four types of shock: hypovolemic, distributive, cardiogenic, and obstructive. Briefly describe the mechanisms and common causes for each.  

Shock is inadequate blood circulation.  

-Hypovolemic shock: too little blood volume, due to blood loss 

-Cardiogenic shock: caused by heart problems due to heart attack, leading cause of death in acute myocardial infarct 

-Obstructive shock: caused by a clot or a form of obstruction to blood filling, ejection or flow eg cardiac tamponade or pulmonary embolism 

-Distributive shock: abnormal distribution of blood flow in smaller vessels, resulting in inadequate blood supply to parts of tissues. This is caused by excessive vasodilation, usually due to sepsis or systemic inflammation. 

 

Briefly describe the general principles of resuscitation in hemorrhagic shock, including choices of fluid 

-Blood components as lost 

-Crystalloid fluids to replace extracellular fluid (colloids also used but not preferred) 

 

Distinguish hemoglobin and hematocrit and briefly discuss the expected changes in these during hemorrhage and resuscitation 

Hemoglobin count determines the ratio of hemoglobin molecules to a volume of blood, whilst hematocrit determines the ratio of red blood cells to a volume of blood. Hemoglobin and hematocrit values do not drop immediately after acute blood loss, but will drop eventually. During resuscitation, crystalloid infusion may cause both values to fall temporarily.

Scenario 

You are a rural GP registrar doing an emergency department rotation in remote Moree. It is 1400 hours and the triage nurse asks you to come down to see Clancy  Bourke, a 70yo farmer who has driven himself to the hospital 1.5 hours after he was pinned against a barbed-wire fence by a cow. He complains of right-sided chest pain and his chest, back and arm are covered in blood-soaked dressings.  

 

Problem: 

The immense loss of blood may lead to hemorrhagic shock. This can lead to fatal consequences later on. Upon arriving at the ED, Clancy will first meet the triage nurse.  

 

 

 

 

Major concerns: 

Circulatory system- Cuts to vessels in the thoracic area such as the aorta, inferior/superior vena cava, subclavian, pulmonary trunk, brachiocephalic vessels, blood loss leads to shock, bruising can lead to cardiac arrest 

Respiratory system- Damage of parts related to breathing, such as the bronchi trachea, diaphragm, collapsed lung, perfusion of lung 

Musculoskeletal system- fractured ribs, flailed chest, fractured clavicle 

Gastrointestinal- ruptured spleen, ruptured esophagus leading to peritonitis 

Nervous system- damaged nerves 

Integumentary system- damaged skin, chances of infection 

Excretory system- damaged kidneys  

 

What should you do next? 

In an emergency situation, the order of history-taking may differ. A physical examination may need to take place before the patient's history of presenting complaint.

List the most common causes of acute and chronic liver failure and briefly describe their epidemiology 

Acute 

-Paracetamol overdose 

-Prescription medications and herbal supplements, drug reactions 

-Hepatitis viruses A, B, E 

-Vascular diseases blocking veins of liver leading to acute liver failure 

 

Drug-related causes account for over 50% of cases in developed countries. Hepatitis B and coinfection of Hepatitis D are more common in developing countries. 

 

Chronic 

-Hepatitis C virus 

-Alcohol abuse 

-Nonalcoholic fatty liver disease caused by obesity, hypertension, high cholesterol

Describe the physical examination signs associated with acute and chronic liver diseases, including fulminant acute hepatic failure 

Acute 

Right-upper quadrant tenderness, ascites, small liver span may be indicative of liver necrosis, enlarged liver may be seen with heart failure, viral hepatitis. Headache due to cerebral edema. Hematemesis or melena due to upper GI bleeding.  

 

Fulminant acute: patient is hypotensive and tachycardic due to reduced systemic resistance, indistinguishable from septic shock. 

 

Chronic 

Jaundice, scratch marks, spider nevi, palmar erythema, bruising, hair loss, white nails, clubbing, hepatomegaly, gynecomastia. Portal hypertension presents with ascites, splenomegaly.  

 

Define encephalopathy and briefly describe its manifestations 

Encephalopathy describes a disease that damages the brain. Hepatic encephalopathy is a decline in brain function that occurs as a result of severe liver disease. The liver cannot remove toxins from the blood, which can impact the brain. Encephalopathy presents as confusion and sleepiness. Patients can become unresponsive and slip into a coma in both acute and chronic hepatic encephalopathy.

Briefly describe the natural history of alcohol-related liver disease (ALD) 

The earliest stage of ALD is steatosis (fatty liver), where excessive fat accumulates in liver cells and impairs liver function. Heavy drinking can cause this to progress to alcoholic hepatitis, where liver cells begin to be destroyed and the liver is inflamed. This then leads to cirrhosis with the replacement of normal liver tissue with non-living scar tissue.  

 

Briefly describe the pattern of liver function test abnormalities associated with alcoholic liver disease (vs other liver disease) 

Raised ALT and AST suggest hepatocellular injury. A 10x increase over the limit suggests acute viral hepatitis or hypoxia. Mild elevation suggests alcohol, fatty liver, or infection. Alcohol often results in a higher AST:ALT (AST > 2x ALT) ratio than other forms of liver damage. 70% of raised GGT due to alcoholic excess.

Briefly describe postnatal lung development and the effect of tobacco and particulate exposure 

Postnatal alveolar stage: formation of alveoli, mostly in first 6 months, virtually complete by 2 years 

Postnatal microvascular maturation: formation of new double capillary layers, followed by remodeling to form the mature single layer 

 

Tobacco smoke was shown to increase the risk of behavioral problems in school-age children. Upper respiratory infections and allergies were also shown to have a strong correlation with postnatal tobacco exposure. Asthma, chronic bronchitis and other respiratory symptoms such as wheezing were shown to be caused more significantly by prenatal smoke exposure than by postnatal smoke exposure.

Describe and differentiate the following sounds: stridor, grunting, wheezing, rales (crackles), rhonci, vesicular and bronchial breath sounds 

-Stridor: inspiratory (expiratory in very severe cases), heart without stethoscope, originates from pharynx/larynx, high-pitched. Caused by foreign body, tumor, inflammation.  

-Croup: upper airway inflammation and blockage, with a distinct barking cough. Can present as stridor or hoarseness 

-Grunting: low-pitched sound hear during expiration that sounds like a cry (not as loud). Grunting keeps the alveolar sacs open during small airway/alveolar collapse. 

-Wheezing: Usually on expiration, of musical quality. Can be high pitched which suggests smaller airway narrowing, or low pitched due to secretions in smaller airways 

-Rhonci: low-pitched wheeze, movie-effect- like.  

-Vesicular: soft and low-pitched, rustling quality, inspiration/expiration ratio of 3:1. These are normal breath sounds. 

-Bronchial: hollow, tubular quality, distinct gap between inspiration and expiration; inspiration and expiration times are equal. These are normal over the trachea, and only abnormal in regions far away from large bronchi. These breath sounds are caused by consolidation or lobar collapse with patent (unobstructed) bronchus. 

 

Briefly describe the techniques and devices used to deliver drugs directly to the lungs (nebuliser, MDI, spacer) 

Metered dose inhaler: handheld device delivering a specific amount of medication via aerosol form per press 

Spacer: holding chamber for the aerosol drug, allows drug to be breathed in without coordination of inhalation and delivery. Also prevents backward flow.  

Nebuliser: turns the aerosol into a finer mist to be inhaled through a face mask or mouthpiece. This is used with high doses. 

 

Describe the uses of spirometry in respiratory evaluation 

There are two clinical tests commonly used in diagnosing and assessing respiratory disorders: 

-Forced Vital Capacity (FVC): forced expiratory volume following maximal inhalation 

-Forced expiratory volume in 1st second (FEV1): maximum volume expelled in 1st second of a maximal forced expiration 

Measurements of FVC and FEV1 are compared against tables of predicted values by gender, age, body size. Evidence of airway obstruction presents if FEV1 < 75%.  

 

Obstructive disorder: FEV1 falls but FVC often is normal, thus FEV1/FVC < 0.8 

Restrictive disorder (unable to maximally expand the lungs):  FEV1 and FVC are both reduced relatively equally, FEV1/FVC does not go below 0.8.

Describe and differentiate obstructive and restrictive patterns on spirometry 

Obstructive lung diseases include conditions which make it hard to exhale all the air in the lungs, whilst restrictive lung diseases cause difficulty in inhalation.  

 

Describe how obstructive disease results in hyperinflation of the lungs 

Static hyperinflation: decrease in elasticity of the lung due to emphysema reduces its deflation following inflation (reduced recoil pressure). This results in a higher resting volume.  

Dynamic hyperinflation: volume inhaled is greater than that exhaled 

 

Describe the CXR of a patient with asthma 

Bronchi and bronchioles are filled with fluid, and smooth muscle hypertrophies due to chronic constriction. This makes the bronchi and bronchioles more visible on a CXR.  

 

Evaluate a CXR for hyperinflation 

Lungs cross intercostal spaces beyond normal

Asthma action plan: 

 

Pulsus paradoxus: 

-Abnormally large decrease in systolic blood pressure and pulse wave amplitude during inspiration (If drop is more than 10mmHg, Pulsus paradoxus is considered.) This is a sign of cardiac tamponade, constrictive pericarditis, chronic sleep apnea, croup, and obstructive lung disease.  

 

Spirometry: 

Spirometry is used to clinically assess lung function, typically involving maximum inspiration to the maximum lung volume followed by a forced maximal expiration to the minimum lung volume possible. Spirometers are often used to record changes in lung volume over time (called a spirogram). The four lung values and four lung capacities are used to analyse a person's lung function. (Test of reversibility is done with two spirometries, before and after bronchodilator administration)

List the common causes of falls 

-Chronic conditions such as heart disease, dementia, and hypotension which can cause dizziness 

-Impairments such as poor vision, muscle weakness 

-Illnesses that affect balance such as labyrinthitis 

 

Environmental hazards 

-Wet floors 

-Dim lighting 

-Unsecure rugs/carpets 

-Inaccessible storage areas requiring extensive reaching 

-Stairs 

 

Note the significance of osteoporosis in older individuals, which increases the likelihood of fractures following falls. 

 

Define syncope 

A syncope is a faint, characterised by fast onset, short duration, and spontaneous recovery, caused by a decrease in blood flow to the brain. Heart-related causes such as problems with heart valves and heart disease can cause this, but this is quite rare. A more common cause is neurally-mediated vasodilation and bradycardia, in response to strong emotions. Finally, postural hypotension caused by certain drugs, dehydration or blood loss can cause this. 

 

Briefly describe the mechanism of vasovagal syncope 

Vasovagal syncope is neurologically mediated. A trigger causing extreme emotional distress can lead to a decrease in heart rate and vasodilation. Reduced blood flow to the brain ensues, hence the syncope. This is usually harmless and requires no treatment, unless injury is sustained in a fall.  

 

Compare the features, factors, and risks of CT to X-ray 

A CT produces detailed, high-quality images of the body in 360 degrees. Contrast dyes are injected into the blood. However, a CT produces much more radiation than an X-ray, and is more expensive and not readily available at all hospitals.  

 

Identify key features on a CT (air, bone, fat, soft tissue, metal) 

CT also utilises x-rays, and thus the 5 shades of grey also apply here. CT cross-sectional images have the right side of the body on the left, and the anterior side of the body on top.  

 

What colour is blood on CT? 

Fresh intercranial hemorrhage coagulates quickly and becomes dense, glowing brightly on CT scans. The clot is then broken down by the body, becoming the same density as the brain after one week and appearing dark after two to three weeks.

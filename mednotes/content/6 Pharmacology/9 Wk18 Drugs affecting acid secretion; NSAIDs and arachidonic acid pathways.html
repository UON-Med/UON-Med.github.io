Briefly describe the arachidonic acid pathways that lead to the production of prostaglandins 

Arachidonic acid is a fatty acid formed from membrane phospholipids. There is a rate-limiting step here. Eicosanoids are metabolites of arachidonic acid: prostaglandins, prostacyclin, thromboxane A2, leukotrienes. Arachidonic acid is produced when needed by the cell, and is not stored. Functions include inflammation, smooth muscle tone, hemostasis, thrombosis, parturition, GI secretion.  

 

Arachidonic acid pathways 

A phospholipid activates phospholipase A2, which produces arachidonic acid (and another product which becomes platelet-activating factor). Arachidonic acid is converted by either cyclo-oxygenase (COX) 1 or 2 into cyclic endoperoxides, which become either a prostaglandin, prostacyclin, or thromboxane. Arachidonic acid can also be converted by 5-lipoxygenase into leukotrienes (eg involved in asthma).  

 

Prostaglandin types 

PGF-2-alpha is a bronchoconstrictor; and a uterine wall constrictor. 

PGD-2 is a vasodilator, and inhibits platelet aggregation. 

PGE-2 is a vasodilator, and is a hyperalgesic (increases sensitivity to pain). 

 

COX 

COX 1 is constitutively (constantly) expressed in most cells, and is generally involved in homeostatic processes eg modulating vascular responses and gastric acid secretion etc.  

 

COX 2 is an inducible enzyme. It is upregulated (levels increases) by cytokines, stress, and growth factors. It is a principle enzyme involved in prostaglandin and thromboxane formation in inflammation and cancer.  

 

Identify the site of and mechanism of action of NSAIDs 

NSAIDS include non-selective COX inhibitors eg aspirin, ibuprofen, and selective COX inhibitors eg celecoxib 

 

Describe the effects of NSAIDS on gastric mucosa 

PGE-2 is a prostaglandin which binds to the EP3 receptor (GPCR), to inhibit gastric acid secretion, increase gastric mucus secretion, enhance epithelial cell HCO3-  secretion, cell turnover, and local blood flow all to protect the gastric mucosa. NSAIDs inhibit the production of PGE-2 and can cause gastric discomfort, dyspepsia (indigestion), constipation, nausea, vomiting, and in some cases gastric bleeding and ulceration.  

 

Systemically, NSAIDs also increase the expression of intercellular adhesion molecules in gastric vascular endothelium, increasing neutrophil adherence to vascular endothelial cells and thus causing mucosal damage due to neutrophil-derived free radicals and proteases.  

 

Topically, an NSAID eg aspirin is a weak acid which may dissociate when in the gastric epithelial cell. In the cell, the ionised form of aspirin (lost H+) is trapped and causes cell damage.  

 

Describe the mechanisms of drugs used to decrease acid secretion and/or prevent acid damage to mucosal surfaces 

-Histamine H2 receptor antagonist: eg ranitidine  

These suppress gastric acid secretion by competitively and reversibly blocking histamine binding to H2 receptors. H2 receptor antagonists also decrease gastrin- and ACH- induced gastric acid secretion.  

 

-Proton pump inhibitors: eg omeprazole 

These suppress gastric secretion by inhibiting the H+/K+ ATPase. All proton pump inhibitors are pro-drugs which require activation in an acidic environment. Generally taken in morning 30min before food.  

 

-Antacids: Al(OH)3 and Mg(OH)2 neutralise acid 

 

-Coating agents: 

Sucralfate (Na2SO4 and Al(OH)3) forms viscous gel in acidic environment to bind to positively charged proteins and adhere to epithelial cells.  

 

Colloidal bismuth is another drug, which works through the mechanism where bismuth salts bind to mucus glycoproteins to form a barrier protecting the gastric mucosa.  This may stimulate bicarbonate and PGE2 secretion, and slows the growth of H pylori. 

 

Both protect the stomach luminal surface from acid and pepsin.  

 

-Prostaglandin PGE-2 analogue: 

These are drugs eg misoprostol which are analogous in function to PGE-2. They reduce gastric acid secretion, enhance HCO3- secretion, mucus production, and blood flow. These are used to prevent NSAID- induced peptic ulcers. 

 

Describe the effect of food, acidity, and gastric motility on drug availability/absorption 

Acidity 

The pH of an environment impacts whether the drug is in the ionised or un-ionised form. Un-ionised molecules are usually more lipid soluble, diffusing readily across the cell membrane. Ionised molecules usually have low lipid solubility, and thus have difficulty diffusing across the cell membrane. Weakly acidic dugs are lipid soluble in low pH eg stomach, and weakly basic drugs are lipid soluble in high pH eg duodenum. Antacids, proton-pump inhibitors and H2 receptor antagonists all alter the stomach's pH.  

 

Motility 

Increased gastric emptying causes faster drug absorption in the small intestines, and decreased gastric emptying slows drug absorption. The use of agents impacting GI motility must thus be taken into consideration (eg dopamine receptor antagonist increasing motility and muscarinic receptor antagonist decreasing motility). 

 

Food 

The composition of food in the stomach influences the rate of gastric emptying. High fat foods delay gastric emptying and delay drug absorption in the small intestine. Some drugs bind to protein in food, causing the drug to be excreted. An empty stomach is less acidic, which is good for acid-labile drugs. Food in stomach is more acidic, which is good for drugs requiring acid activation.  

 

Tetracycline antibiotics chelate Ca2+, Mg2+ and Fe2+ ions in foods and other drugs (eg antacids), and decrease drug absorption.  

 

Discuss the potential for drug interactions in patients on drugs that alter stomach acidity (eg chelating issues with antibiotics, interactions with clopidogrel) 

Clopidogrel inhibits platelet activation to decrease blood clotting. It is administered as a pro-drug which is metabolised by the liver by a certain enzyme into its active form. However, proton pump inhibitors inhibit the enzyme, therefore reducing plasma levels of active clopidogrel thus decreasing its effectiveness.

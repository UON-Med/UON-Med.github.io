What is heart failure? 

Forward failure is the inability of the heart to pump blood forward at a rate sufficient to meet the metabolic demands of the body. Backward failure is the ability to do so only if cardiac filling pressures are abnormally high.  

 

Congestive heart failure involves reduced cardiac output. There is a reduced stroke volume, and hence which each cycle the end systole and end diastolic volumes increase. Hence, the preload burden increases over time. The ventricular chamber has limited size, and thus blood starts to back up in either the lungs or in systemic circulation.  

 

The causes of heart failure are: 

-Excess preload (hypertrophy) 

-Excess afterload 

-Inadequate inotropy 

 

Hence, drugs used can be classified as preload-reducing, afterload-reducing, and positive inotropic agents. Preload-reducing drugs have the ultimate goal of a slight reduction in preload, to optimise (increase) stroke volume and cardiac output. Excess dosage of the drug easily leads to dramatic drops in cardiac output after a certain point.  

 

Diuretic (preload) 

Throughout the kidneys, pumps exist to move Na+ and Cl- ions into the interstitial fluid, along with some K+. This prevents their passage in urine. These naturally result in the retention of water in the body, which may contribute to excess plasma leakage and edema. Thus, diuretics are used to inhibit these pumps, ensuring that these salts continue to pass through the body and water retention is reduced.  

 

Thiazide diuretics inhibit the Na+/Cl- symporter in the distal convoluted tubule. This promotes urination to decrease blood volume, and provides a small venodilatory effect. There is a small reduction in preload, to help reduce edema. These are not as effective as loop diuretics at promoting excretion of water. 

 

Loop diuretics eg furosemide inhibit the Na+/K+/2Cl- symporter in the thick ascending limb of the loop of Henle to prevent reabsorption. Loop diuretics have high efficacy, and are thus referred to as high-ceiling diuretics. Unfortunately, the symporter is also found in the inner ear and thus loop diuretics can cause hearing loss.  

 

Nitrates (preload) 

Isosorbide dinitrate is an organic nitrate with a similar mechanism of action to GTN. They are efficient dilators of veins, but have a much smaller effect on arteries. These target preload, with little to no effect on afterload. These are often combined with hydralazine (arteriole smooth muscle relaxant) or ACE inhibitors to produce arterial dilation (in controlling afterload). Like GTN, daily drug-free periods are needed to prevent the development of tolerance to the drug. 

 

ACE inhibitors (-pril, eg captopril) (preload and afterload) 

ACE inhibitors are the first line therapy for left-sided systolic failure, when used in conjunction with diuretics. They also have the added benefit of reducing the rate of disease progression by reducing aldosterone, and thus reducing cardiomyocyte hypertrophy. 

 

These block the angiotensin converting enzyme at the lungs, preventing the conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II. This reduces vasoconstriction, aldosterone release for Na+ reabsorption, and vasopressin. ACE inhibitors also reduce the angiotensin I receptor-mediated activation of phospholipase C, to reduce IP3 messenger levels. This reduces the IP3-mediated liberation of Ca2+ from the sarcoplasmic reticulum in smooth muscles, leading to the vasodilation of both veins and arteries. This reduces both preload and afterload.  

 

Note: It has been shown that aldosterone promotes cardiomyocyte hypertrophy. Hence, the reduced aldosterone availability is further beneficial. 

 

Furthermore, bradykinin is a peptide which allows vasodilation through NO and prostaglandins. Angiotensin converting enzyme destroys bradykinin. Hence, ACE inhibitors play a useful role in reduce this. 

 

Other afterload-reducing drugs include angiotensin II receptor antagonists, Ca2+ channel blockers, and direct vasodilators eg organic nitrates. These primarily target arterial vasoconstriction.  

 

Digoxin (inotropic) 

Digoxin blocks the Na+/K+ ATPase pump. This delays the efflux of Na+, blocking the passive Na+/Ca2+ exchanger. This causes an elevated level of intracellular Ca2+, forcing transport of Ca2+ into sarcoplasmic reticulum. Thus, more Ca2+ is available for each subsequent contraction. This is an inotropic drug, improving contraction efficiency, improving stroke volume, and therefore cardiac output. 

 

Dobutamine (inotropic) 

Dobutamine is a beta-adrenergic agonist, used as a short-term agent. It as an identical mechanism of action to adrenaline at the heart (leading to vasodilation, heart rate, etc), but its maximum effect is much less than that of adrenaline. Hence, it does not create strain on the already compromised heart.  

 

Define therapeutic index 

The therapeutic index compares the dose-response curve of a beneficial effect vs that of toxic effects. This assumes a single toxic dose-response curve, and dose-response curves will vary significantly between individuals.  

The therapeutic index can have values calculated from: 

-Max non-toxic dose on min effective dose 

-LD50/ED50 

-TD50/ED50

Routes of drug administration 

Parenteral administration = avoiding GIT, but often used to describe injection. 

 

-IV injection: 100% bioavailability, rapid onset, precise control of dose, suitable for large volumes of fluids and for irritant drugs. However there is high cost, inconvenience, danger of infection and embolism. 

-Oral: convenience, economic, safe, however there is a variability of absorption 

-Subcutaneous: slow rate of absorption, but can provide slow onset and sustained effect eg insulin 

-Intramuscular: faster absorption due to increased blood flow in skeletal muscle, suitable for depot preparations (drugs slowly released from gels or insoluble crystals). Inconvenient and uncomfortable.  

 

Describe the factors that affect drug absorption after oral administration 

Lipid solubility 

Lipid-soluble drugs can cross cell membranes easily by diffusion. Many such drugs are absorbed by simple diffusion, whilst larger drugs require a carrier protein. Active transport and facilitated diffusion may be used for non-lipid soluble drugs.  

 

Nature of absorbing surface 

Most orally-administered drugs are absorbed through the small intestine, which has extremely large surface area. This is fast. Drugs absorbed through the stomach and large intestine will not be absorbed as quickly.  

 

Environment pH and acidity of drug 

When a drug is unionised, it is more lipid-soluble and diffuses across the luminal barrier more readily. For example, a weakly acidic drug in the stomach will remain unionised. It will diffuse across the gastric mucosal barrier into blood plasma. In plasma with pH 7, the weak acid will ionise and is hence trapped in the plasma (unable to diffuse back across barrier). Weakly basic drugs can also be absorbed in the stomach, as not all molecules of the drug will be ionised (due to weak nature). 

 

Amount of blood flow to GIT capillaries 

This can be moderated by autonomic nervous system action. Higher blood flow keeps the drug concentration low in the vessel, providing a steeper concentration gradient.  

 

GIT motility 

Increased gastric emptying = faster drug absorption in small intestines. There are agents which can increase or decrease GIT motility, which may be taken alongside a drug for the desired rate of absorption. Note that fatty food decreases GIT motility. 

 

Food 

Drugs binding to substances eg digoxin binding to fiber increases drug excretion. An empty stomach is less acidic, which is good for drugs easily broken down by acid. Food in stomach = more acidic, good for drugs requiring acid activation. Tetracyclines chelate Ca2+, Mg2+, Fe2+, antacids and cause drug excretion.  

 

Describe how the preparation of the drug affects absorption eg enteric coatings, sustained release, lipophilic vs hydrophilic 

 

-The rate of dissolution of solid drugs impacts absorption 

-Enteric coating is pharmacologically inactive but has a threshold of dissolution around pH 5-6, prevents breakdown in the stomach to prevent stomach irritation, and allows drug to present at sites in the ileum and colon for absorption in order to treat certain diseases eg Crohn's 

-Sustained release drugs are formulated for slow, uniform absorption of the drugs for 8 hours or longer. This maintains the therapeutic effect overnight, reduces intensity of peaks and troughs in drug levels in blood to reduce adverse effects, increased compliance by patient. Capsules can be designed to remain intact for hours after ingestion to delay absorption. A mixture of slow and fast release particles can produce a rapid, sustained period of absorption.  

-Lipophilic drugs are easily absorbed by diffusion, which occurs with lipid soluble drugs, down a concentration gradient 

-A carrier protein can provide facilitated diffusion 

-Water soluble drugs can be transported by passive facilitated diffusion through an aqueous channel (down concentration gradient) or by active transport by ATP-binding cassette transporter 

 

Describe the interactions of the small intestine epithelium with drugs (absorption, metabolism, exsorption)  

-Drugs are absorbed through simple diffusion, facilitated diffusion and active transport. 

-Metabolic enzymes can inactivate a drug before absorption in the small intestine. This can be caused by CYP450 enzymes, gut microbiota. This reduces the amount of drug absorbed. 

-Efflux transporters eg P glycoprotein (active transporter) pump drugs back into the lumen of the intestines to reduce amount of drug reaching the systemic circulation.

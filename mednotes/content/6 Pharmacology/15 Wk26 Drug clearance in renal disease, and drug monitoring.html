Describe the mechanisms of kidney metabolism and clearance of drugs (exogenous substances); compare and contrast renal excretion and hepatic excretion of exogenous substances 

Compared to the liver, the kidney contains low levels of CYP450 enzymes. Available evidence shows that only CYP3A5 and CYP2B6 are expressed, but these may be enough for significant metabolic capacity. There are also three renal UDP-glucuronosyltransferases (UGTs) which catalyse glucuronidation (conjugation). 

 

After drugs are metabolised through the liver, more hydrophilic substances are eliminated quickly. Routes of excretion include renal, biliary, lungs, dermal, fecal and breast milk.  

 

Biliary excretion 

If the drug is excreted by the liver into the bile, the drug can undergo two paths. Steroids, digoxin, and some chemotherapeutic agents will be secreted in bile and then passed through feces. The other pathway is undertaken by morphine, whereby it undergoes enterohepatic circulation. Morphine emptied via bile into the duodenum can be reabsorbed in the small bowel and returned through the portal vein, liver, and into systemic circulation.  

 

Renal excretion 

There are four distinct processes in the renal excretion of drugs: glomerular filtration, active tubular secretion, passive tubular reabsorption and active tubular reabsorption.  

 

1, Glomerular filtration: only unbound drug passes into the filtrate, as drugs bound to proteins are unable to pass through the filter. 

2, Active tubular secretion: Active transport (carrier-mediated) adds drug from blood into the tubular fluid. Transporters include the organic cation transporter (OCT, for morphine) and the organic anion transporter (OAT, for penicillin). Note that drugs can also enter the proximal tubule by passive diffusion (uncharged drug) or facilitated diffusion (charged or uncharged).  

3, Passive tubular reabsorption: Ionised forms of weakly acidic or basic drugs are pH-trapped and excreted in urine. However, non-ionised molecules will undergo net passive reabsorption into the blood passively. The pH of urine can be manipulated by the body in drug overdose. For example, overdose of aspirin (weak acid) will cause increased secretion of NaHCO3 into the urine. Thus when aspirin is secreted or filtered into the tubules, it will dissociate and lose its lipophilicity. This traps aspirin in the tubules. There will also be increased urine H2O flow rate to dilute the salts, hence faster drug elimination.  

4, Active tubular reabsorption: some drugs eg theophylline are actively reabsorbed into the blood (theophylline due to structural similarity to adenosine) 

 

Note: the proximal tubule has a brush border and generally more reabsorption occurs in the proximal tubule. 

 

Describe the effect of CKD on the kidney ability to clear drugs 

In chronic kidney disease, changes to protein structure/binding, blood flow, and functional state of nephrons impact renal clearance negatively. Interstitial fibrosis, tubular atrophy and nonfunctional glomeruli decrease GFR, increase arteriole permeability and decrease the clearance of drugs dependent on filtration or renal transporters for elimination. 

 

Describe principles of dosage adjustment and monitoring using the example of aminoglycosides 

There are a small group of drugs which are not inactivated by metabolism. Hence, the rate of renal elimination is the main factor determining their duration of action. These drugs must be used carefully, as patients with impaired renal function are in danger. Aminoglycosides are excreted almost entirely by the kidneys, and serve as such an example. Their normal half-life is 2-3 hours, but with impaired renal function can go up to 24-48 hours.  

 

Patients require renal function monitoring and hearing tests (ototoxicity) for long term treatment, with the duration of dosing kept as short as possible and a loading dose used for short course therapy. The optimal therapeutic dosing is known as "steady state levels", with the highest dosing below toxicity and lowest dosing above minimally effective level. Dosage adjustment is used for individual patients.

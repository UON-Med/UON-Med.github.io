Pharmacology of blood pressure 

Tuesday, June 6, 2017 

11:08 PM 

Explain the relationship between the targets of the following drug classes and their potential to alter blood pressure: 

-Beta blockers 

-Calcium channel blockers 

-ACE inhibitors 

-Diuretics 

 

Blood pressure depends on the volume of blood and the diameter of the blood vessel. Thus, blood pressure can be altered by either reducing volume or by vasodilation. Blood pressure is detected by baroreceptors (stretch receptors). Chemoreceptors respond to the partial pressure of O2 and CO2.  

 

Control of vascular tone 

Alpha-1 adrenergic receptors use phospholipase C to promote vasoconstriction, whilst beta-2 adrenergic receptors cause vasodilation through adenylate cyclase. 

 

Beta blockers (-olol group) 

Beta-blockers can cause vasoconstriction, by inhibiting vasodilation. However by blocking beta-1 adrenergic receptors in the heart, there is reduced Ca2+ channel activation, and prolonged action potential duration in the heart. This reduces heart rate. Contraction force is also reduced, and thus cardiac output is decreased. This reduces blood pressure. (Cardiac output is optimised as opposed to excessively reduced.)  

 

Furthermore, beta-receptors in the vasomotor centre of the brain stem are blocked, reducing sympathetic outflow. (Metoprolol crosses the blood-brain barrier well, and inhibits vesicle mobilisation in the release of NA.) Reduced noradrenaline release reduces alpha-1 adrenergic receptors on vascular smooth muscle, resulting in less PLC and thus less liberation of Ca2+ from the sarcoplasmic reticulum. This reduces cardiac output again by reducing contractility. Also, reduced vasoconstriction through decreased alpha-activation reduces blood pressure.  

 

Hence, better members of the beta-blocker group will also block alpha-1 adrenergic receptors.  

 

Calcium channel blockers 

These block voltage-gated Ca2+ channels in arteries. A decrease in Ca2+ influx into vascular smooth muscle reduces the liberation of Ca2+ from the sarcoplasmic reticulum, reducing smooth muscle contraction and thus reducing blood pressure. 

 

ACE inhibitors 

These not only inhibit the RAAS system, but also reduce angiotensin-1 receptor-mediated activation of PLC. This reduces IP3- mediated release of Ca2+ from sarcoplasmic reticulum stores. This allows the vasodilation of both arteries and veins. These also reduce aldosterone release from the adrenal cortex, decreasing water retention and thus blood volume. It also allows bradykinin to produce NO, a vasodilator.  

 

Diuretics 

These promote the excretion of fluids through the renal system, thereby reducing blood volume and reducing blood pressure.  

 

Important things to note 

Both adrenaline and noradrenaline will act on all alpha and beta receptors, except: noradrenaline does not act on the beta-2 receptor.  

 

Both alpha receptors result in the contraction of smooth muscle and hence vasoconstriction. Beta-1 receptors cause an increase in contractile force and heart rate. Beta-2 receptors cause smooth muscle relaxation and thus vaso- and broncho-dilation.  

 

There is a large number of alpha-1 receptors relative the beta-2 receptors on vascular smooth muscle. Hence, adrenaline will result in an overall increase in vasoconstriction.

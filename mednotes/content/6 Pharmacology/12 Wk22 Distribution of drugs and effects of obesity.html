Describe the factors affecting drug concentration (once drugs get in body and past liver) 

Once past the liver, the concentration of the drug remaining depends on: 

-Cardiac output, as increased output accelerates second pass through liver etc 

-Local concentrations are impacted by regional blood flow 

-Capillary permeability 

-Tissue volume 

-Tissue affinity for the drug 

 

Note: if two drugs bind with high affinity to the same plasma protein, there will be competition for this binding and hence the concentrations of the free forms of both drugs will increase. This can cause an increased risk eg increased warfarin causing bleeding risk. However, this change is transient as rates of clearance by the liver will increase in order to bring the concentrations to safe levels (at equilibrium).  

 

Describe the determinants of drugs crossing barriers to different body compartments, such as the blood brain barrier 

The well perfused organs will receive the blood first, such as the liver and kidneys. The brain is well-perfused, but the BBB raises a few further implications. Followed by this are the heart, adipose, other muscle and organs etc. Larger tissue volume allows for larger drug storage within it.  

 

-The capillary endothelial membrane is highly permeable, and diffusion occurs rapidly for small, lipophilic drugs.  

-The pH gradient between tissue and blood (7 vs 7.4) is small and thus pH trapping is minimal 

-Blood-tissue partitioning (binding of drug to plasma protein such as albumin, alpha-1-acid glycoprotein, and tissue macromolecules) limits concentrations of free drug 

 

Blood Brain Barrier 

At the BBB, tight junctions between endothelial cells prevent diffusion through these spaces. Small, lipophilic drugs can move across the membrane by passive diffusion but all other drugs must either be actively transported or become excluded. This is used in drug design, eg non-sedating anti-histamine will be designed to have poor diffusion into the brain. 

 

The BBB also involves efflux transporters which pump xenobiotics, including drugs, out of the brain. As such, therapeutic drug levels may often not be reached in the brain eg loperamide that is systemically active has no impact on brain due to P-glycoprotein efflux pump.  

 

Placenta 

Drugs taken are often not excluded by the placenta. The fetus will be exposed to some extent, but efflux pumps such as P-glycoprotein will limit fetal exposure to potentially toxic agents. Lipid solubility, plasma binding and ionisation of acids/bases will impact drug transfer across the placenta. 

 

Four-compartment model 

Drugs enter and leave compartments in the order: blood, vascular rich group, muscle, adipose 

 

Volume of distribution (Vd) 

This is calculated by (volume of drug taken in mol)/(plasma drug concentration mol/L) which gives a volume in liters. This represents the extent of drug distribution to tissues and not to plasma. Vd is low for drugs retained mainly in the bloodstream, eg warfarin at 8L. Vd is high for drugs highly distributed to nonvascular compartments eg amiodarone at 4620L, chloroquine 12000L, and digoxin 645L.  

 

Describe the effect of increased body fat on drug concentrations, clearance and activity 

-Some drugs have extensive distribution into adipose, whilst others don't. However in the drugs that do eg lipophilic drugs, Vd will be markedly increased in obese patients and hence drug dose adjustment is needed for appropriate therapeutic levels.  

 

-Tissue perfusion may be reduced in different parts of the body in obesity which impacts local drug concentrations and distribution 

 

-Cardiac structure and function may change impacting cardiac systemic output 

 

-Fatty buildup in liver can alter hepatic blood flow and alter drug clearance 

 

Describe the effect of blood flow on drug distribution and clearance (eg in poorly perfused tissues) 

-Poorly perfused tissues will have less of a drug distributed to the area 

-Increased cardiac output increases rate of hepatic clearance

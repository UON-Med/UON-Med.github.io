Sites of drug metabolism 

-The majority of drug metabolism occurs in the liver 

-Significant drug metabolism occurs in the small intestine 

-Interestingly, metabolism can also occur in the lungs, nasal passages and kidneys 

 

Describe first pass metabolism and its significance for the bioavailability of a drug 

First pass metabolism describes the process by which the concentration of a drug is greatly reduced by the liver before it reaches systemic circulation. This decreases the bioavailability of the drug. Furthermore, a small amount of metabolism in the small intestine also reduces the systemic circulation of the drug.  

 

Bioavailability = (Amount of drug reaching systemic circulation)/(dose administered) 

 

IV injection bioavailability = 100% 

 

 

 

Distinguish phase 1 and phase 2 metabolism 

Metabolism involves 2 phases. Phase 1 involves oxidation/reduction/hydrolysis of a drug (usually increases water solubility slightly), and Phase 2 involves conjugation of the drug or conjugation of the phase 1 product with another molecule.  

 

Phase 1 

Phase 1 involves the addition of -OH, -COOH, -SH, -O-, and NH2 functional groups. This process can dramatically alter biological properties of a drug, usually inactivating it. However, it can also convert a pro-drug to an active drug. Phase 1 prepares a drug for conjugation to a functional group.  

 

Enzymes include: CYP450 superfamily (includes heme protein and involved in 75% of all drug metabolism), flavin-containing mono-oxygenases, and epoxide hydrolases. CYP450 enzymes are located in the endoplasmic reticulum of hepatocytes.  

 

When a drug is transported to the ER of the hepatocyte, in binds to oxidised CYP enzyme (containing Fe3+) to form a complex. NADPH donates electrons to create reactive oxygen atoms, which then bind with a drug to oxidise it.  

 

drug + O2 + NADPH + H+ à drug-OH + H2O + NADP+ 

 

Phase 2 

Phase 2 involves the attachment of a substituent group, usually producing a conjugated drug that is inactive and water-soluble. An enzyme eg glutathione-S-transferase catalyses the binding of the conjugating side group (glutathione) onto the drug. Other enzymes include: 

-N-acetyltransferase 

-Sulfotransferase 

-Thiopurine methyltransferase 

-UDP-glucuronosyltransferase 

 

The rate of conjugation is generally faster than phase I oxidation. Phase 2 mainly occurs in the cytosol of the hepatocytes as this is the location of the enzymes. Exception: glucuronidation occurs in the ER. 

 

Describe the potential for drug interactions due to changes in liver metabolism 

-If drug A inactivates drug B's enzymes by binding to them and inducing conformational changes, drug B's enzymes no longer work with drug B. This will increase plasma levels of drug B by impeding metabolism, and hence toxicity can occur. 

-If drug A inhibits the enzyme involved in the conversion of prodrug B to drug B, there will be decreased plasma levels of active drug B.  

-If drug A and drug B are metabolised by the same enzyme, drug plasma levels will be determined by the affinity with which each drug binds to the enzyme (or which drug has higher concentration). The plasma levels of both drugs will generally rise.  

 

Note: many drugs are inhibitors of their own enzymes, eg clarithromycin is a substrate of, and yet inhibits CYP3A4. As such clarithromycin will remain in the blood for extended periods of time.  

 

-Xenobiotics and drugs can also activate transcription and induce the expression of genes encoding the drug-metabolising enzyme. As such, they can induce their own metabolism. Many ligands and receptors do the same to regulate their levels. 

-Drug A can induce the synthesis of enzymes which metabolise drug B, eg rifampicin activates Pregnane-X Receptor (PXR) which induces transcription of CYP3A4. CYP3A4 metabolises oral contraceptive pill and can lead to its failure. 

 

Amongst individuals, polymorphisms (mutations) in genes encoding one or more enzymes can result in different levels of enzyme activity. As such, pharmacogenetic differences must be accounted for in dosing a drug.  

 

Explain in detail the processing of alcohol and paracetamol by the liver, including the potential for interaction between them 

 

Ethanol 

Oral ethanol is rapidly absorbed through both the stomach and small intestine. Ethanol is metabolised to acetaldehyde by alcohol dehydrogenase, then to acetic acid by aldehyde dehydrogenase. CYP2E1 is induced by alcohol, and can contribute to the ethanol => acetaldehyde conversion process.  

 

Acetaldehyde is a reactive and toxic compound, contributing to hepatotoxicity. Acetaldehyde causes flushing, tachycardia, hyperventilation, panic and distress. Acetaldehyde is then converted to acetic acid by aldehyde dehydrogenase. Acetic acid is converted to Acetyl-CoA.  

 

Ethanol metabolism causes ratio of NAD+:NADH to fall, increasing lactate and slowing down Krebs cycle. Increased NADH and acetyl-CoA supports fatty acid synthesis and triglyceride accumulation. An increase in ketone bodies exacerbates lactic acidosis.  

 

Paracetamol 

Paracetamol is an analgesic and anti-pyretic, with almost no anti-inflammatory effect. It is predominantly metabolised in phase 2 only, with the production of non-toxic glucuronide and sulfate conjugates which can be excreted through urine. However if conjugation is saturated, CYP450 enzymes convert paracetamol into the highly reactive metabolite NAPQI which can cause cell death and hepatic necrosis. Nucleophilic glutathione can however bind NAPQI for excretion through urine, but this is limited by glutathione reserves in a paracetamol overdose.  

 

Interaction 

Both can contribute to hepatotoxicity if taken in large amounts. Thus the damage on the liver is magnified if both are taken in excess together. 

 

Furthermore, alcohol metabolism can induce CYP2E1 which can cause glutathione depletion. As above, glutathione depletion places an individual at risk of hepatotoxicity if there are large amounts of NAPQI being produced.  

 

Describe the toxic metabolites produced in paracetamol overdose and the mechanism by which NAC protects the hepatocyte from toxicity 

Paracetamol overdose causes nausea and vomiting, and leads to hepatotoxicity 24-48 hours after overdose through the damaging effects of NAPQI. N-acetylcysteine (NAC) is an antidote, as it is hydrolysed to cysteine to assist glutathione synthesis. This is important as the presence of cysteine is the rate-limiting factor in glutathione synthesis. With increased glutathione availability after dosage of NAC, more NAPQI can be conjugated by glutathione (into mercapturic acid) and excreted in urine.  

 

Describe how metabolism affects the analgesic effects of opioids 

Opioid example: codeine prodrug converted to morphine 

Morphine is 200x more potent than codeine in its analgesic effects. However, multiple enzymes can come into play: 

-CYP2D6 poor metabolisers will result in no analgesic effect. 

-CYP2D6 rapid metabolisers will cause 10% conversion of codeine to morphine, causing normal analgesia 

-CYP2D6 ultra-rapid metabolisers will cause high conversion to morphine, with good analgesia but increased risk of certain adverse effects eg respiratory depression 

 

Morphine is then conjugated with glucuronic acid. Morphine-6-glucuronide has 2x higher potency, and morphine-3-glucuronide causes neuro-excitation.

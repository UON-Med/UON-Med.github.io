Explain the relationships between the targets of the following drug classes and their potential to relieve ischemia: 

-Nitrates 

-Beta Blockers 

-Calcium channel blockers (dihydropyridine and non-dihydropyridine) 

 

Ischemic heart disease involves an imbalance between supply of blood to the heart and the heart's blood demand. Therefore, the pharmacological goals are to increase supply of blood without increasing heart work, or reduce excessive heart work (reduce demand by heart). 

 

Ischemia involves the lack of O2 and nutrients to the heart, and prevents the removal of waste.  

 

Nitric oxide as a vasodilator 

Nitric oxide is an endogenous gas (gas flowing inside the body) made by the conversion of arginine to citrulline (citrullination). This is the most potent local vasodilator (local due to short half-life and reactive nature) in blood vessels. A problem arises however, when the presence of atherosclerotic plaque between endothelial cells (innermost layer of the tube around a vessel) and smooth muscle cells narrows the vessel and therefore prevents NO from accessing smooth muscle and causing vasodilation.  

 

Other local vasodilators include: adenosine (increases cGMP), prostaglandins (inhibit the aggregation of blood platelets therefore preventing needless clot formation) 

 

Nitrates provide this exogenous source of nitric oxide, and exogenous NO bypasses the plaque. NO activates the guanylate cyclase protein, which is an enzyme converting GTP to cGMP. The cGMP increases the activity of protein kinase G (PKG) to inhibit the calcium channels at the sarcoplasmic reticulum, and activate the myosin-light-chain-phosphatase (MLCP) to dephosphorylate myosin heads, reducing their action in muscle contraction.  

 

Thus, the mechanism of action is as follows: 

-Potent, immediate vasodilation 

-Small decrease in venous return reduces preload, which reduces workload, thereby reducing demand 

-Causes small decrease in afterload which reduces workload and thus demand, via relaxation of elastic and large muscular arteries 

-Optimises efficiency of ejection as more time is available for ejection, ensuring adequate blood flow (improves supply) without increased workload 

 

Nature of adverse reactions to drugs 

-Type A: dose-related (80% of adverse reactions), eg NO-mediated vasodilation causing headaches, dizziness as dose increases 

-Type B: dose-independent, allergic reactions to drug or interaction between drug and plasma proteins that creates unique antigenic determinants 

 

Beta Blockers 

-In SA node: block beta adrenergic receptors and decreases production of cAMP and hence reduces PKA activation 

-Decreased influx of Ca2+, delaying depolarisation to prolong action potential duration, decreasing heart rate (decreased cardiac work and blood demand), and increased filling time due to use of elastic recoil to optimise ejection fraction (increase supply) without increased workload 

 

-In cardiomyocytes: decreased production of cAMP, decreased Ca2+ channel opening, reduced liberation of Ca2+ from sarcoplasmic reticulum, reduced contraction force (less blood demand due to less work), reliance on elastic recoil optimises ejection without increased workload (increased supply) 

 

Calcium channel blocker 

Non-dihydropyridines are cardio-selective, whereas dihydropyridines are vascular-selective. Cardio-selective calcium channel blockers should not be used with beta blockers in patients with a history of dysrhythmias.  

 

Cardio-selective 

In SA node cells: blocks voltage-gated Ca2+ channels, decreasing Ca2+ influx into SA node. This delays the rapid depolarisation to prolong action potential duration, decreasing heart rate (decreased cardiac work), increasing filling time (without increased workload). 

 

In cardiomyocytes: block voltage-gated Ca2+ channels, reducing influx into myocytes, reduced release of Ca2+ from sarcoplasmic reticulum, reduced contraction force (less cardiac work and demand), increased filling time (increases supply) with elastic recoil to optimise ejection without increased workload 

 

Vascular-selective 

This blocks voltage-gated Ca2+ channels in arterial cells, decreases Ca2+ influx into vascular smooth muscle and reduces release of Ca2+ from sarcoplasmic reticulum. There is reduced smooth muscle contraction and thus vasodilation, and a reduced force is required to overcome diastolic BP and eject stroke volume.

Define chemotherapy 

Chemotherapy describes drugs that target microorganisms (bacteria, viruses, fungi, other parasites) and cancer. They may sometimes be called cytotoxic drugs. The basis of chemotherapy involves targeting selective differences between the infectious microorganism or cancer cell, and the normal host cell. Ideally, there is no damage done to the structure or function of the host cells and organs.  

 

Ultimately, the goal is selective toxicity, aka the inhibition of biochemical pathways or targets that are critical for the survival and/or replication of the pathogen or cancer cell.  

 

Briefly compare antibiotics, antivirals, and antifungals 

-Antibiotics and antifungals can both be divided into drug classes: drugs which kill the pathogen and those which inhibit its growth, relying upon the body's natural immune system to clear the infection. Antivirals are only able to inhibit replication of the virus. (Most antivirals are polymerase inhibitors which prevent genetic replication) 

-Antibiotics and antifungals can be administered after a period of infection and provide therapeutic benefit, whilst antivirals need to be given within a very short timeframe  

 

Briefly describe the discovery of penicillin and its mechanism of action against pneumococcus 

Alexander Fleming discovered in 1928 that staphylococci grown on a culture plate were killed by contamination with Penicillium notatum mould. This was a serendipitous discovery, where observation was key. The mould produced the antibacterial substance penicillin. Penicillium broths were grown in order to produce the penicillin required for treatment.  

 

Penicillin drugs are beta-lactam antibiotics. They interfered with the synthesis of the bacterial cell wall, weakening it by inhibiting transpeptidases that cross-link the peptide chains attached to the peptidoglycan backbone. The weakening of the cell wall leads the bacterial lysis and death.  

 

Briefly describe the strategies that allow antimicrobial drugs to target bacteria as opposed to human cells 

 

Mechanisms of selective targeting include: 

-Unique targets (genetic or biochemical pathway) of the pathogen/cancer cell eg penicillin targets synthesis of bacterial peptidoglycan wall 

-Drugs for unique targets generally have a large therapeutic window (range of doses that produces therapeutic response without causing an adverse effect in patients) 

 

-Selective targets such as a protein isoform unique to the pathogen/cancer cell, eg inhibitors of dihydrofolate reductase enzyme can target particular protein isoforms in bacteria, However, the dihydrofolate reductase enzyme is also used in the synthesis of nucleotides that make up DNA in many microorganisms, and so an excessive dosage of the drug will produce adverse effects. 

-Drugs for selective targets generally have a smaller therapeutic window than those for unique targets 

 

-Common targets: the drug targets metabolic requirements specific to the pathogen/cancer cell. Although the pathogen/cancer cell may share common biological and physiological pathways as the host, it may require a certain metabolic activity, or it may be impacted by a drug to a greater degree than the host. These are targeted, eg drugs targeting DNA synthesis, mitosis and cell cycle progression will impact bacteria and cancer cells more due to their greater rate of division than most host cells.  

-Drugs for common targets have a narrow therapeutic window.  

 

Briefly describe an overview of major antibiotic classes  based on general mechanisms of action (ie bacterial target such as cell wall, protein synthesis, etc.) 

Antibiotics can be classified by either the class/spectrum of bacteria they kill, the biochemical pathway they interfere with, or their chemical structure which allows them to bind to a specific microbial protein/receptor 

 

By biochemical pathway: 

-Bacteriostatic: targets metabolic pathways essential for growth, rather than survival, with the host immune system killing the bacteria 

-Most protein synthesis inhibitors (RNA, transcription, translation inhibitors) are bacteriostatic (with aminoglycosides an important exception) 

 

-Bactericidal: antibiotics that kill the bacteria 

-Inhibitors of cell wall synthesis are bactericidal 

-Inhibitors of DNA synthesis and integrity are generally bactericidal (sulfonamide is bacteriostatic when used alone, but bactericidal when combined with trimethoprim) 

 

Define "antibiotic resistance" and briefly describe the general principles behind its development 

Antibiotic resistance is the ability for a certain strain of bacteria to survive in high concentrations of a certain drug. The use of a drug inherently selects for pathogens that can survive the drug. The replication of this pathogen leads to antibiotic resistance. In a population of bacteria, high cell number, rapid growth rate and high mutation rate promote the development of an antibiotic-resistant population.  

 

The two major driving forces are: 

-Evolution of rapidly dividing microbes 

-Clinical/environmental practices aided by poor therapeutic choice (eg prescribing antibiotics for viral infections, or incomplete antibiotic courses), and indiscriminate use in agriculture/animal husbandry 

 

Resistance may develop at any point in the process by which a drug reaches and binds to its target. Resistance can involve: 

-Reduced entry of the antibiotic into the microbe 

-Enhanced export of the antibiotic by efflux pumps 

-The release of microbial enzymes that destroy the antibiotic 

-Changes to microbial proteins required for antibiotic action 

-Changes to the microbial target protein for antibiotics 

-Development of alternative biochemical pathways to those inhibited by the antibiotic 

 

Penicillin 

Beta-lactamases are a type of microbial enzyme which destroys antibiotics. Beta-lactamases cleave the beta-lactam ring in antibiotics such as penicillin. In combating such resistant bacteria, clavulanic acid can be utilised as a beta-lactamase inhibitor.  

 

Briefly describe the concept of broad vs narrow spectrum antibiotics 

This describes the number of microbial species that are affected by an anti-microbial agent. 

Narrow spectrum- affects few species eg Penicillin G which is effective against gram-positive bacteria and few other microbes 

Broad spectrum- affects many species, eg tetracyclines which are effective against gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria

Describe the adrenergic and cholinergic control of GI motility and the mechanism of action of drugs that affect these processes 

Parasympathetic control 

ACH is used, with the presence of M2 and M3 muscarinic receptors in the GIT. Activation of these receptors increases enteric plexus activity, with the contraction of smooth muscle and an increase in GIT motility and secretions. This aids in digestion and defecation. Too much GIT motility causes diarrhea. 

 

Sympathetic control 

Adrenaline and noradrenaline are used, with the presence of alpha 1, 2, and beta 1 adrenergic receptors. Activation of these receptors inhibit enteric plexus activity, causing the relaxation of smooth muscle and a decrease in GIT motility. Too little GIT motility causes constipation.  

 

Describe the mechanism of action of the agents used to treat diarrhea, including the opioid loperamide, and bile salt sequestrants 

Approaches for treating severe acute diarrhea: 

-Maintenance of fluid and electrolyte balance 

-Use of anti-infective agents eg antibiotics 

-Use of spasmolytic (reduces smooth muscle spasm) or other antidiarrheal agents such as opioids 

 

Enteric neurons express a high level of opioid receptors, with some receptors also on the secretory cells of the GIT. Stimulation of m-opioid receptors decreases GIT motility, and stimulation of d-opioid receptors decreases GIT secretions. The stimulation of these receptors will also cause changes to absorption. 

 

Loperamide 

This is a m-opioid receptor agonist. Stimulation of the receptor inhibits ACH release, relaxing the longitudinal and circular smooth muscle of the intestine to decrease GIT motility. This may also reduce GIT secretions. Overdose can cause constipation. 

 

Cholestyramine (bile salt sequestrant) 

Cholestyramine binds to bile acids, lipid soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K), some bacterial toxins, and some other drugs. An excessive accumulation of bile salts in the GIT causes excessive water and electrolyte secretion which leads to diarrhea. By binding to them, diarrhea can be reduced. 

 

List the neurotransmitters and neuropeptides that trigger nausea and vomiting 

5-HT3 receptor - serotonin 

D2 receptor - dopamine 

M receptor - ACH 

H1 receptor - histamine

Briefly describe the mechanism of action of drug classes that are effective in reducing nausea and vomiting (eg serotonin receptor antagonists, dopamine receptor antagonists, neurokinin receptor antagonists, corticosteroids, histamine receptor antagonists, muscarinic receptor antagonists) 

 

Serotonin receptor antagonist (Setron) eg ondansetron 

Setrons inhibit the 5-HT3 receptors on vagal afferents in the periphery to prevent signal travelling to CNS, and also inhibit 5-HT3 receptors in the chemoreceptor trigger zone and solitary tract nucleus centrally. This prevents the activation of the emetic center. Most effective against cytotoxic drug induced emesis.  

 

Dopamine receptor antagonist eg domperidone, metoclopramide 

These inhibit D2 receptors in the chemoreceptor trigger zone and solitary tract nucleus. Interestingly, they also increase the coordination of GIT motility due to the complex action of dopamine receptor inhibition causing a restored balance of ACH release onto GIT smooth muscle. These drugs also may have weak antagonist action at 5-HT3, histamine, and muscarinic receptors. Most effective against cytotoxic drug induced emesis.  

 

Neurokinin receptor antagonist eg aprepitant  

The neuropeptide substance P causes vomiting, and is released by vagal afferents. The NK1 receptor antagonist blocks the effects of substance P in the chemoreceptor trigger zone (although not marked in diagram) and in the solitary tract nuclei. Most effective against cytotoxic drug induced emesis.  

 

Histamine H1 receptor antagonist eg promethazine, cyclizine  

These are most effective for motion sickness and other vestibular disturbances. It blocks effects of histamine in vestibular afferents and histamine H1 receptors in the solitary tract nucleus. Cyclizine also blocks M1 receptors.  

 

Muscarinic M receptor antagonist eg hyoscine  

These are also most effective for motion sickness and other vestibular disturbances. This blocks the effects of ACH in vestibular afferents.

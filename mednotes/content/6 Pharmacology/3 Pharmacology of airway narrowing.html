Explain the relationships between the targets of the following drug classes and their potential to relieve airway narrowing:  

-Short and long acting bronchodilators (beta-adrenoreceptor agonists, muscarinic receptor antagonists) 

 

Bronchodilators and anti-inflammatory drugs are given by inhalation of aerosol (metered dose inhaler MDI + spacer) or powder (Dry powder inhalation DPI). Bronchodilators can also be given by nebuliser.  

 

Beta-2 adrenergic agonists target receptors on the airway smooth muscle, airway epithelium (eg cilia) and glands, airway mast cells, and in the alveoli. The stimulation of these receptors on bronchial smooth muscle activates the [adenylate cyclase - cAMP] pathway, which increases cAMP and reduces smooth muscle tone. It also increases the calcium-sensitive K+ channels in airway smooth muscle, leading to membrane hyperpolarisation and relaxation. In other cell types, the adrenergic agonists inhibit the function of inflammatory cells (mast, basophil, eosinophil, neutrophil, lymphocyte), increases cAMP to inhibit inflammatory mediator and cytokine release, and increases mucociliary clearance.  

 

Type 1- Reliever: SABA (short acting beta-2 adrenoreceptor agonists) eg salbutamol, with rapid onset of action (5-15 mins), peaking 15-30 mins and lasting 3-5 hrs. This short half-life of 2.7-5 hours requires it to be repeated 3-4 times a day, and administered when required. 

 

Note: salbutamol can cause tremors (beta-2 adrenergic receptors in skeletal muscle), tachycardia (beta-1 adrenergic receptors stimulating heart), and tolerance [both SABAs and LABAs]. 

 

Type 2- Controller: LABA (long acting beta-2 adrenoreceptor agonists) eg salmeterol, onset of action takes 10-30 mins, peaks 3-4 hours, longer half-life (acts for 8-12 hours). This is due to high lipophilicity, which allows it to stay in the cell membrane for longer. This is administered twice daily to maintain bronchodilation.  

 

Contraindications: Beta-blockers antagonising the effects of adrenaline will cause a greater influence of parasympathetic control, leading to bronchoconstriction that is dangerous in asthmatics. Precautions must also be taken in cardiovascular patients. Desensitisation also occurs, primarily through sequestration into endosomes, and then the degradation of receptors by the lysosome.  

 

Muscarinic receptors antagonists include short and long-acting, SAMA and LAMA.  

Mechanism of action: antagonism at M3 receptors prevents activation of G-alpha-q/phospholipase C/IP3 signaling pathway, which reduces Ca2+ release, reducing smooth muscle tone, and causing bronchodilation. Mucus secretion is inhibited, and mucociliary clearance of bronchial secretions may be increased. There are few side effects (dry mouth, throat irritation).  

 

-Anti-inflammatory drugs (inhaled corticosteroids) 

These are preventer drugs, which have no bronchodilation action. These are synthetic glucocorticoid steroid hormones with potent anti-inflammatory effects. They activate the glucocorticoid receptor in the cell's cytoplasm which migrates to the nucleus and binds DNA, regulating the expression of specific genes and changing the levels of proteins involved in inflammation.  

 

Inhaled corticosteroids (ICS) are usually taken with LABA using a metered dose inhaler. The combination inhaler generally reduces the amount of steroidal drug use, minimising adverse effects. Furthermore, patients given two inhalers instead of one will often take the LABA more often due to its faster action, leading to adverse effects eg tolerance. Convenience is also a factor.  

 

Adverse corticosteroid effects are rare. These effects include thrush, voice hoarseness, throat irritation, and bruising. Their occurrence can be reduced by using a spacer with an MDI.  

 

-Other drug classes (methylxanthines, leukotriene antagonists, cromoglycate) 

 

Methylxanthines (not a very good drug, only small improvement in lung function, severe side effects eg heartburn, insomnia, headache, tachycardia, tachypnea [rapid breathing]) 

Bronchodilator/controller, first developed to be phosphodiesterase inhibitors, reducing cAMP metabolism, increasing cAMP, which may account for some bronchodilator effects (mechanism of action is uncertain). Methylxanthines are adenosine receptor antagonists, which reduce adenosine-induced bronchoconstriction. Note: methylxanthines curiously upregulate adenosine A1 receptors in asthma. Activation of these receptors causes bronchoconstriction.  

 

These drugs increase apoptosis of eosinophils, decrease inflammatory mediators and reduce endothelial leakage. They also cause bronchodilation in airway smooth muscle.  

 

Chromones (preventer) 

Cromoglycate and nedocromil are mast cell degranulation inhibitors. They inhibit the immediate allergic response to antigen presentation. They also inhibit mediator release from eosinophils, neutrophils, monocytes, macrophages, lymphocytes. They rarely have adverse effects.  

 

Montelukast (leukotriene antagonist) 

This is primarily an anti-inflammatory (preventer), but is also able to produce bronchodilation. They are an antagonist at cysteinyl leukotriene receptor type 1, reducing leukotriene-induced smooth muscle contraction and inflammation.  

 

Briefly describe anti-immunoglobin E therapy for allergic asthma 

Omalizumab is a drug used for anti-IgE therapy. IgE bound to omalizumab cannot bind to IgE receptors on mast cells, thereby reducing the response to allergens. This drug has a long half-life of 26 days, and is therefore injected every 2-4 weeks.

Briefly describe the phenomena of desensitisation and tolerance to effects of beta agonists 

-Uncoupling of G-Protein-Coupled Receptor: although an agonist is bound to the receptor, downstream cascade effects do not occur. 

-Down-regulation: The receptor is sequestered into an endosome, which is transported to the lysosome for digestion. 

 

Briefly describe the benefits of targeted drug delivery for airway narrowing (eg reduced systemic absorption leading to reduced adverse effects) 

-Faster action 

-Better absorption 

-Targeted beta-blockers are needed to prevent antagonising SABAs and LABAs 

-Muscle tremors and tachycardia can be caused by beta-adrenergic agonists 

-Corticosteroids cause a very wide range of adverse effects if taken orally and not inhaled, including glaucoma (elevated pressure in eyes), fluid retention causing swelling in lower legs, hypertension, psychological effects, weight gain, osteoporosis, adrenal gland hormone production, thin skin 

 

 

Briefly describe how drug half-life changes dosing of drugs for airway narrowing 

Relievers and controllers have a short half-life, and are used as required as they have a faster action. 

Preventers have a long half-life and slower action, and thus are taken regularly.  

 

Briefly describe how the formulation of drugs for airway narrowing can affect drug compliance 

-Usage of a combination inhaler improves compliance 

-Combining short- and long- acting drugs will prevent patients from using only the short, faster-acting drug. 

-Combination allows reduced use of certain drugs eg corticosteroids, to reduce side effects. This encourages drug compliance.

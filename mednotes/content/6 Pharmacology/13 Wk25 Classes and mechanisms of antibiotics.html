Describe the general features, spectrum of activity, and mechanisms of action of the main classes of antibiotics 

 

The three types of antibiotics by site of action 

Antibiotics can be divided into three categories by their site of action. We have: 

-Inhibitors of cell wall synthesis eg penicillin, vancomycin 

-Inhibitors of transcription and translation eg tetracycline, rifampin, macrolides 

-Inhibitors of DNA synthesis and integrity eg sulfonamides, quinolones, trimethoprim 

 

Inhibitors of cell wall synthesis: mainly beta-lactams but also glycopeptides etc 

 

Beta-lactams (bactericidal) 

Bacterial cells produce the peptidoglycan cell wall through polymerisation, which requires extensive cross linking between side chains of amino acids. The transpeptidase enzyme activates the side-linking between these chains. Beta-lactams form covalent bonds with the transpeptidase enzyme to prevent further cell wall maintenance. This causes cell lysis due to the influx of water by osmosis. 

 

Spectrum of action 

The spectrum of action of these drugs depends on their ability to cross the cell wall/membrane and their affinity for transpeptidase (aka penicillin-binding-protein PBP).  

 

Hydrophilic drugs can diffuse through the thick murein layer and pass through the outer membrane pores of gram-negative bacteria with more ease than hydrophobic drugs. Hence, hydrophilic drugs like ampicillin and amoxicillin are more broad-spectrum than hydrophobic drugs like dicloxacillin and benzylpenicillin.  

 

Broad spectrum beta-lactams will also have a strong affinity for multiple transpeptidases.  

 

Glycopeptides eg vancomycin (bactericidal) 

These drugs inhibit the release of the amino acids used for cross linking by inhibiting glycosyltransferase. These are mainly effective for gram-positive bacteria. 

 

Inhibitors of DNA synthesis 

 

Disruption of folate synthesis (bacteriostatic) 

The dietary folate synthesis pathway for the production of DNA exists in bacteria but not humans.  

Sulfonamides and trimethoprim disrupt folate synthesis to inhibit DNA synthesis, causing bacteriostatic action for eradication by host immune system. Both sulfonamides are trimethoprim are broad-spectrum over gram+ and gram-.  

 

Topoisomerase (DNA gyrase) inhibitor (bactericidal) 

DNA replication and gene transcription requires the unwinding and separation of supercoil DNA strands. In bacteria, topoisomerase II cuts and reseals the DNA whilst topoisomerase IV facilitates the separation of the two circular DNA copies into the two daughter cells. These topoisomerases are different to those found in humans.  

 

Quinolones such as ciprofloxacin inhibits both topoisomerase II (targets gram-) and topoisomerase IV (targets gram+), thus possessing broad spectrum bactericidal effects.  

 

Inhibitors of transcription and translation 

 

Transcription inhibitor eg rifamycin (bactericidal) 

Drugs in the rifamycin category eg rifampicin form highly stable complexes with RNA polymerase, inhibiting RNA synthesis and causing bacterial death. (Used to treat TB.) 

 

30s ribosome inhibitor (aminoglycosides bactericidal, tetracyclines bacteriostatic) 

Aminoglycosides eg streptomycin block the initiation of protein synthesis by causing the misreading of mRNA. These are narrow spectrum (used for aerobic gram- bacteria), and have a concentration dependent bactericidal effect.  

 

Tetracyclines prevent tRNA binding to the 30s ribosomes, blocking the initiation of protein synthesis. These have a broad-spectrum effect.  

 

50s ribosome inhibitor (bacteriostatic) 

Macrolides eg clarithromycin, erythromycin bind irreversibly to the 50s ribosome to inhibit translocation and movement of the peptide chain. Erythromycin is effective against most gram+ bacteria and spirochetes.  

 

Lincosamides eg clindamycin bind to the 50s ribosome to inhibit peptide bond formation. It is effective against most aerobic gram+ and most anaerobic bacteria. 

 

Chloramphenicol binds reversibly to the 50s ribosome to inhibit peptide bond formation. It can also inhibit mitochondrial protein synthesis in mammalian cells. It is broad spectrum.

Describe the mechanisms by which bacteria can develop antibiotic resistance and the pharmacologic strategies to overcome or prevent this 

 

The two main driving forces to antibiotic resistance are the evolution of microbes and environmental practices. Bacteria undergo mutations and gene amplifications/duplications to transform from a low resistance colony toward higher resistance. Bacteria stop growing when the available space is filled.  

 

The bacterial genome involves not only the bacterial DNA, but also the plasmids (short circular double strand DNA) which are capable of self-replicating. Plasmids can code for additional genes in certain situations, eg antibiotic resistance (R plasmids). Antibiotic resistance can be transferred from one bacteria to another if the relevant genes occur on transposons.  

 

Overview of antibiotic resistance mechanisms: resistance can develop at any point in the process 

-Reduced entry of drug into microbe 

-Enhanced export of drug 

-Microbial enzyme release inactivating drug 

-Changes to microbial proteins required for pro-drug activation, or changes to targeted proteins 

-Development of alternative biochemical pathways to those inhibited 

 

Reduced entry 

Loss of porin channels, active influx channels and other cell wall mutations can reduce the permeability of drugs like beta-lactams, aminoglycosides, tetracycline etc. 

 

Enhanced export 

Tetracyclines, beta lactams and macrolides can all be subject to enhanced export resulting in insufficient accumulation within the bacteria. 

 

Inactivation by microbial enzymes 

Aminoglycosides are inactivated by phosphorylation, adenylation or acetylation. Beta-lactams are broken down by beta-lactamase. (Beta lactamase can be inhibited by clavulanic acid.) 

 

Changes to proteins 

Single point or multi-point mutations can change the conformation of the target protein. This can reduce the binding affinity between antibiotic and target, or between prodrug and enzyme.  

 

-A chromosomal mutation on the 30s ribosome can cause aminoglycoside resistance 

-Plasmid-mediated change to 50s ribosome can cause resistance to erythromycin 

-A point mutation in DNA gyrase can cause quinolone resistance 

-A chromosomal mutation in DNA-dependent RNA polymerase can cause rifampicin resistance 

 

Development of alternative biochemical pathways 

-Plasmid directed synthesis of dihydrofolate reductase with low trimethoprim affinity can cause trimethoprim resistance 

-Plasmid mediated production of dihydropteroate synthetase with low affinity for sulfonamides can cause sulfonamide resistance 

 

Describe the relevant considerations when choosing empiric antibiotic treatment 

-What is the most likely infecting organism? 

-What is the local antibiotic resistance pattern for that organism? 

-What are potential consequences for this patient of choosing an antibiotic whose spectrum is too narrow?

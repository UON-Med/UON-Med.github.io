Compare mucus membranes to skin in terms of vulnerability, exposure, colonisation and immune function 

Vulnerability 

The skin is much less vulnerable, as it has more layers of defense. The epidermis of the epithelium is a protective layer over the body's surface, providing a mechanical barrier. It is also colonised with a diverse population of microbes, which deter colonisation by other pathogenic microbes. The epithelium also secretes low pH, sebaceous fluid and fatty acids that inhibit the growth of disease-producing bacteria. Trauma or burns are usually required to break this barrier to lead to infection.  

 

Mucosal membranes are located in the respiratory tract, GI tract and reproductive tract, and largely serve as the point of contact between the external and internal environments. The barrier produces a sticky mucus which traps pathogens and regulates immune function. Microstructures such as cilia help propel pathogens out of the body. Chemical factors, low pH and antimicrobial peptides can be produced to directly kill bacteria. Physical movements such as the peristalsis of the gut and urine flow can minimise the chances of bacteria attachment. 

 

Exposure 

The body is exposed to almost no pathogens via the skin barrier, as it is covered in a protective mechanical barrier. However, the body is more exposed to pathogens through mucosal membranes as these membranes are more permeable to gases, amino acids, nutrients, and other matter.  

 

Colonisation 

Pathogenic bacteria colonise different layers of the skin architecture in small numbers. They compete with indigenous microbiota for nutrients. When they multiply, they break the skin barrier and a skin disease develops. Severe colonisation and growth often occurs after lacerations, bite wounds, sharp injuries, burns, surgery, and other forms of trauma.  

 

Pathogens can colonise the mucosal membranes without injury to the body, through inhalation, ingestion, or inoculation. Pathogens gain access to the lamina propria by endocytosis. Colonisation of the mucus membrane is more difficult, due to its regulation of white blood cells (by mucosal epithelial cells), the mucociliary escalator and the secretion of antibodies. The highly hydrated mucus gel covers the mucosal surface and protects the epithelial cells against chemical, enzymatic, microbial and mechanical damage. Highly diverse glycoproteins and glycolipids form the glycocalyx coating which protects the host from infection. The GI mucus membrane is also colonised by a large amount of native microbiota, resulting in competition for nutrients. The mucosal environment is generally difficult for pathogens to thrive in, with antimicrobial peptides and an unsuitable pH range.

M cells (specialised mucosal epithelial cells) facilitate the transfer of luminal antigens, viruses, bacteria and other pathogens to the Peyer's patches (lymphoid tissue in ileum). Interplay between antigen presenting cells and CD4+ T helper cells contributes to the production of sIgA by B cells. SIgA is crucial in preventing infection and inflammation at the mucosal surface. Cell-mediated immunity including CD4+ T helper cells and CD8+ cytotoxic T cells efficiently recognise and lyse infected cells, preventing the replication of viruses. 

 

 

Briefly describe the methods of transmission of infectious diseases and give two common disease examples for each method 

-Physical contact: Hepatitis B, HIV 

-Inhalation: flu, varicella 

-Ingestion: Hepatitis A, cholera 

-Inoculation: rabies, Hepatitis C 

-Congenital: herpes, rubella (unborn fetus/newborn infant infected by mother) 

-Hospital and laboratory: Pneumonia, UTIs 

 

Briefly describe the epidemiologic triad of infectious disease 

The triad of agent, host and environment is described as the epidemiologic triad of infectious disease. Agent and host are brought together in an environment to cause a disease in the host. A vector is an organism which transmits infection by conveying the pathogen from one host cell to another without causing disease itself (eg mosquitoes that are a parasite for malaria).  

 

Discuss the factors that determine and individual's susceptibility to infection, including exposure, age, vaccination, comorbidity, and variation (anatomical anomalies, foreign bodies, stasis, etc.) 

-Exposure: 

The pathogenicity of the microbe influences the individual's susceptibility to infection by it 

-Age: 

The young and the elderly have less effective immune systems, and are therefore more susceptible to infections in general. 

-Vaccination: 

Vaccinated individuals are prepared to mount an immune response, protecting them from disease-causing infections  

-Comorbidity: 

An individual that is already suffering from a disease is more vulnerable to receiving another infection due to the body's weakened immune system. 

-Variation: 

Genetic variations that protect an individual against a particular pathogen may increase the individual's risk to another. Environmental variations in weather, food and water quality will also impact an individual's susceptibility to infection.

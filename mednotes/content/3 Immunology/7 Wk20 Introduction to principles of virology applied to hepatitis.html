Describe and compare the structure and replication strategies of Hepatitis A, B, C 

Hepatitis A (picoRNAviridae)  

-Positive sense, genome in capsid 

-Single stranded RNA virus 

-Non-enveloped 

-Viral protein covalently linked to the 5' end of the genome, used as a primer for transcription 

-6 subtypes 

 

Hepatitis B (hepaDNAviridae)  

-Virus particle aka Dane particle has a nucleocapsid protein core inside a lipid envelope, non-infectious filamentous and spherical bodies are also produced 

-HBsAg surface antigens: 3 types, Large, Middle and Small 

-HBcAg  (and HBeAg splice variant) core antigens 

-Double stranded with partial single-stranding 

-One end linked to viral DNA polymerase which has reverse transcriptase activity similar to retroviruses 

-8 known genotypes, at least 24 subtypes 

 

Hepatitis C (flaviviridae)  

-Positive sense 

-Single stranded RNA virus 

-Genome surrounded by both protein capsid and lipid envelope 

-Seven major genotypes, 67 subtypes 

-E proteins at surface of lipid envelope mediate attachment to host receptors and viral entry 

 

Describe the impact of having a viral envelope on transmission and replication, and compare this with the role of the capsid for non-enveloped viruses 

HepA: +ssRNA, capsid 

HepB: dsDNA-RT, envelope 

HepC: +ssRNA, envelope 

 

The envelope or capsid determines the mechanism of viral entry and virion release. Envelopes are derived from host cell membranes.  

 

Non-enveloped viruses like HepA are more stable and can survive much longer in the environment, participating in fecal-oral transmission and through contaminated food and water. 

 

The lipid-bilayer envelope on viruses like HepB and HepC is sensitive to desiccation, heat, and detergents. These viruses have limited survival outside of the host environment, and must transfer directly from host to host eg through blood or bodily fluids. However, enveloped viruses can adapt and change surface proteins to evade the immune system and therefore cause persistent infections.  

 

Note: Enveloped viruses have a longer incubation period and therefore take longer to replicate. 

 

Means of replication 

HepA: the virus attaches to host receptors and undergoes endocytosis. The +ssRNA is translated to viral polyproteins, which are then cleaved to form structural viral proteins. +ssRNA is transcribed to -ssRNA which is used to make new viral genomes. New virions are assembled and released through cell lysis.  

 

HepB: the virus enters via clathrin-dependent endocytosis, with the viral envelope fusing with the cell membrane to release the virus core into the cytoplasm. The viral DNA is transferred to the cell nucleus, where partially dsDNA is converted to fully dsDNA by host DNA polymerases, transforming it to cccDNA used as a template for viral mRNA transcription. The mRNA is used to make new genomes, capsid core protein and DNA polymerase. Assembly occurs at the endoplasmic reticulum, and viral release occurs. (No need for cell lysis) 

 

Hep C: the virus binds to a cell receptor with at least 4 different proteins, and undergoes endocytosis. The capsid protein coat dissolves and viral RNA is released. The viral RNA induces the formation of a membranous web compartment close to the nucleus where RNA replication occurs. Viral proteins are made then cut by proteases from both the virus and infected cell. Hundreds of HepC RNA are made by a polymerase enzyme, then assembled in a capsid. Budding into a fluid-filled sac occurs in the cell, then exocytosis occurs. 

 

Briefly describe the acute and chronic illness caused by hepatitis viruses, including progression to hepatocellular carcinoma 

Hepatitis is an inflammation of the liver. In acute form, it is self-limiting. However, it can also be chronic (longer than 6 months) leading to fibrosis, cirrhosis and finally liver cancer.  

 

HepA doesn't usually result in chronic hepatitis. HepB and HepC can cause liver cancer. 

 

Relate the modes of transmission of hepatitis virus to the identified "at risk" groups in the community 

HepA 

This is transmitted via the fecal-oral route, contaminated food/water, or through direct contact with an infectious person. Hence, poor sanitation, water, living in a household or being a sexual partner with an infectious patient are all risk factors. 

 

HepB 

This is transmitted from mother to child, or through bodily fluids and blood. (Blood especially in children <5 years old). The likelihood of chronic HepB infection is high when the patient is young, but is smaller when the patient is an adult. People at risk are newborns, HIV patients, healthcare workers, IV drug users, travelers and people living with infected patients. 

 

HepC 

This is a blood-borne virus. Transmission occurs via sharing needles, unsterilised medical equipment, unscreened transfusions, sexually, and from mother to baby. People at risk include IV drug users, intranasal drug users, patients receiving inadequately controlled blood products or invasive procedure, children born to an infected mother, sexual partners of infected patients, people with HIV, people with tattoos/piercings.  

 

Briefly describe and compare the mechanisms of anti-viral medications available for hepatitis B and C and HIV 

HepB 

This is treated with an immunomodulatory drug releasing interferon. Interferons are used to treat chronic HepB infections in adults, and children at risk for liver disease. It is delivered by subcutaneous injection, and blocks the reverse transcriptase or nucleoside analog function. 

 

HepC 

Oral direct acting antivirals (DAAs) are rapidly being developed. Drugs include NS3/4A protease inhibitors, NS5A inhibitors, NS5B polymerase inhibitors, cyclophilin inhibitors, non-nucleoside inhibitors, and other inhibitors of gene replication. All patients are considered for antiviral therapy and should abstain from alcohol consumption. The goal of therapy is negative HepC RNA on the sustained virologic response (SRV) test 24 weeks post therapy. Such a result 12 weeks post therapy is also indicative of a cure.  

 

HIV 

Six classes of antiretroviral agents currently exist as follows: 

-Nucleoside and non-nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors 

-Protease inhibitors 

-Integrase inhibitors 

-Fusion inhibitors 

-Chemokine receptor antagonists 

 

Each class targets a different step in the viral life cycle. 

 

Briefly describe the development and use of vaccines to prevent hepatitis 

HepA 

Passive immunisation from mother gives < 3 months immunity to the baby. Active immunisation is a better option. Four HepA monovalent vaccines are available, prepared from different strands of HepA. Nearly all people develop protective levels of antibodies to HepA within one month of a single dose. Also, a single dose of the vaccine within two weeks after viral exposure still has protective effects. Hence, the vaccine can be used both preventatively and therapeutically.  

 

HepB 

The HepB vaccine contains the HepB surface antigen, produced using recombinant DNA technology. Infants are given a birth dose, then at 2, 4, 6 months. In children/adolescents a 3-dose schedule (0, 1, 6 months) from first dose is used. Proper immunisation yields 95% protective rate. 

 

There is currently no HepC vaccine.

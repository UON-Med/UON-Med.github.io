Describe and compare the structure, types and epidemiology of influenza virus and pneumococcus 

Structure 

The influenza virion is roughly spherical. It is enveloped by a lipid bilayer membrane that is taken from the host cell in which it multiplies (Classified as enveloped virus). The lipid membrane contains glycoprotein "spikes," hemagglutinin and neuraminidase. These are the proteins that determine the flu's subtype. These "spikes" are targeted by our antibodies and certain drugs. Also embedded in the membrane is the M2 ion channel protein, also the target of certain drugs. Inside the lipid membrane is the M1 matrix protein, which forms a shell giving strength and rigidity to the lipid envelope. Within the interior of the virion are the viral RNAs: 8 of them (single stranded RNA) each encoding one or two proteins for Influenza A.  

 

The pneumococcus bacteria are gram positive, and small (1 micrometer). They exist as elongated cocci, with one broad end and the other end pointed, presenting a flame shaped/lanceolate appearance. They occur in pairs, with the broad ends opposing each other. They are capsulated, with a capsule enclosing each pair. They are non-motile and do not produce spores.  

 

Types 

Influenza can be classified as A, B and C. Flu A and B are common causes of acute respiratory illnesses, with flu A the principal cause of larger epidemics. Variations in the "spikes" in the membrane result in different subtypes. Different combinations of RNA strands making up the 8 within the virion lead to further variations.  

 

The pneumococcal bacteria is surrounded by a polysaccharide capsule, with more than 90 serotypes.  

 

Epidemiology 

Seasonal influenza causes approximately 5 million cases of severe illness and 500 000 deaths annually worldwide. The population at high risk includes older people, young children, and immunocompromised patients. Other risk factors include obesity, pregnancy, smoking, diabetes mellitus, chronic cardiovascular disease, renal disease, and delayed presentation to hospital. Influenza is naturally hosted by birds.  

 

Prevention/treatment: handwashing, clean surfaces, properly cook bird products, face mask, vaccine, antiviral treatment 

 

Pneumococcal disease causes around 1.6 million deaths annually. Young, elderly, and patients with underlying health issues are at greater risk of pneumonia, as healthy individuals often do not develop the disease when exposed to pneumococcus. Other risk factors include patients with chronic organ failure of the heart, lung, kidney or liver, diabetes mellitus, alcoholism, children with spleen removed, sickle-celled children, immunosuppressed individuals (especially those with asplenia [dysfunction of spleen]). Patients recovering from previous pneumococcal disease require the vaccine for protection against subsequent pneumococcal infection.  

 

Prevention/treatment: Vaccine, antibiotics 

 

 

Describe and compare the mechanisms of viral infection (influenza) to acute bacterial infection (pneumococcus) 

 

Influenza replication cycle 

 

Pathogenesis: transmitted from person to person in droplets released by sneezing and coughing. Some inhaled virus lands on the lower respiratory tract, which is the primary site of infection (nasopharynx also involved). The superficial mucosa suffers destruction and desquamation (epithelial cells break into lumen) because of the infection of bronchial epithelial cells.  

 

Note: following severe influenza infection, lung epithelial cells and alveolar macrophages produce a large amount of chemokines/cytokines (mainly interferons) and initiate the cytokine storm that causes more extensive immune responses and inflammation.  

 

Bacteria 

Entry of pneumococci into nasopharynx leads to colonisation of the nasopharynx. This may cause an infection of the middle ear, paranasal sinuses and respiratory tract by direct spread. The infection of the meninges may also occur, by contiguity or blood. The bacteria enters the blood causing bacteremia, which may also lead to disseminated infections in the heart, peritoneum or joints.  

 

Note: Streptococcus pneumoniae are natural inhabitants of the upper respiratory tract of human beings. Many of us have it in our nasopharynx, oropharynx and skin.  

 

Briefly describe and compare the virulence factors of influenza and pneumococcus 

Influenza 

-Antigenic shift (process by which two or more different strains of a virus, or of different viruses, combine to form a new subtype having a mixture of the surface antigens of the two or more original strains) 

-Antigenic drift (accumulation of mutations that facilitate evasion of the host immune response) 

-New viruses constantly emerge from the environment, from sources such as waterfowl, swine, poultry and sea mammals 

 

Pneumococcus 

-Polysaccharide capsule, >90 serotypes, resistant to phagocytosis and antibiotics 

-Pneumococci in intimate contact with lung cells show reduced capsular polysaccharide 

-Pneumolysin toxin is a cholesterol-dependent cytolysin which forms pores in healthy cells' membranes, leading to cytokine synthesis and an inflammatory response.

Briefly describe the mechanism of vaccination in the prevention of influenza and pneumonia 

Influenza 

-Seasonal quadrivalent vaccine update annually to keep pace with antigenic drift 

-Specific swine H1N1 virus pandemic vaccine 

 

Pneumococcus 

-23-valent pneumococcal vaccine contains purified capsular polysaccharides derived from 23 Streptococcus pneumoniae serotypes 

-Serotype coverage: 85-90% of serotypes responsible for all cases of invasive pneumococcal disease 

-Vaccine includes major serotypes that have developed antibiotic resistance (90%) 

-Vaccine induces antibodies that enhance opsonisation, phagocytosis, and bacterial killing

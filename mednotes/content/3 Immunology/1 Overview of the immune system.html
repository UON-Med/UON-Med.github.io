Briefly describe and differentiate the innate and adaptive immune response 

The immune system has 3 levels of defense.  

1st line: physical barriers of the skin and mucosal surfaces eg lung and gut epithelium. Chemical agents like saliva, sebum, tears also count.  

2nd line: Innate immunity (non-specific resistance) which destroys invaders in a generalised way, inflammation which produces swelling, fever which inhibits bacterial growth and increases rate of tissue repair. The 2nd line also triggers the activation of the adaptive immune system (3rd line). 

3rd line: Specific resistance (acquired immunity), relying on antigens found in foreign microbes to produce an immune response. In the humoral immune response, a B lymphocyte will rapidly multiply when it encounters an antigen it recognises. These cells then secrete antibodies to assist in killing off the pathogens. An immunological memory is created, with subsequent encounters involving less collateral tissue damage 

 

Briefly describe the role of the spleen, lymph system, bone marrow and thymus in the immune response 

Primary lymphoid organs: Red bone marrow and thymus gland 

-Where lymphocytes are formed, matured and educated 

-Remove self-reactive lymphocytes 

Secondary lymphoid organs: Lymph nodes, spleen, Peyer's patches, mucosal tissues (NALT, Adenoids, Tonsils)  

-Generate immune responses and peripheral tolerance  

 

The lymphatic system 

This is a network of drainage vessels that collects fluid from tissues, has unidirectional flow toward the heart and is often the first place where antigens are detected.  

-Plasma is the colourless fluid part of blood, lymph and milk, in which corpuscles (fat globules) are suspended 

-Interstitial fluid (ISF) is the main component of the extracellular fluid, alongside plasma and transcellular fluid 

-Lymph is the clear yellowish, slightly alkaline, coagulable fluid containing white blood cells conveyed to the bloodstream via lymphatic vessels 

-Lymph nodes responsible for the maturation of the adaptive immune response, are the 1st line of response to antigens (with high specificity) 

  

Red bone marrow 

This is used for hematopoiesis (responsible for producing all cell type constituents of the blood), B cell maturation, B cell selection, and produces mature, naïve B cells. 

 

Thymus 

The thymus is responsible for central immunological tolerance by selecting immunocompetent, non-self reactive T cells and for killing self-reactive T cells. 

-Takes in immature T cells and puts out mature (immunocompetent) T cells 

-Increases diversity of T cells 

-T-cell selection 

-T-cell tolerance is induced in the thymus 

 

Spleen 

The spleen is compartmentalised into both red pulp and white pulp throughout.  

-Red pulp filters out old Red Blood Cells 

-Responds to antigens in the circulatory system 

-White pulp produces activated T cells 

-Important in combating bacterial infections, removes antibody-coated bacteria and antibody-coated blood cells  by way of blood and lymph node circulation 

 

Briefly describe the role of the key immune cells in the immune response- this includes phagocytes (neutrophils and monocyte/macrophages), dendritic cells, and lymphocytes.  

-Helper T lymphocytes assist other leukocytes in immunologic processes including the maturation of B cells into plasma cells and memory B cells and the activation of cytotoxic T cells and microphages. Helper T cells are activated when they are presented with peptide antigens by MHC class II molecules on the surface of antigen-presenting cells. 

-Cytotoxic T cells destroy virus-infected cells and tumour cells. They are also implicated in transplant rejection. These cells recognise their targets by binding to antigens associated with MHC class I molecules.  

-Memory T lymphocytes persist long-term after an infection has occurred, and quickly expand to large numbers of effector T cells upon re-exposure to their cognate antigen. 

-B lymphocytes are part of the adaptive humoral immunity system. B cells express the B cell receptor (BCR) on their external surface to enable a B cell to bind to a specific antigen.  B cells make antibodies, perform the role of antigen-presenting cells, and develop into memory B cells after activation by antigen interaction.  

-Plasma B cells: fully differentiated B-lymphocyte which produces a single type of antibody. Plasma cells are non-motile, and are located in the secondary lymphoid organs. Both T cells and B plasma cells can form memory B and T cells if the survive long enough. Both of these memory cells will activate and clonally proliferate upon re-contact with an antigen to elicit an immune response. 

-Granulocytes are a category of white blood cells, characterised by granules in their cytoplasm. The most common, neutrophils, carry out phagocytosis and inflammation. Eosinophils release toxins to kill multicellular pathogens. Basophils contain histamine to dilate vessels and bring more blood to the area of injury, also secreting anticoagulants to function against hypersensitivity reactions. Mast cells, like basophils, contain histamine an anticoagulants. They play a key role in the inflammatory and allergic process, degranulating and releasing these chemical mediators upon binding to immunoglobin E or physical injury or exposure to microbial pathogens. 

-Dendritic cells are antigen-presenting cells (APCs) which process antigen material and present it on its surface to T cells. These act as messengers between the innate and adaptive immune systems.  

-NK cells are cytotoxic, and kill viral-infected or tumour cells by inducing apoptosis within them. NK cells are non-specific.

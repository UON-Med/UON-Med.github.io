Describe the maintenance of DNA and control of fidelity in replication 

DNA is maintained by proto-oncogenes and tumour suppression genes. Caretaker genes perform DNA repair. Telomeres act as protective caps at the end of chromosomes, preventing the chromosomes from binding to each other.  

 

Proto-oncogenes 

These genes are involved in the production of growth hormones, the receptors for these hormones/factors, proteins involved in signal transduction, and encode for transcription factors resulting in changes to gene expression.  

 

Oncogenes 

Proto-oncogenes can become an oncogene if mutation occurs. These can result from spontaneous DNA mutation (replication error), carcinogens, or retroviruses (viruses). Mutations may result in point mutations, gene amplifications, or chromosomal arrangements. The result is over-expression/gain of function of the oncogene.  

 

Tumour suppression gene 

These code for proteins that restrain cell growth and division. The p53 tumour-suppressing gene induces cell cycle arrest and apoptosis (blocking S-phase entry at G1-S checkpoint). More than half of human tumours have a p53 mutation. 

 

Describe how genetic mutations can lead to an inherited predisposition to cancer (using examples from colon cancer) 

The two-hit hypothesis suggests that mutation in one allele (on one chromosome of the pair) predisposes a person to cancer. A mutation in the other allele causes cancer. Someone who inherits a mutation in a tumour suppression gene has inherited and thus already taken their "first hit".  

 

Describe the cellular aging process in terms of DNA and telomeres 

Telomeres are protective, repetitive sequences at the end of each chromosome. They eventually become shorter with each round of cell division, and eventually become too short (senescence). Hence, cells age and die. The telomerase enzyme adds a telomere sequence to the end of the chromosomes. Telomerase is expressed in only a few adult cells. In cancer cells, re-activation of telomerase allows cells to replicate indefinitely.  

 

Describe the multistep model of carcinogenesis 

Most cancers are caused by 2-8 sequential mutations in key genes, with some cancers having up to 200 mutations. Cancer occurs via the accumulation of mutations over time. 

 

 

 

Define carcinogen and briefly describe the role of carcinogens in the development of cancer 

A carcinogen is a substance capable of causing cancer in living tissue. Carcinogens damage DNA, interfere with normal cellular processes, and increase the chances of cancer growth.

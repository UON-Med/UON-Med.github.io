Briefly describe the normal range for blood glucose and the consequences of levels that are either too high or too low (in general overview terms) 

 

Normal fasting state: 70-100 mg/dL 

Normal post meal: 70-140 mg/dL  

 

Hypoglycemia leads to decreased cognition, coma and death.  

Hyperglycemia can cause long-term complications such as macrovascular disease (coronary artery disease, cerebrovascular disease, peripheral vascular disease), or microvascular disease (retinopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy).  

 
List the major hormones secreted by the endocrine pancreas and the general role of each 

Insulin 

-Secreted by beta cells 

-Increases expression of glucose uptake transporters in adipose and muscle cells, thus increasing glucose uptake 

-Increases muscle and liver glycogen synthesis 

-Increased active transport of amino acids at muscle and liver to promote protein synthesis 

-Increases LPL at adipose tissue capillaries to facilitate uptake of FFAs and their storage as TGs 

-Inhibits HSL to decrease lipolysis and thus prevent ketone body formation 

-Inhibits ApoB100 production to reduce VLDL formation 

 

Glucagon 

-Secreted by alpha cells 

-Acts only at liver to promote glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis to increase blood sugar 

 

Describe insulin synthesis, secretion, transport and function in metabolism 

Synthesis 

 

Insulin is synthesised as a pre-pro-hormone, with a signal peptide, hormone, and amino acid chain. Cleaving occurs to only leave the insulin hormone, made of two chains connected by disulfide bridges. Mature insulin is packed and stored in vesicles until released by exocytosis due to a calcium signal.  

 

Secretion 

Glucose enters beta cell through GLUT-2 transporter, where glycolysis occurs then leading to ATP inactivation of the K+ efflux channel. There is membrane depolarisation and Ca2+ flows in, causing insulin release. Insulin release is always biphasic: 

 

Note: insulin has a short half-life of 5-8 minutes 

 

Describe membrane bound hormone receptors and their activation/inactivation, up/down regulation, and sensitisation/desensitisation 

 

Reception of insulin 

Receptor tyrosine kinase (RTK) is the human insulin receptor. Binding of insulin to the receptor activates intrinsic tyrosine kinase activity of the receptor, which in turn leads to activation of insulin receptor substrates and signaling cascades.  

-Rapid: glucose uptake through upregulation of GLUT-4 transporters, activation and inhibition of enzymes 

-Long term: protein synthesis (metabolic enzymes), cell growth 

 

 

The cascade from the reception of insulin by RTK leads to the activation of glycogen synthase. Insulin also inhibits glycogen breakdown due to inactivation of glycogen phosphorylase. At the liver and in muscle, insulin stimulates protein synthesis and increased amino acid uptake through increased active transport.  

 

Insulin promotes lipogenesis by increasing lipoprotein lipase (LPL) production in adipose tissue capillaries, which breaks down triglycerides to form FFA which are taken up by the adipose tissue then recombined to form triglyceride stored in liquid droplets. Hormone-sensitive lipase (HSL) has the same function as LPL except it is located in adipocytes. Thus, insulin inhibits lipolysis by suppressing HSL activity. As a result of this, insulin also turns off ketone body formation in the liver.  

 

Insulin inhibits VLDL formation in the liver by inhibiting production of ApoB100.  

 

Reception of glucagon 

Glucagon is received by GPCRs only at the liver, activating cAMP signaling.  

 

At the brain 

Insulin and leptin can enter the brain through receptor mediated transport or across weak BBB areas. These hormones activate certain processes at the brain which cause the liver to decrease glycogenolysis and decrease gluconeogenesis.  

 

Describe the transmission of signals across membranes via second messengers, using the specific examples of insulin and glucagon 

Insulin 

Binding of insulin to RTK activates intrinsic tyrosine kinase activity of the receptor. This can cause the MAP kinase signaling pathway for cell growth, gene expression, or the PI-3K signaling pathway which increases the synthesis of lipids, proteins, glycogen, and upregulates GLUT-4 transporters.  

 

Glucagon 

Glucagon activates cAMP signaling first through a GPCR then through adenylate cyclase. In the liver, this signaling causes decreased glycogen synthase activity and increased glycogen phosphorylase activity. 

 

Describe the regulation of blood glucose levels, including the insulin and anti-insulin balance and feed forward loop regulation 

 

Insulin decreases glucose levels by promoting glucose uptake and inhibiting glucose release from liver.  

Glucagon (acts at liver only), adrenaline (acts at liver and muscle), cortisol and growth hormone increase blood glucose levels. This triggers the release of insulin, hence a loop.  

 

Describe the storage of excess energy (regulation, sites, and forms of storage) 

In a fasted state, the liver breaks down glycogen to form glucose. Liver makes new glucose from precursors: amino acids, glycerol and lactate using energy from FFA oxidation. Once glucose cannot be made, the liver makes ketone bodies.

Describe the functional organisation of an intestinal epithelial cell 

The intestinal lumen is folded with plicae, with villi to increase surface area to maximise the efficiency of nutrient, enzyme, hormone and waste exchange across the epithelium. Microvilli on enterocytes further increase this surface area. Tight junctions between enterocytes allow the flow of water through the paracellular pathway. 

 

Describe the sequential intraluminal digestion of ingested starch and proteins as they move along the GIT, and  

compare the membrane transport mechanisms responsible for uptake of sugars, amino acids, and di-peptides by intestinal epithelial cells 

Monosaccharides and disaccharides are transported to the small intestine for processing, where intestinal disaccharidases (a brush border enzyme) hydrolyse disaccharides into simple sugar units. The digestion of a polysaccharides such as starch begins at the mouth, where salivary amylase begins digesting it. Gastric acid inactivates the salivary amylase, but pancreatic amylase is able to continue the digestion of starch into short oligosaccharides. Disaccharidases hydrolyse these oligosaccharides into simple sugars. Note: glucose, galactose absorbed by Na+ symporter, fructose by diffusion. The secondary active transport symport action of Na+/glucose is driven by electrochemical gradient, from  efflux of Na+ from the basolateral side due to the Na+/K+ ATPase. The GLUT-2 transport protein allows facilitated diffusion of glucose into blood. 

 

Note: galactose and fructose are converted to glucose at the liver. 

 

Protein digestion begins at the stomach, where it is denatured by HCl and cleaved into smaller polypeptides by pepsin. Peptidases are released from the small intestine and pancreas. In the small intestine: Trypsin, chymotrypsin and elastase are endopeptidases which cleave bonds at the center of the polypeptide while exopeptidases like carboxypeptidase, aminopeptidase cleave bonds from the polypeptide's ends. Ultimately, peptides are broken down into amino acids. Amino acids are absorbed by the same mechanism as glucose: through secondary active transport, via Na+ symporter. Movement to blood is via facilitated diffusion through transporter. Peptides (abundant absorption of di- and tripeptides, virtually no absorption of peptides greater than 4 amino acids) are absorbed through secondary active transport via symport with H+ through PepT1 peptide carrier. Peptides are broken down to amino acids in the enterocyte via peptidases, then exported to blood via facilitated diffusion. 

 

Describe the mechanisms and molecules mediating the solubilisation and digestion of lipids within the GIT, and describe the uptake, processing and release of lipids by the small intestine epithelium 

Lipids are hydrophobic, whilst digestive enzymes are hydrophilic. This impedes the action of the digestive enzymes. Lingual lipase is secreted at the mouth, but there is limited action here, especially in the stomach after the mixing of lipase and water. Arrival of lipids in duodenum serves as a stimulus for bile secretion. Bile (made in liver from cholesterol) salts emulsify fats in the small intestine, allowing these enzymes to break down the lipids. The emulsifying action converts the large fat globule into small droplets that repel each other. As more fat is exposed to the enzymes, digestion is more efficient. These enzymes hydrolyse triglycerides into fatty acids and monoglycerides.  

 

Fatty acid + monoglyceride + bile salts = micelle 

 

Fatty acid and monoglyceride leave the micelle when it arrives at the mucosa, and they diffuse into the enterocyte. At the enterocyte, where they form triglyceride again and are combined with apolipoproteins, phospholipids and cholesterol to form a chylomicron. Chylomicrons are released into lacteals.  

 

The chylomicron is the biggest lipoprotein, lowest in density produced by the intestine.  

 

Compare the fate of lipid-soluble vs water-soluble molecules after absorption by the intestinal epithelium (ie handling by the epithelium) 

 

Lipid-soluble: packaged into chylomicrons with other lipids, undergoes exocytosis into lacteals, carried into lymph and into blood 

 

Water-soluble: facilitated diffusion into capillaries from basolateral end of enterocyte. Note enzymes eg peptidase which may continue to break a peptide into amino acids in the enterocyte.  

 

Describe the unique absorption strategies for key micronutrients: iron, calcium, B12 

Iron 

Dietary intake of non-heme (Fe3+) iron is reduced by Ferri-reductase protein to Fe2+. Fe2+ enters cell with H+ via DMT1 symport. Dietary heme iron (Fe2+ with porphyrin) is transported into the cell via a different channel, where heme oxygenase releases Fe2+ from the porphyrin. Fe2+ in the enterocyte is exported by Ferroportin channel, with some stored in ferritin protein (intracellular iron storage which releases iron when needed, later lost when intestinal mucosa is shed). Ferroxidase oxidises Fe2+ to Fe3+ for transport in blood by transferrin (each carrying 2 Fe3+ ions).  

 

Calcium 

TRPV5/6 channel allows Ca2+ into the cell (transcellular pathway). Calbindin transport protein carries Ca2+ to the basolateral side, where it is secreted into the capillaries via NCX exchanger and active transport Ca2+ extrusion pumps. Passive diffusion via the paracellular pathway is very limited. Active vitamin D (calcitriol) is very important, as it promotes the synthesis of genes at the nucleus which increases the expression of calcium influx proteins, carrier proteins, and export protein pumps.  

 

B12 

B12 binds to a glycoprotein: intrinsic factor, which is released by stomach parietal cells, forming a complex in the duodenum which resists digestion. However, the B12-intrinsic factor complex is recognised by surface receptors at the terminal ileum, and then absorbed by endocytosis. B12 is transported around the body by transcobalamin II, and can be delivered to cells or stored in the liver (3 years' storage supply). Note that B12 is synthesised by microorganisms in the colon but cannot be absorbed.  

 

Compare the absorption of fat soluble and water soluble vitamins 

Fat soluble:  

Vitamins A, D, E, K require bile salts and are carried by micelles. They diffuse into enterocytes and are packaged with other lipids into chylomicrons, then transported through lymph to blood. 

 

Water soluble: 

Vitamins B, C are absorbed in the upper small intestine through diffusion, then diffuse directly into the blood. Note B12 is water soluble, but binds to intrinsic factor.

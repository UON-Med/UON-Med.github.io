Describe biological oxidation, its purpose, and the major enzymes involved 

Oxidation = increase of a species/molecule's oxidation state, involving a removal of one or more electrons from it.  

 

Mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation during aerobic metabolism of oxygen produces reactive oxygen species such as the superoxide radical, hydroxyl radical, and peroxide.  

 

 

 

Other catalysts for the conversion of O2 to O2- include: 

-NADH/NADPH oxidase 

-Xanthine oxidase 

-Lipoxygenase 

-Cycloxygenase 

-CYP450 monoxygenase 

 

The superoxide anion then undergoes the following: 

2O2- + 2e- + 2H+ => 2H2O2 

 

Describe the role of iron in creating reactive oxygen species that damage organs 

 

H2O2 => OH. (hydroxyl radical) 

 

Iron Fe2+ is a catalyst for the above reaction (Fenton reaction). This is unfortunate as iron is essential for life and the Fenton reaction is unavoidable in an oxygen-rich environment. Iron-chelating drugs are used to reduce the impacts of iron overload and uncontrolled ROS production. 

 

 

 

 

Define free radical and describe the generation of superoxide free radicals 

Free radicals are unstable due to the presence of an unpaired electron in the outer shell. They are strong oxidants. When they oxidise another species, the other species will become a free radical. As such, a chain reaction begins. Superoxide free radicals are formed due to intrinsic bodily processes. 

 

Damage done 

Excess reactive oxygen species produced by the mitochondria will oxidise proteins, lipids and DNA to cause mitochondrial damage, ultimately leading to energy failure and cell death.  

 

Define antioxidant and list the important antioxidants in the body 

An antioxidant inhibits oxidation by reactive oxygen species, by stabilising free radicals before they attack cells. Antioxidants can donate an electron to an ROS without becoming unstable, thereby terminating potentially harmful chain reactions. 

 

Major antioxidants = vitamins A, C, E. Flavonoid is also an antioxidant. 

 

Note: antioxidant supplements have not been found to reduce disease. Antioxidant overdose eg in vitamin E can slow wound healing and decrease clotting time. 

 

Antioxidant mechanisms in the body 

Glutathione peroxidase is a major antioxidant in the body. Others include catalase and superoxide dismutase (SOD). Importantly however, these require cofactors eg Selenium, iron, copper, zinc, manganese 

 

 

Oxidative stress 

This results from an imbalance between levels of ROS and levels of antioxidants. Normally, cells balance the production of oxidants and antioxidants such that there is redox equilibrium. Oxidative stress results when there is excess ROS or a depletion in antioxidants. Oxidative stress is thought to play a key role in many diseases eg cancer, cardiovascular disease, Parkinson's disease, dementia, etc. 

 

Briefly describe the general mechanisms by which the liver can detoxify drugs and metabolic by-products 

The liver inactivates drugs by converting them to less noxious and water-soluble metabolites for excretion. Kupffer macrophage cells ingest bacteria and other small foreign material from blood.  

 

Note: the liver also removes waste eg excretion of bilirubin and cholesterol into bile 

 

There are two phases of detoxification. The first phase involves the breaking down of a toxin into a different form, so that it can then be conjugated in the second phase to promote excretion.  

 

Phase 1: CYP450 

The Cytochrome P450 enzyme superfamily contains the most diverse catalysts known, and are located primarily in the endoplasmic reticulum membrane. They contain heme groups and can catalyse reactions such as oxidation, reduction, hydrolysis, hydration, and dehalogenation. Note that there is a large variability in content and composition of P450 enzymes among individuals. Thus, there are large variations in drug metabolism by humans and can contribute to adverse drug reactions.  

 

Phase 2: Conjugation pathways 

Conjugation pathways include sulfation, glucuronidation, glutathione conjugation, acetylation, amino acid conjugation, and methylation. Conjugation involves the binding of the toxin with another substance to give in specific properties eg water solubility to increase ease of excretion.  

 

Eg: Detoxification of paracetamol 

Paracetamol does not require phase 1. Around 95% of paracetamol is conjugated by glucuronide or sulfates and then excreted in urine. However, the rest is metabolised by a CYP450 enzyme into the toxic NAPQI metabolite. NAPQI can be conjugated by glutathione and then excreted in urine, or otherwise cause hepatotoxicity. 

 

Note: Excessive activation of CYP enzymes can also result in the generation of ROS and toxic by-products.  

 

Describe the role of the liver in processing and synthesising amino acids 

Excess dietary proteins resulting in excess amino acids need to be removed. The first step in amino-acid catabolism is the removal of the amine group. This can occur either through deamination (removal of -NH2 group) or transamination (transfer of -NH2 group to a keto acid to form a new amino acid).  

 

Deamination (endothermic) converts an amino acid into ammonia + a keto acid, with NAD => NADH as the energy source.  

 

Transamination converts an amino acid to a new keto acid, while generating a new nonessential amino acid: 

 

 

Transamination requires transaminase catalysts and Vitamin B6 cofactor. Transaminases include aspartate AST and alanine ALT. 

 

Fate of the keto acid 

Keto acids are used as an energy source. Glucogenic amino acids are used to make glucose with the carbon skeleton converted to pyruvate or other intermediates of the Krebs cycle. Ketogenic amino acids are used to make ketone bodies with the carbon skeleton converted to acetyl-CoA or acetoacetyl-CoA. Some amino acids can undergo either pathway.  

 

Ammonia is converted to a less toxic form, urea, in the liver.  

 

Note: severe liver disease results in lower blood levels of vitamin K-dependent carboxylase, an enzyme required for the synthesis of proteins involved in hemostasis (blood clotting). This increases risk for uncontrolled hemorrhage. As such, vitamin K deficiency leads to defective blood clotting, whilst excess vitamin K leads to liver damage and anemia.  

 

Describe the structure of ammonia, its role in the body and consequences of elevated serum levels 

Ammonia is a waste product, and is toxic. Hyperammonemia can result from liver damage whereby ammonia is not converted to urea. Elevated blood serum levels causes lethargy, growth inhibition, seizures, shortness of breath, and many other symptoms.  

 

Describe the role of the liver in removal of excess ammonia from the body 

NH3 reacts with H2O to form NH4+. NH4+ + CO2 together enter the urea cycle at the mitochondria. Carbamoyl phosphate is formed, followed by citrulline. Citrulline moves to the cytosol, where it is converted to arginino-succinate, then arginine, then urea and ornithine.  

 

 

List the plasma proteins synthesised in the liver 

 

Most blood proteins except antibodies are synthesised and secreted by the liver. (Note that the liver also stores glycogen, triglycerides, lipid-soluble vitamins, B12, and metals.) 

 

-Albumin: accounts for 25% of all proteins made in liver, serum albumin is used as marker of liver damage 

-Hemostatic factors eg blood clotting factors, fibrinolysis factors 

-Carrier proteins 

-Hormones, pro-hormones, growth factors 

-Apolipoproteins 

-Enzymes 

-Bile proteins 

-Collagen (from stellate cells) 

 

Important functions of albumin 

-Transport function for lipophilic hormones and vitamins, fatty acids, bilirubin, ions, and exogenous drugs 

-Buffering 

-Anticoagulant by binding NO 

-Wound healing/inflammation: albumin carries essential proteins and denatures into amino acids used for repair 

-Antioxidant properties through radical scavenging and ligand binding 

-Detoxification by binding to toxic substances

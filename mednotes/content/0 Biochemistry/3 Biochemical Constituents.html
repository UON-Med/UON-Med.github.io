Describe the structure and functions of the normal biochemical constituents of the body. 

Carbohydrates: Biological molecules containing C,H,O, providing energy, supplying carbon for the synthesis of cell components, involved in cell structure and cell-cell communication. Longer carbohydrate chains are condensation polymers, with Dietary fibers the longest carbohydrate chains. Oligosaccharides are 3-10 monomers long, polysaccharides are >10 monomers long. 

 

-Soluble dietary fiber (pectin, guar gum, oat bran) is used for absorbing water, delaying the absorption of sugar and binding bile salts. 

-Insoluble dietary fiber (cellulose, lignin) decreases transit time. Resistant starch is insoluble but is fermented like soluble fiber. 

 

Proteins: Macromolecules which play a key role in constructing and maintaining living cells (Enzymes, many hormones, storage, transport, structure, protection of organs, contraction of muscles). A polypeptide is 50-100 amino acids linked; a protein is greater than 100 amino acids linked. (There are 20 amino acids from which all proteins are built) 

 

Lipids: Part of cell membranes, fat vacuoles, and used for hormones at the smooth ER. They are a storage form of energy, protect internal organs, are metabolic regulators, and conduct signal transduction. Phospholipids are made up of two fatty acid tails, whilst a glycolipid has two fatty acid tails with a sugar (carbohydrate) attached to the head. Cholesterol (a derived lipid) is a steroid (4-ring structure) found in animal foods only. Their functions include the management of plasma membrane fluidity, and are a precursor of bile acids, sex hormones, vitamin D.  

Note: fatty acids are a hydrocarbon chain with a carboxylic acid at one end, with chains of 14-20 atoms commonly seen. Unsaturated acids involve a double bond which is rigid and creates a kink in the chain. Fatty acids, when stored as an energy reserve, are attached to the glycerol by an ester linkage (hence "triglyceride"). Triglycerides can form large spherical fat droplets in the cell cytoplasm. 

 

 

 

Electrolytes: control fluid balance, which is important in muscle contraction, energy generation, and nearly all biochemical reactions. 

 

Metabolism 

There are two types of metabolism: catabolism and anabolism 

-Catabolism involves the breakdown of body compounds which releases energy 

-Anabolism involves the synthesis of body compounds, which requires energy 

Note: the liver processes fats, carbohydrates, proteins from diet; synthesizes and distributes lipids, ketone bodies, glucose for other tissues; converts excess nitrogen to urea 

 

Describe the structure of ATP and its role as energy currency in the cell: 

ATP is made of an adenine nitrogenous base, ribose, and a triphosphate group. ATP captures and stores energy in the bonds between phosphates. ATP is converted to ADP + Pi upon hydrolysis. Cells contain a small quantity of ATP that must be continually re-synthesized at the rate at which it is consumed. Uses of ATP include: the biosynthesis of macromolecules, muscle contraction, active molecule transport, and thermogenesis (body heating). 

 

A healthy diet 

A healthy diet includes macronutrients at the largest quantity, micronutrients at a small amount, and vitamins and minerals. Water-soluble vitamins are absorbed directly into the blood, are freely transported, are not stored, and are easily excreted in urine. Fat-soluble vitamins are absorbed with lipids, requiring a carrier protein. They are stored and not easily excreted, hence the risk of overdose.  

 

Roles of vitamins in the cell 

-Vitamins B1,2,3,5 are essential for releasing nutrient energy 

-B12 used for myelin sheath formation and are needed for red blood cells 

-Folate used for red blood cells and reducing neural-tube defects in babies 

-C is used for cell protection and wound-healing 

-A is used for vision 

-D is used for calcium absorption and bone metabolism 

-E is an antioxidant 

-K is a coagulant, helps in bone formation 

 

Minerals: 

Minerals are inorganic elements/compounds that are part of metabolic reactions or provide structural support in body tissues.  

 

Major minerals: 

-Na (mainly extracellular) maintains normal fluid and acid-base pressure, helps transmit nerve impulse, and assists with muscle contraction, excess causes hypertension, hypo causes nausea, muscle cramps, mental apathy 

-K (mainly intracellular) maintains normal fluid and acid-base pressure, helps transmit nerve impulse, important for heart /muscle contraction, and is important in energy metabolism. Excess causes chest pain, nausea, palpitations/skipped heartbeats, hypo causes hypertension 

 

Minor minerals:  

-Fe: Component of hemoglobin and myoglobin (in muscle cells) for oxygen transport, and is a component of enzymes involved in energy production. Excess causes hemochromatosis (fatigue, nausea, joint/abdominal pain, skin colour changes, liver and sexual dysfunction) 

 

-Mg: Used for bone mineralisation/calcium and vitamin C metabolism, relaxation of skeletal and smooth muscle, coordination of heart contractibility, energy production. Excess causes nausea, weakness, irregular heartbeat, low blood pressure, urine retention, respiratory distress. Too little causes muscle cramps, anxiety, growth retardation in kids, metabolic problems

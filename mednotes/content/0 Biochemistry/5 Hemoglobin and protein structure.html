Describe the levels of protein structure using hemoglobin as an example 

Primary structure- Sequence of amino acids in a polypeptide chain, determined by nucleotide sequence in the gene that encodes this protein 

Secondary structure- shape of the polypeptide chain, held in place by weak H-bonds between peptide bonds, alpha-helix or beta-pleated sheet 

Tertiary structure- 3D conformation forming distinct, independently folded regions called domains 

Quaternary structure (applies to proteins composed of more than one polypeptide chain)- Hemoglobin is a heterotetramer of 4 polypeptides (4-chain globin): 2 alpha chains and 2 beta chains.  

 

Protein denaturation is a process of modifying the structure of a protein. Denatured proteins lose their proper folding/3D structure, and thus their function. (Often irreversible)

Relate the structure of hemoglobin to its ability to function in the transport of O2 and CO2 

Hemoglobin can bind 4 oxygen molecules in a "cooperative" manner. The structure of hemoglobin (made of 4 heme groups, each made of a single porphyrin ring capable of binding to a metallic ion [Fe2+]) is such that it becomes easier and easier to bind oxygen as more oxygen is bound. Hemoglobin's affinity for oxygen increases after an oxygen molecule is bound. Hemoglobin can release oxygen very quickly.  

 

Compare and contrast the transport of CO2 and O2 in blood 

Myoglobin is a cytoplasmic hemoprotein located only in cardiac myocytes and oxidative skeletal muscle fibers. This is a single polypeptide, and contains one iron. Myoglobin has high affinity for O2 compared to hemoglobin, allowing it to act as O2 storage reserve in striatal muscle.  

 

CO2 can be transported in the blood as: 

-HCO3- 70% 

-Carbaminohemoglobin (protein-bound) 20% 

-Dissolved in plasma 10% 

 

O2 is transported in the blood as: 

-Oxyhemoglobin 97% 

-Dissolved in plasma 3% 

 

The Haldane effect 

The removal of O2 from hemoglobin increases its affinity for CO2. Thus, deoxygenated blood is higher in CO2 content than oxygenated blood.

Define cyanosis and briefly describe its biochemical and physiologic basis 

Cyanosis is caused by an increase in the concentration of deoxyhemoglobin. This can be caused by poisoning, most often by CO. Other poisons include methemoglobinemia and sulfhemoglobinemia.  

 

Carbon monoxide is very dangerous, and can be eliminated from the blood via treatment with high pO2 over time.  

Cyanosis is the bluish or purplish discoloration of the skin or mucous membranes due to the tissues near the skin surface having low oxygen saturation.  

 

Peripheral cyanosis refers to cyanosis impacting the limbs, extremities and fingers, and can be caused due to exposure to cold, shock, congestive heart failure, peripheral vascular disease, which all constrict the blood vessels. Low oxygen states can also cause cyanosis, eg pneumonia, CO poisoning, cardiac tamponade, emphysema, pulmonary embolism.  

 

Central cyanosis refers to discoloration of the mucous membranes of the lips and tongue. This can be caused by intracranial hemorrhage, pneumonia, pulmonary embolism, heart failure, etc.

Describe the basic mechanisms used by the body for communication and control of body systems and distinguish the general functional features of neural control (rapid responses) vs endocrine control (slower processes) 

 

Cells communicate with each other to maintain body homeostasis, and to bring about coordinated changes in body physiology. Communication is induced by extracellular signal molecules that can act locally, over short distances, or over long distances. Cells grow, divide, or die according to the signals they receive.  

 

Local communication and short-range: 

-Juxtacrine: membrane-bound signal molecule 

-Gap junctions: direct flow to neighbour (ions, small solutes) 

 

Through extracellular fluid: 

-Paracrine: secretory cell releases a signal molecule to the receptors on an adjacent target cell with local mediator 

-Autocrine: Target sites on same cell 

 

Across synaptic clefts: 

-Very fast but brief transmission 

-Effects limited to very specific area (postsynaptic receptor) 

 

Long distance communication: 

Through circulatory system 

-Endocrine 

-Neuroendocrine 

These systems communicate through hormones. A hormone is defined as: a "chemical messenger that is secreted in circulation by a cluster of specialised cells, transported in blood, acting on specific target cells where a response is stimulated".  

 

Define gland, and differentiate endocrine and exocrine 

Glands are body structures which produce and secrete chemicals into ducts or the blood, formed by folds of epithelial tissue. 

Endocrine glands secrete their hormonal products directly into the bloodstream (ductless glands) 

Exocrine glands secrete their products into ducts that carry these products to other organs or outside the body (sweat glands, gallbladder) 

Note: Pancreas is endocrine + exocrine 

 

Role of hypothalamus in homeostasis 

The hypothalamus is a major link between the nervous and endocrine systems, integrating signals for appropriate response. The hypothalamus secretes several hormones and controls glandular secretions, helping regulate heart rate, metabolic rate, water/solute balance.  

-Hypothalamus secretes regulatory hormones through portal vein to the anterior pituitary lobe, controlling its activity. Hormones then secreted by the anterior pituitary control other endocrine organs.  

-Hypothalamus directly produces ADH and Oxytocin through posterior pituitary lobe.  

-Control of sympathetic output to adrenal medullae, resultantly controlling the secretion of epinephrine and norepinephrine. 

 

Neurohypophysis: hormones synthesised in neurosecretory cell bodies (ADH and Oxytocin in different nuclei) within the hypothalamus travel down the axons to be stored in secretory vesicles in the neuronal terminals of the posterior pituitary. These are released into the bloodstream upon excitation of hypothalamic neurons 

 

Adenohypophysis: activated hypothalamic neurons release hormones into the hypophyseal portal system. These either inhibit or promote the release of hormones synthesised by secretory cells in the anterior pituitary. Pituitary hormones then promote the release of specific hormones in "subordinate" endocrine glands/end organs.  

 

Briefly describe feedback mechanisms in hormonal control pathways 

Negative feedback mechanisms regulate the blood levels of most hormones.  

-Critical role of hypothalamus-pituitary axis: RH (Regulatory hormone) secreted into anterior pituitary, anterior pituitary secretes Hormone 1 into target organ, target organ secretes Hormone 2 to inhibit secretion of hormone from the hypothalamus and pituitary.  

-Thyroid hormones: Hypothalamus releases TRH into anterior pituitary, which releases TSH (trophic hormone) into the thyroid. The thyroid then produces lipid-soluble hormones (T4 Thyroxine, T3) to the target cells and target genes. An increase in basal metabolic rate (protein, fat, carbohydrate metabolism) occurs, along with growth control. This is involved in the maturation of the CNS. 

-HGH: Hypothalamus secretes mainly growth hormone-releasing hormone (GHRH), and very little somatostatin (GHIH). The anterior pituitary then synthesises and releases water soluble growth hormone, with direct effects on many parts of the body (notably increased cartilage formation and skeletal growth, and increased protein synthesis, cell growth and proliferation). Increased lipolysis and blood sugar (anti-insulin effects) also occur.  

 

Positive feedback is much rarer in the control of hormones. 

-Childbirth: Oxytocin stimulates and enhances labor contractions.  

 

Briefly name and describe the role of key hormones involved in growth: 

-Sex hormones: Lipid-soluble steroid hormones which stimulate the growth of the penis and testes (Androgens like testosterone) or the uterus and vagina (Progesterone and Estrogens like Estradiol). They are responsible for secondary sex characteristics, and either spermatogenesis or ovulation.  

-Human growth hormone: Increases cartilage formation and skeletal growth, increases protein synthesis and cell growth and proliferation, increases lipolysis, increases blood sugar and anti-insulin effects 

-Insulin: a water soluble hormone involved in blood glucose homeostasis, whose role is to reduce blood sugar levels by signaling the liver, muscle and fat cells to take in glucose from the blood. It stimulates cell growth, proliferation and gene expression through one signaling pathway; and the synthesis of lipids, proteins, glycogen or for cell survival through a second signaling pathway.  

-Thyroid hormone: Lipid soluble hormones which increase basal metabolic rate to control the growth (with GH) of multiple targets, to control protein, fat, and carbohydrate metabolism, and is involved in the maturation of the CNS.  

 

Briefly describe the basic strategies for getting hormonal signals across the cell membrane and distinguish hormones that act at the cell surface vs hormones that are able to enter the cell: 

 

Cellular responses to hormones are mediated by receptors that initiate a complex array of cellular events upon ligand binding. 

-All water-soluble hormones such as peptide hormones and amino-acid derivatives (except thyroid hormones) bind to cell-surface receptors.  

-Most lipid-soluble hormones (thyroid and steroid hormones) interact with intracellular receptors. 

-Some lipid-soluble hormones like Eicosanoids and some steroids stimulate extracellular receptors. 

 

Extracellular receptors 

Hormone-mediated activation of an extracellular receptor leads to the activation of intracellular signaling cascades resulting in fast effects and rapid changes in cell behaviour (the non-genomic response). However, the genomic response involving the cell nucleus and long-term effects is more common, with the activation of signaling cascades that regulate gene transcription/protein synthesis hence the slow changes in cell behaviour. There are 3 main receptor classes with the specificity of the extracellular receptor influencing the specificity of the intracellular signal.  

-G protein- coupled receptors amplify signals 

-Enzyme-linked receptors lead to a diverse range of effects 

-Ion channel- linked receptors alter the electrical potential across the cell membrane 

 

Intracellular receptors 

A free hormone diffuses into a cell, and a hormone-receptor complex often translocates to the nucleus. The nuclear hormone-receptor complex affects gene transcription, changing the synthesis of specific proteins. This exerts slow, long-term physiological changes.  

 

Secretion 

Water-soluble hormones are pre-made and packaged into vesicles that are stored in the cytoplasm until they are released by exocytosis in response to a stimulus. (This is regulated secretion.) Water-soluble hormones are free to travel in the blood once released. 

 

Lipid-soluble hormones are synthesised anew in response to a stimulus and then slowly released by diffusion across the plasma membrane. There is no intracellular storage and no specific release mechanism. The majority of these hormones are attached reversibly to carrier proteins, with a tiny amount biologically active (free) in blood. There is a dynamic equilibrium between the bound, inactive hormones and the free, active hormones. Carrier proteins delay metabolism to protect the hormones from enzyme degradation and kidney filtration, providing a reservoir of hormones and facilitating their distribution throughout the vascular system. Most of these carriers are hormone-specific, but some are bound to the non-selective plasma albumin transporter.  

 

Classes of hormones 

Amino acid derivatives: small molecules structurally related to individual amino acids eg Thyroid hormone from tyrosine 

Peptide/polypeptide/protein hormones: chains of amino acids including glycoproteins and polypeptides (<200 amino acids) eg insulin, GH 

Lipid derivatives: steroid hormones structurally related to cholesterol eg sex hormones, or eicosanoids derived from arachidonic acid

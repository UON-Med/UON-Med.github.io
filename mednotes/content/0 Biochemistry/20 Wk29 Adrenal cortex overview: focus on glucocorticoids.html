List the three main classes of adrenal steroids and identify where in the adrenal gland they are produced 

The adrenal gland from outermost to innermost: 

Glomerulosa- mineralocorticoids: mainly aldosterone targeting kidneys 

Fasciculata- glucocorticoids: mainly cortisol (hydrocortisone) and corticosterone causing amino acid release from skeletal muscle, lipid release from adipose, glycogen and glucose synthesis at liver, anti-inflammatory 

Reticularis- androgens: testosterone etc, regulating male characteristics 

Medulla- catecholamines 

 

Glucocorticoids 

The main functions of glucocorticoids: 

-Mobilisation of energy reserves, eg release of lipids from adipose, amino acids from skeletal muscle 

-Conservation of glucose, with lipid breakdown a source of energy 

-Elevation of blood glucose concentration through gluconeogenesis 

 

Draw simple pathways identifying the main intermediates in the synthesis of adrenal steroids 

 

 

The conversion of cholesterol to pregnenolone is the rate limiting step. ACTH increases cholesterol uptake (immediate), leads to an increased expression of P450 enzymes for conversion of cholesterol to pregnenolone (long term), and leads to trophic action (increased gland volume).  

 

Identify the main glucocorticoid in humans and describe its principle actions 

Cortisol possesses anti-insulin effects, and increases gluconeogenesis in the liver (primarily from amino acids). It also stimulates liver glycogen synthesis. It promotes protein catabolism in muscle, with the efflux of glucogenic amino acids used in the liver for gluconeogenesis.  

 

High cortisol leads to preferential deposition of fats as visceral fat, whilst peripheral subcutaneous depots get depleted. It also has an immunosuppressive effect to reduce inflammation. High cortisol leads to the development of insulin resistance and type 2 diabetes.  

 

Describe the two main classes of corticosteroid receptors and state how they differ in their distribution, in their steroid specificity and in their affinity for cortisol and aldosterone 

Type I: mineralocorticoid receptor- roughly equal affinity for mineralocorticoids and glucocorticoids. These are expressed in many tissues eg kidney, colon, heart, brown adipose tissue, sweat glands etc.  

Type 2: glucocorticoid receptor- higher affinity for glucocorticoids, expressed in nearly all cells 

 

Describe the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis and summarise the mechanisms which control the secretion of the three main classes of adrenal steroids 

Corticotropin releasing hormone (CRH) is released from the hypothalamus in response to stress. CRH binds to the anterior pituitary through GPCR causing synthesis and release of ACTH. ACTH then signals the adrenal fasciculata to release glucocorticoids. Cortisol levels in the blood are detected by the hypothalamus and adenohypophysis to inhibit further release.  

 

Pulsatile ACTH and hence cortisol release occurs throughout the day, with peaks in the morning and evening.  

 

Describe the consequences of inadequate cortisol production 

Cushing's disease (pituitary tumour) or syndrome: high cortisol 

Addison's disease (adrenal damage) or syndrome: adrenal insufficiency

Briefly describe the biochemical changes that can occur as a result of respiratory impairment (hypercarbia, respiratory acidosis, hypoxia, lactic acidosis) 

Hypercarbia (hypercapnia) is defined as an abnormally high level of CO2 (ie PaCO2 > 45mmHg) in arterial blood. This occurs when the ventilation of CO2 is inadequate and CO2 is retained in the lungs (due to airway obstruction, respiratory depression, etc). This leads to respiratory acidosis, where symptoms include rapid breathing, dyspnea, increased HR and BP, dizziness, flushed skin, confusion etc. Hypercarbia leads to a decrease in blood pH, below 7.3 (normal blood pH is tightly regulated between 7.35 and 7.45).  

 

Immediate response: intracellular buffers, with proteins (especially Hb in RBCs) and phosphates playing a big role. The bicarbonate system does not act intracellularly. Delayed response: kidney compensation 

 

Hypoxemia is defined as an abnormally low level of O2 (ie PaO2 <60mmHg) on 60% O2. Hypoxemia refers to low O2 in blood, whilst hypoxia refers to low O2 at the tissue level. This leads to anaerobic metabolism and lactic acidosis.  

 

Briefly describe in general the potential consequences of altered pH on cellular function 

PH is important for protein integrity (structure, enzyme activity, binding), maintains membrane gradients (action potentials in neurons), are needed to produce the H+ ion gradient to generate ATP in mitochondria, and affects O2 delivery to tissue (hemoglobin has a greater affinity for O2 at higher pH).mamac 

 

How can pH change? 

PH is decreased by inhaled CO2, metabolism, fecal bicarbonate loss, etc. An increase in pH can be caused by respiratory removal of CO2 or kidney removal of H+.  

 

Briefly describe the biochemical mechanisms involved in the maintenance of blood pH, including the role of the respiratory system, red blood cells and hemoglobin, serum proteins and very briefly, the role of kidneys and the gut 

 

3 major mechanisms of acid-base balance: 

1, Chemical buffer systems (immediate response)- first line of defense taking less than one second. This involves buffering by bicarbonate/phosphate/protein systems. These temporarily limit to fall in pH. 

2, Respiratory regulation (within minutes)- regulates removal of CO2 from blood by controlling release of CO2 from lungs, involves CO2 transport and hemoglobin buffering. Plasma pH are returned toward normal values but acid-base status cannot be corrected completely 

3, Renal mechanisms (several hours to days)- only kidneys can rid the body of metabolic acids through excretion, involving the renal secretion of H+ and reabsorption and production of HCO3-. Renal function plays an important role in conserving and renewing HCO3-. Acid-base status can be corrected completely through excretion of excess acids/bases in urine.  

 

Respiratory system 

A mechanism the body uses to control blood pH involves the controlled release of CO2 from the lungs. This involves the CO2 transport and bicarbonate and hemoglobin buffer systems. By adjusting the speed and depth of breathing, the respiratory system regulates the blood pH minute by minute. Respiratory problems can cause acid-base imbalance, ie respiratory acidosis and alkalosis. Respiratory compensation to low pH increases the rate and depth of breathing to eliminate (expel) CO2 from the lungs, and introduce O2 into the blood. Respiratory compensation to high pH involves slower and more shallow pulmonary respiration, in order to accumulate more CO2 in the blood.  

 

The 3 main physiological buffers are: 

-Carbonic acid - bicarbonate buffer system 

A weak acid (carbonic acid) and an excess of its conjugate base (HCO3- stored as NaHCO3) are present in blood and extracellular fluid, producing a buffer system. Carbonic acid anhydrase (CA) present in red blood cells catalyses the rapid interconversion of CO2 to carbonic acid. This reaction occurs slowly if CA were absent.

-Protein and amino acid buffer systems 

These are important in blood and intracellular fluid, and interact with other buffer systems. Amino acids exist as zwitterions (dipolar ions) at around pH 7. Because of their -COOH and -NH2 chemical groups, they are amphoteric, acting as both proton donors and acceptors. Proteins, composed of amino acids act as buffers. Protein buffer -COOH and -NH2 groups are used, and when applicable, side chains are used for buffering (eg basic and acidic side group chains).  

 

Protein buffers are slower than other chemical buffers. 

 

Hemoglobin buffer (RBC only): 

This is a very important buffer in blood, occurring only intracellularly in the red blood cells. These help prevent changes in pH when there are changes in pCO2. 

 

Hb + H+ <--> HHb (deoxyhemoglobin) 

Hb + O2 <--> HbO2 (oxyhemoglobin) 

Hb + CO2 <--> HbCO2 (carbaminohemoglobin) 

 

CO2 diffuses into blood plasma then into RBC. Carbonic anhydrase is used to break down carbonic acid into H+ and HCO3-. A bicarbonate/chloride anion antiporter allows bicarbonate out of the cell and chloride into the cell, leading to a chloride shift to maintain the balance of anions and cations. Hb binds to the H+ to reduce blood acidity. Hb is also attracted to CO2 to reduce its concentration in the cell.  

 

Hemoglobin buffering capacity is decreased by O2 binding. Deoxyhemoglobin is a better buffer than oxyhemoglobin. The buffering action of Hb allows large quantities of carbonic acid to be carried in blood without major changes in blood pH.  

 

Predict the effect of changes in CO2 concentration on serum pH 

An increase in concentration of CO2 decreases serum pH, whilst a decrease in concentration of CO2 increases serum pH.

Describe the determinants and normal regulation of metabolic rate 

Thyroid hormones regulate metabolic rate. Thyroid hormone secretion is controlled by the pituitary, which is controlled by the hypothalamus. Catecholamines and cortisol also increase metabolic rate.  

 

Insulin decreases metabolic rate. 

 

Describe the determinants and normal regulation of body temperature 

Temperature is detected by thermosensitive ion channels externally on the skin, internally in the abdominal and thoracic cavity, and at the dorsal root ganglion. These channels cause firing at different frequencies depending on the temperature. (Higher the temperature, higher the frequency). The hypothalamus uses this information to regulate thyroid function, thus regulating metabolism and temperature. 

 

Describe the actions of thyroid hormones 

Physiologic effects of T3 

-Essential for normal brain development 

-Initiates cell growth and differentiation 

-Increases basal metabolic rate and thus widespread effects in peripheral tissue, including increased rates of oxygen and energy consumption, increased heat production, heart rate, contractility, sensitivity of sympathetic stimulation through receptor upregulation, and accelerates mineral turnover in bone 

 

Too much T3 can cause osteoporosis in older patients. 

 

Describe the metabolic consequences of thyroid hormone excess or deficiency 

Hypothyroidism 

This has a primary cause 90% of the time (iodine insufficiency, autoimmune, surgery, drugs, genetics), but may also have a secondary (~10%) cause from pituitary failure, or a rare tertiary cause from hypothalamus failure.  

 

Primary hypothyroidism: elevated TSH, low free T4 

Subclinical hypothyroidism: elevated TSH, normal free T4 (before clinical presentation of low free T4) 

Secondary hypothyroidism: normal/low TSH, low free T4 

 

Hypothyroidism causes a decreased metabolic rate, weight gain, bradycardia, fatigue, cold intolerance, constipation.  

 

Hyperthyroidism 

Again, this also has a primary cause 90% of the time, eg Grave's disease (autoimmune), too much iodine, synthetic T4 overdose, cancer, or thyroiditis. A secondary cause may be a pituitary adenoma.  

 

Hyperthyroidism results in decreased TSH levels and elevated free T4 levels in blood tests. 

 

Hyperthyroidism results in an increased metabolic rate, weight loss, heart palpitations/heart failure due to high output, muscle weakness, heat intolerance, fatigue, diarrhea. 

 

Describe the relationship between thyroid hormone and adrenaline 

In humans, adrenaline is around 80% of the body's catecholamine output. It increases cardiac activity, blood pressure, glycogenolysis, blood glucose, and release of lipids from adipose tissue. It is water soluble, and is synthesised from tyrosine taken up by chromaffin cells in the adrenal medulla. Adrenaline is stored in granules and released by exocytosis in response to stimulation. Secretion of adrenaline is stimulated by many stresses eg hypoglycemia, trauma, exercise, emotions, etc.  

 

Thyroid hormones and adrenaline result in similar physiologic changes for the body. An excess of adrenaline production caused by a tumor (pheochromocytoma) leads to severe hypertension, tachycardia, palpitations, excess sweating, and heat intolerance- all symptoms similar to hyperthyroidism. 

 

Metabolism during stress 

Catabolic actions occur during stress, similar to in a fasting state. In stress, adrenaline antagonises insulin to increase plasma glucose.  

 

Compare the fasting state with the hyperthyroid state 

In the fasting state, most glucose is taken up by the brain and RBCs. Glycogen stored in the liver is the source of glucose for all other organs during periods of fasting. However, liver glycogen reserve is only sufficient for a few hours. Once this is depleted, gluconeogenesis is stimulated at the liver by glucagon and lactate + amino acids (both from muscle) + glycerol (from adipose tissue) are used. The energy for this process is supplied by beta-oxidation of free fatty acids from triglyceride breakdown. Lactate and amino acids are also released from the muscle, with lactate entering to Cori cycle. Intermediates are converted to oxaloacetate (main precursor for gluconeogenesis). Alanine is a major amino acid released from muscle.  

 

Adrenaline and glucagon both activate hormone-sensitive lipase (HSL) in adipocytes to cause lipolysis, with glycerol used for glucose production at liver and fatty acid used for energy production. 

 

When there is a lack of oxaloacetate due to gluconeogenesis, acetyl-CoA made from FFA can't react with oxaloacetate to produce citrate and accumulates. This is transformed into ketone bodies. Ketone bodies are released into the blood and used as fuel.  

 

Hyperthyroid state- same as above, but with bad consequences 

In the hyperthyroid state, there is excessive glycogenolysis, lipolysis, proteolysis at muscle, and gluconeogenesis. This causes excessive weight loss and leads to loss of muscle function. Hyperglycemia leads to other pathologies.

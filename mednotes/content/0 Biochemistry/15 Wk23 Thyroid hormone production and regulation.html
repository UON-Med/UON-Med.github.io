Describe the dietary sources of iodine and its absorption, distribution and excretion 

-Seafood 

-Fortified food 

 

Iodine is absorbed in the small intestine and enters blood plasma as iodide. It is then collected at thyroid follicle cells where it is stored (80% [15mg] stored here, enough for around 100 days).  

 

When thyroid hormones are metabolised, iodine is released and can be then recycled from blood as iodide. However, some of it will be excreted through the kidneys.  

 

Describe the biosynthesis, storage and secretion of tri-iodothyronine (T3) and thyroxin (T4)  

The follicle cells of the thyroid gland produce and store T3 and T4 hormones. The follicles trap circulating iodine. They also synthesise thyroglobulin (Tg), which is then stored in a colloid reservoir. Tg is a precursor of T3 and T4. 

 

1,Trapping of iodine 

Transport occurs via sodium-iodide symporter into the cytoplasm of follicular cells, then to sites of hormone synthesis. TSH from the adenohypophysis stimulates this active transport, and is the rate-limiting step in thyroid hormone synthesis.  

 

2, Iodination of thyroglobulin 

Thyroid peroxidase (TPO) oxidises iodide, and transports it to follicular cell-colloid interface. Iodine is incorporated by TPO into selected thyroglobulin and tyrosine residues to form diiodotyrosine (DIT) or monoiodotyrosine (MIT).  

 

3, Synthesis 

TPO-catalysed coupling of two iodotyrosine residues occurs. Coupling of 2 DIT makes tetraiodotyrosine (T4 precursor), or coupling of DIT with MIT makes triiodotyrosine (T3 precursor). T3 and T4 are synthesised and stored within thyroglobulin in colloid.  

 

Note: iodine is very rare in diet and thus this trapping and concentrating process is critical. Abnormalities can lead to thyroid disease, eg deficiency in iodine or TPO (due to anti-TPO antibodies), or due to other genetic mutations inactivating processes or producing antibodies targeting the symporter gene.  

 

The sodium-iodide symporter cannot distinguish normal iodide from radioactive iodide, thus allowing diagnosis and treatment.  

 

4, Release 

T4/T3 are embedded within Tg and need to be released. Following TSH stimulation, pinocytosis of colloid droplets occurs, which fuse with lysosomes. Digestion of Tg releases T3/T4 and allows passage into capillary. Some T4 is deiodinated in this process. Tg is released into the blood as a by-product, and levels of Tg correlate with thyroid mass/TSH stimulation. Blood Tg test used to monitor certain thyroid cancers. 

 

 

 

Activation of TSH GPCRs on thyroid follicle cells activates cAMP signaling to alter gene expression.  

 

Note: In euthyroid (normal) state, thyroid gland secretes 100% of body's T4 but only 10-20% of body's T3. T4 is the primary secretory product of thyroid that is in large excess over T3. Most of the body's T3 is derived from deiodination of T4 in other body tissues (mainly by deiodinase enzymes at liver, peripheral tissues can also regulate local T3 levels by synthesis from T4). T4 also has a longer half-life, so concentration of circulating T4 always much higher than that of T3.  

 

Describe the transport of water insoluble hormones and the effects of binding proteins on availability and delivery to target receptors 

Water insoluble hormones are bound to carrier proteins in blood. For example, >99% of circulating T4 and T3 are bound. 75% bound to thyroxine-binding globulin (TBG) with high affinity, 25% bound to albumin and thyroxine-binding prealbumine (TBPA/transthyretin).  

 

Only a tiny fraction of total T4/T3 is free. Free hormones are able to enter cells and are metabolically active. Although those bound to a carrier are metabolically inactive, the carrier facilitates their distribution throughout the body and delays hormone metabolism such that a reservoir of hormones is produced.  

 

Describe the regulation of thyroid hormone production 

Thyroid hormone production is regulated by TSH which increases the activity of active transport mechanisms causing iodide trapping. A deficiency in iodide or TPO can however lead to hypothyroidism.  

 

Total thyroid hormone concentration in serum is normally kept at a level proportional to the concentration of carrier proteins, appropriate to maintain a constant free hormone level. (Not only thyroid hormones, most protein carrier-dependent hormones act similarly as opposed to being dependent on changes in affinity.) Thyroid hormone concentration in plasma can also be affected by drugs, diseases, etc. 

 

Note: variations in TBG and/or albumin can alter thyroid function tests. TBG can alter total T4/T3 whilst albumin can interfere with T4 immunoassays.  

 

 

Metabolism of T4/T3 

-T4 converted by monodeiodination to either active T3 or inactive rT3 (rT3 can balance T3 to change rate of metabolism) 

-T3 and rT3 metabolised to inactive T2 

-Some T4/T3 eliminated via liver conjugation (21%) 

 

Note: all deiodination is catalysed by tissue-specific selenoenzymes, with the released iodine recycled. 

 

Thyroid function test 

TSH + free T4 (FT4) is taken. An inverse relationship between TSH and thyroid hormone levels suggest normal thyroid function. When developing a thyroid problem, TSH is the first abnormality before changes in T4.  

 

Compare the mechanisms of feedback and communication specific to long-term acting hormones like thyroxin and compare to short-acting hormones like insulin 

 

Long term 

Many long-term hormones are lipid soluble and bind to carrier proteins. This results in only a small amount being metabolically active and able to enter cells at any one time. However, the carrier proteins create a reservoir of hormones due to an extended half-life, and thus sustained but slow actions result. Feedback is also slower often due to a longer chain of feedback events.  

 

Short term 

Short term hormones are often water soluble eg insulin, and a large amount can be metabolically available at once. Large fluctuations in insulin levels can result due to the fast nature of feedback. A decrease in blood glucose will quickly decrease insulin levels due to a cease in secretion and their short half-life. However, an increase in blood glucose from a dietary source can rapidly spike insulin release from pancreatic beta cells.

Planes 

-Median: straight down middle, dividing left and right 

-Frontal/coronal plane: divides anterior and posterior 

-Sagittal planes: parallel to median plane  

-Transverse plane: divides superior and inferior 

 

Anatomical relationships 

Anterior (Ventral): Nearer to front 

Posterior (Dorsal): Nearer to back 

Proximal: Nearer to trunk of limb 

Distal: Further from trunk of limb 

Superior (cranial): Nearer to head 

Inferior (caudal): Nearer to feet 

Palmar/dorsal: palm and back of hand 

Plantar/dorsal: bottom of feet and top of feet 

 

Appendicular: limbs and girdles 

Axial: Skull, vertebrae, ribs, sternum 

 

Identifying bones 

Short bone- carpals, tarsals 

Sesamoid- patella, in distal portions of the first metacarpal bone and at the connection between the big toe and the first metatarsal bone 

Flat bone- thin with broad surfaces, often protect organs eg scapula, ribs, sternum, clavicle 

Long bone- femur, humerus, radius, ulna etc 

 

View, name and identify bony features 

-Glenohumeral joint: shoulder ball-and-socket joint 

-Medial and lateral epicondyles of the humerus: rounded projections at the end of the humerus, at the elbow joint, serving as an attachment point for the upper and lower arm muscles 

-Radius and ulna: radius attached to thumb, ulna rotates about the radius 

-Tibia and fibula: tibia is the big shin bone and the fibula hides behind it 

-Knee joint: Femur connected to tibia via anterior cruciate ligament and medial meniscus, whilst connected to fibula via lateral collateral ligament 

-Talocrural joint: ankle joint 

-Spine of scapula: diagonal ridge across the flat plane 

-Foramen magnum: hole in base of skull through which the spinal cord passes 

 

Movements 

-Abduction: rise of shoulders laterally, adduction is lowering 

-Flexion: decrease of angle between two body parts, extension is increase in angle (sagittal plane) 

-Medial/lateral rotation: rotation in or outward 

-Dorsiflexion/plantarflexion: as per the foot (plantar is down) 

-Inversion/eversion: lateral and medial rotation of the foot at the ankle, respectively 

-Circumduction: conical movement of a limb from joint 

 

 

Function of bone 

Consists of: 

-Dense connective tissue (tendons and ligaments) 

-Epithelium (outside layer of bone) 

-Osseous tissue (bone tissue) 

-Adipose tissue 

-Neural tissue 

Functions: 

-Mechanical 

-Mineral homeostasis (calcium and phosphorous reservoir) 

-Haemopoiesis (production of red and white blood cells and platelets) 

-Triglyceride storage 

 

Macroscopic features of bone 

-Compact (cortical) bone for weight-bearing 

-Spongy (cancellous) bone 

-Articular (relating to joint) cartilage 

-Periosteum (outer layer except for articular cartilage), has nociceptive (pain) nerve endings 

 

Red bone marrow: creates blood cells, exists predominantly in spongy tissue on the outside 

Yellow bone marrow: storage for Ca, P and triglycerides, found predominantly in central marrow cavity 

 

Bone Formation in embryonic development 

Intramembranous ossification 

-Development of flat bones from mesenchyme embryonic tissue (stem cells in mesenchyme differentiate into osteoblasts) 

-Osteoblasts secrete protein/fibers that make up the bony matrix (osteoid) 

-Osteoid combines with Ca to form calcified bone 

-Calcified bone engulfs further osteoblasts to turn them into osteocytes 

-Osteoid is laid randomly around blood vessels, with pores formed where the blood vessels are located hence spongy bone (with trabeculae formed around the vessels like little beams) 

-Blood vessels outside spongy bone condenses into periosteum 

-Trabeculae thickens, osteoblast continues to lay down osteoid, condensing into compact bone around the spongy bone, with red bone marrow appearing where the blood vessels were in the spongy spaces 

 

 

 

Endochondral ossification  

-Embryonic development of long bones 

-Skeletal framework made of hyaline cartilage 

-Outer layer of cartilage, the perichondrium, is vascularized (penetrated by tiny blood vessels delivering nutrients which stimulate mesenchymal stem cells to differentiate into osteoblasts which deposit osteoid) 

-Osteoblasts gather at diophysis wall to form bone collar (periosteum) 

-Centre of all this is the "Primary Ossification Centre" 

-Formation of bone collar causes chondrocytes (cells responsible for building initial hyaline cartilage) remaining in the bone cavity to enlarge and send a signal causing surrounding cartilage to calcify. This causes an impermeability to inner portion of developing bone, causing nutrients to be blocked off and hence cell death 

-Vascular invasion: eventually vessels in the periosteum pass through the bony collar and invade the inner cavity, with the hole that the vessels poke through called the "nutrient foramen". Many other components enter through this nutrient foramen, such as nerves, lymphatics, osteoclasts, osteoblasts, nutrients etc. The remaining cartilage is broken down by osteoclasts and the osteoblast secretes trabeculae (which form spongy bone) 

-The diaphysis begins to elongate as the bone shaft continues to be invaded. Vessels bud into the hyaline cartilage at the ends of the long bones to form secondary ossification centers. 

-Epiphyseal ossification: Vascular invasion occurs again except spongy bone is formed and hyaline cartilage is left at ends of the bones (articular cartilage) and epiphyseal (growth) plates are formed.  

 

Widening: 

Osteoblasts in the periosteum builds circumferential rings of hard, calcified compact bone while osteoclasts destroy the lining of the cavity, allowing the cavity to grow

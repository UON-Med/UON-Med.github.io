Laboratory medicine

Abstract

Laboratory medicine involves the analysis and evaluation of body fluids such as blood, urine, or CSF, the results of which are important for the prevention, diagnosis, and staging of diseases. Laboratory medicine plays an important role in daily clinical practice; however, the evaluation of results should always take into account the patient's medical history, as well as clinical and diagnostic findings. In addition to the basics of laboratory medicine, this learning card covers important laboratory parameters such as liver function tests and iron metabolism. Further parameters of clinical relevance may be found in other learning cards and are listed in the section “Overview of important laboratory values”. Current laboratory reference values can be found under “Tips and links” below.

Basics of laboratory analysis

Pre-analytical phase

The pre-analytical phase encompasses the selection of relevant diagnostic tests, as well as the collection and transport of samples.

  • Specificity and sensitivity are important when selecting an appropriate diagnostic test.
  • The most common materials used for analysis are:
    • Blood
    • Urine (see diagnostic evaluation of the kidney and urinary tract)
    • Cerebrospinal fluid (see lumbar puncture, differential diagnosis of meningitis)
    • Fluid collection from puncture (e.g., ascites)
  • Interference factors → influence sample quality
    • An error during sample collection: e.g., prolonged venous stasis during blood collection
    • An error during sample transport, which might include:
      • Exposure to strong light
      • Shaking of blood samples

Errors that strongly affect results frequently occur in the pre-analytical phase!

Analytical phase

The analytical phase comprises of sample processing and the release of results.

  • Qualitative methods: With qualitative methods, the only conclusion that can be made is if the result is positive or negative.
    • Classical example: pregnancy test, urine test strip
  • Quantitative methods: With quantitative methods, a statement can be made based on the concentration detected in the sample.
    • Immunochemistry: e.g., ELISA for the detection of prostate-specific antigen
    • Photometry: e.g., indirect determination of the concentration of glucose
    • Microscopy: e.g., manual differential blood cell count

Post-analytical phase

The post-analytical phase consists of diagnosis and evaluation of the results. The most important points include:

  • Correct allocation of the result to the patient
  • Plausibility check
  • Conveyance of critical results
  • Finally, medical interpretation using a combination of all results

Results from laboratory testing are not to be considered individually, but always in association with medical history and clinical examination!

Liver function tests

  • Liver function tests can be divided into three categories
    • Parameters of hepatocellular damage
    • Parameters of cholestasis
    • Parameters of hepatic synthesis

Parameters of hepatocellular damage

  • Relevance
    • Damage to hepatocytes results in the release of various enzymes that are detectable in blood and provide some indication of the severity of hepatic cell damage.
Laboratory parameter Physiological function Characteristics Frequent causes of elevation

Transaminases

Alanine aminotransferase (ALT)

  • Enzyme involved in gluconeogenesis and the generation of urea
  • Organ-specific
    • ALT is specific to hepatic cells.
    • AST is present in the liver, heart, muscle, and erythrocytes.
  • Subcellular localization
    • ALT and AST are both present in the hepatocyte cytoplasm.
    • AST is also present in the mitochondria.
    • AST/ALT ratio
      • The AST/ALT ratio can be used to estimate whether hepatic damage is mild or severe (mitochondrial involvement). A ratio > 1 most likely indicates severe damage (see causes below).
  • AST and ALT: in all types of hepatocyte damage!
  • Only AST
    • Muscle damage
    • Myocardial infarction
Aspartate aminotransferase (AST)
  • Enzyme involved in amino acid metabolism
Glutamate dehydrogenase (GLDH)
  • Enzyme involved in amino acid metabolism
  • Organ-specific: GLDH is liver-specific.
  • Subcellular localization: GLDH is only present in the mitochondria.
  • Severe hepatitis
  • Toxins (such as α-amanitin)
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma, liver metastases
AST/ALT ratio < 0.7 (up to 1) = "inflammatory type" ≥ 1 = "necrotic type"
Possible causes
  • Uncomplicated viral hepatitis
  • Minor fatty liver disease
  • Extrahepatic cholestasis
  • Alcoholic hepatitis (AST/ALT ratio typically > 2)
  • Fulminant, necrotic hepatitis
  • (Decompensated) cirrhosis
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma, liver metastases
  • Differential diagnosis: muscle damage

Parameters of cholestasis

Laboratory parameter Physiological function Characteristics

Frequent causes of elevation
γ-glutamyl transpeptidase (γ-GT or GGT)
  • Membrane-bound enzyme of glutathione metabolism and amino acid transport
  • The most sensitive parameter for diseases of the liver and/or biliary tract!
  • Found in many tissues
  • Cholestasis (obstructive or nonobstructive)
  • Alcohol abuse
Alkaline phosphatase (ALP)
  • Enzyme responsible for cleaving phosphate groups off various substances under alkaline conditions
  • Found in various isoenzymes in numerous tissues
  • Cholestasis (obstructive or nonobstructive)
  • Increased osteoblast activity
  • Pregnancy: third trimester
Bilirubin Direct bilirubin
  • Bilirubin, which is conjugated by the liver, is water-soluble and is excreted by the gallbladder.
  • Cholestasis (obstructive > nonobstructive)
Indirect bilirubin
  • Lipophilic (unconjugated) by-product of hemoglobin
  • Disturbed conjugation (intrahepatic)
    • Cholestasis (nonobstructive)
  • Increased accumulation (prehepatic)
    • Hemolysis/large hematomas
    • Ineffective erythropoiesis

Parameters of hepatic synthesis

  • Relevance
    • The liver is one of the most important organs of protein synthesis.
    • The synthesis capacity of the liver can be reduced by the destruction of hepatocytes (e.g., liver cirrhosis) or a deficiency of basic nutrients (e.g., in malnutrition).
    • In addition to the parameters listed in the table below, which provide an interpretation of the synthetic function of the liver, coagulation factors can also be used.
    • Vitamin K-dependent coagulation factors are particularly relevant, as their function is also indirectly reflected in the prothrombin time (PT, INR).

Albumin
  • Albumin makes up the majority of total plasma proteins, amounting to 60%. It has two main functions:
    • Maintenance of colloid osmotic pressure
    • Transport protein for degradation products and enzymes (such as indirect bilirubin)
  • Albumin is exclusively produced in the liver.
  • Dehydration
  • Decreased synthesis capacity of the liver (e.g., liver cirrhosis)
  • Loss of protein loss (e.g., nephrotic syndrome)
  • Malnutrition
Cholinesterase (CHE)
  • Nonspecific cholinesterase, which cleaves various choline esters and demonstrates similarities to acetylcholinesterase of nerve cells
  • Involved in the breakdown of muscle relaxants
  • Increased synthesis capacity of the liver
  • Diabetes mellitus, coronary artery disease
  • Decreased synthesis capacity of the liver (e.g., liver cirrhosis)
  • Occupational or accidental organophosphate pesticide exposure

Parameters of renal function

  • Relevance
    • The kidney values listed here include substances that allow for an indirect interpretation of the glomerular filtration rate (GFR).
    • These parameters are usually determined in blood.
Laboratory parameter Physiological function Characteristics

Frequent causes of elevation

Creatinine
  • Creatinine is a constantly accumulating by-product of creatine catabolism with an unknown biological function.
  • The creatinine value is susceptible to interference.
  • Acute or chronic renal failure
    • "Creatinine blind range"
    • See creatinine clearance
Cystatin C
  • Cystatin C inhibits cysteine proteinases and is formed in the majority of nucleated cells.
  • Low susceptibility to interference
  • Acute or chronic renal failure
  • Glucocorticoid administration
  • Hyperthyroidism

Other common renal substances

  • Relevance: Urea and uric acid are largely dependent on renal excretion; however, they provide an inaccurate assessment of renal function.

Pancreatic parameters

  • Relevance
    • The serum pancreatic parameters lipase and amylase are important primarily in the diagnosis of pancreatitis.
    • Elastase is a parameter of exocrine pancreatic function and is usually determined from several consecutive stool samples (also in serum during acute pancreatitis).

Electrolytes

  • Relevance
    • Electrolytes are chemical compounds that disintegrate into ions in aqueous solutions.
    • May be differentiated into cations (positively charged) and anions (negatively charged).
    • The most important electrolytes are sodium, potassium, chloride, magnesium, phosphate, and hydrogen carbonate ions.
    • The electrolytes that most commonly cause pathological changes (and are therefore standard in analysis) are sodium, potassium, and calcium, which are discussed in detail in other learning cards (see disorders of sodium balance, disorders of potassium balance, disorders of calcium balance).

  • Magnesium is a calcium antagonist that normally serves to prevent the intracellular accumulation of calcium.
  • Renal failure
  • Rhabdomyolysis
  • Congenital magnesium depletion in the intestine or kidney
  • Secondary deficiency, for example:
    • Polyuria
    • Malnutrition
    • Laxative abuse

  • Phosphate is an important source of energy in the form of adenosine triphosphate.
  • Renal failure
  • Hypoparathyroidism
  • Vitamin D intoxication
  • Malabsorption or malnutrition
  • Chronic alcohol abuse

  • Vitamin D deficiency

    • Formation of a compound with calcium
  • Hypophosphatemia
    • Weakness and paresthesias
    • Hemolysis and thrombocytopenia
    • Insulin resistance
    • Osteomalacia
    • Unconsciousness to coma

Iron metabolism

  • Relevance
    • Iron metabolism is closely associated with the formation of blood and the disease state of anemia.
    • In addition to the parameters listed here, hematological parameters such as hemoglobin, MCV, or MCH are important in assessing iron metabolism and identifying causes of anemia.
    • Iron deficiency is discussed in detail in a separate learning card (see iron deficiency).

Serum iron
  • Central trace element involved in hematopoiesis.
  • Iron levels are subject to significant daily fluctuation.
  • Iron overload, e.g., via:
    • Hemochromatosis
    • Repeated blood transfusions
  • Iron toxicity
  • Iron deficiency, e.g., via:
    • Hemorrhage
    • Malnutrition
    • Increased demand (e.g., in pregnancy)
  • Impaired distribution of ion, for example:
    • Anemia of chronic disease
Ferritin
  • Protein complex that is responsible for iron storage.
  • Ferritin is the parameter of choice in determining iron deficiency.
  • Acute phase protein
  • Acute phase reaction
  • Anemia of chronic disease
  • Iron overload, e.g., via:
    • Hemochromatosis
    • Repeated blood transfusions
  • Iron deficiency (basically conclusive in suspected cases!)
  • Nephrotic syndrome
Transferrin*
  • Transport protein for iron
  • Transferrin saturation (TfS) can be calculated from both transferrin and iron values.
  • Anti-acute phase protein
  • Iron deficiency
  • Pregnancy
  • Acute phase reaction
  • Iron overload e.g., via:
    • Hemochromatosis
    • Repeated blood transfusions
  • Nephrotic syndrome
*There are two additional forms of transferrin that may be used for diagnosis:
  • Beta-2 transferrin (for the detection of a CSF leak in skull fractures)
  • Carbohydrate-deficient transferrin (laboratory marker used to detect chronic alcoholism)

A decrease in ferritin with a reduced hemoglobin concentration is essentially evidence of iron deficiency anemia!

An elevated ferritin level does not exclude iron deficiency anemia. Concurrent chronic inflammation can increase ferritin levels!

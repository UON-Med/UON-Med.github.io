Wilms tumor (Nephroblastoma)

Abstract

Wilms tumor (nephroblastoma) is the most common renal malignancy in children, typically affecting those 2–5 years of age. A minority of cases are associated with specific syndromes (e.g., WAGR, Beckwith-Wiedemann) and gene mutations (e.g., WT1). Wilms tumor is typically an incidental finding that presents as a large abdominal mass. Other signs and symptoms may occur, such as hematuria and abdominal pain, especially in tumors that are large, ruptured, or metastasized. Treatment consists of tumor resection and chemotherapy for all stages (except for very low-risk tumors), while radiation is also used in advanced disease.

Epidemiology

  • Peak incidence: 2–5 years
  • Most common malignant neoplasm of the kidney in children

Etiology

The exact etiology of Wilms tumor remains unknown, but it has been associated with several genetic mutations and syndromes.

Genetic predisposition

  • Gene mutations have been found in children both with and without genetic syndromes who have Wilms tumor.
  • The WT1 (Wilms tumor 1) gene is the most important Wilms tumor gene (mutated in ∼ 10–20% of cases)
  • p53 tumor suppressor gene mutations (frequent in Wilms tumors with anaplastic histology)
    •

Associated syndromes

  • Approx. 10% of Wilms tumors occur in children with syndromes.
  • WAGR syndrome; (deletion of WT1 gene)
    • Wilms tumor
    • Aniridia
    • Genitourinary (GU) anomalies
    • Intellectual disability (mental Retardation)
  • Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome (mutations of WT2 gene = 11p15.5 locus)
  • Denys-Drash syndrome (point mutation in WT1 gene)
    •
  • Horseshoe kidneys
    •
  • In a minority of cases, Wilms tumor is associated with a positive family history or with other isolated GU anomalies such as renal ectopia or duplicated collecting systems.

WAGR is the syndrome of Wilms tumor, Aniridia, Genitourinary (GU) anomalies, and intellectual disability (mental Retardation)!

Clinical features

  • Abdominal mass (often found incidentally!)
    • Non-tender
    • Unilateral, not crossing midline (however, up to 10% of cases are bilateral and/or multifocal)
    • Smooth and firm
  • Other signs and symptoms
    • Abdominal pain (∼ 40% of cases)
    • Hematuria (∼ 25% of cases)
    • Hypertension (∼ 25% of cases)
    • In cases of subcapsular hemorrhage: anemia, and possibly fever
    • Symptoms caused by metastatic spread (e.g., pulmonary symptoms)

Wilms tumor should be suspected in a toddler with a non-tender abdominal mass, especially if it is firm, smooth, and associated with hematuria and/or hypertension!

Stages

There are two staging systems for Wilms tumor:

  • National Wilms Tumor Study (NWTS) system: used in the U.S. and Canada, and based on surgical evaluation before chemotherapy
  • International Society of Pediatric Oncology, or Société Internationale d'Oncologie Pédiatrique (SIOP) system: used in Europe, and based on surgical evaluation after chemotherapy.

Staging according to the National Wilms Tumor Study (NWTS)

NWTS staging is used in the U.S. and Canada. It is based on surgical evaluation prior to chemotherapy. The first four stages are applicable to unilateral disease.

Stage Description
I
  • Tumor affecting kidney only
  • Resection complete, capsule intact, no renal sinus vessel involvement, no tumor tissue outside area of resection
  • No rupture or previous biopsy
  • Microscopic regional lymph node inspection consistent with Stage 1
I
  • Tumor extending beyond kidney, but resected without evidence of tumor outside area of resection
  • Penetration beyond the renal capsule, into soft tissue of the renal sinus, or blood vessels beyond renal parenchyma (but within resected specimen)
III
  • Residual tumor post-op, but in abdomen only (e.g., regional lymph nodes or peritoneum affected, tumor resection incomplete, tumor rupture with spillage, previous biopsy, or preoperative chemotherapy before removal of the tumor)
IV
  • Hematogenous metastasis (e.g., lung, liver, bone, brain), lymph nodes affected outside of abdomen
V
  • Bilateral renal involvement (5–7% of patients); each side staged separately

Diagnostics

  • Urinalysis: hematuria may be present
  • Imaging
    • Best initial test: ultrasound: mostly uniform echogenicity with hypoechoic areas of necrosis
    • Abdominal CT/MRI: assess extent of involvement and help with surgical planning
    • CT thorax/CXR: determine metastases and staging
  • Additional evaluation
    • Serum creatinine
    • Liver function tests
    • Serum calcium
    • CBC and coagulation studies
    • Genetic tests can aid in predicting outcome and assessing the risk of relapse.

Biopsy is usually reserved for assessing nodules that are suspected metastases, as tumor capsule rupture and spillage results in more advanced staging and intensive treatmen!

Pathology

Wilms tumor consists of embryonic glomerular structures and may include cysts, hemorrhage, or necrosis. It typically has a pseudocapsule. Classically it is made up of three different cell types, though some tumors may have two or just one of these types.

Prognosis

  • Good prognosis: five-year survival rates up to 90%
    • Very low-risk tumors (stages I and II, patients < 2 years, tumor mass < 550 g, favorable histology): 98% five-year survival rate
    • Diffuse anaplastic (high-risk) tumor: four-year event-free survival approx. 83% for stage II, 65% for stage III, and 33% for stage IV

References:[6]

Prevention

  • Regular screening (primary prevention) of children with associated syndromes, including WAGR, Beckwith-Wiedemann, and Denys-Drash (e.g., abdominal ultrasound every three months until the age of 8)
  • The majority of tumor recurrences happen within two years of treatment.
    • Screening for recurrence (tertiary prevention)
      • Abdominal ultrasound
      • CXR or CT thorax

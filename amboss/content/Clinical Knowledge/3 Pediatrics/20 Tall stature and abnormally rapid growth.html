Tall stature and abnormally rapid growth

Abstract

Tall stature is defined as a height of more than 2 standard deviations above the population mean or exceeding the 97th percentile for age and sex. In most cases, tall stature represents an acceptable normal variation in growth. However, tall stature can also be the result of an underlying pathology (i.e., endocrine or genetic abnormalities). Differentiating between normal or pathological growth variations involves a thorough medical history and clinical examination. Bone age tests, karyotyping, and endocrinological tests can confirm the diagnosis of a pathological cause.

Overview

  • Definition: a height of more than 2 standard deviations above the population mean or exceeding the 97th percentile on the normal growth curve for age and sex.
  • Most tall children do not have a pathological cause

Evaluation of growth

  1. History
    • Determine midparental height
    • Assess milestones (exclude developmental delay)
  2. Physical examination
    • Accurate serial measurements of individual body areas
      • Length or height
      • Growth velocity
      • Proportional vs disproportional growth
    • Exclude the following:
      • Secondary sexual characteristics
      • Neurological lesions
      • Dysmorphisms
  3. Further diagnostic measures (if a pathological cause is suspected)
    • Bone age
    • Karyotyping
    • Endocrine laboratory tests (depends on which pathology is suspected e.g., Insulinlike growth factor for excess growth hormone, or TSH and T4 for hyperthyroidism)

Endocrine disorders

Gigantism

  • Definition: Rare disorder characterized by abnormal linear growth during childhood due to growth hormone excess while the epiphyseal growth plates are still open.
  • Pathophysiology
    • Most common: â growth hormone (GH) secretion from the anterior pituitary (i.e., adenoma ) â GH induces IGF-1 synthesis â IGF-1 stimulates cell growth and proliferation (for more details see pathophysiology section in learning card on âacromegalyâ)
    • â GHRH secretion from the hypothalamus (i.e., tumors)
    • â production of IGF-binding protein â prolongs half-life of IGF-I
  • Risk factors (associated with an â incidence of pituitary tumors)
    • Multiple endocrine neoplasia (MEN) type I
    • McCune-Albright syndrome
    • Carney complex
    • Neurofibromatosis
    • Tuberous sclerosis
  • Clinical features
    • Tall stature
    • â growth of distal limbs (i.e., hands, feet, fingers, toes)
    • Progressive macroencephaly
    • Coarse facial features, frontal bossing, prognathism
    • Obesity
    • Tumor mass symptoms: headaches, visual changes , features of hypopituitarism
  • Diagnosis
    • â serum IGF-1
    • â GH after oral glucose test confirms pituitary gigantism
      • Oral glucose test
        • Two baseline GH levels should be taken prior to glucose administration
        • Tests GH 1 hour after oral ingestion of 100 g glucose
        • GH > 10 ng/mL confirms pituitary gigantism
        • GH < 5 ng/mL normal
    • After a biochemical diagnosis is established
      • cMRI: pituitary mass
      • CT if MRI is negative: exclude other GH-secreting tumors (e.g., pancreas, adrenal glands, ovarian, bronchial)
  • Treatment
    • Transsphenoidal surgery: pituitary adenoma excision
    • Medical therapy
      • Somatostatin analogs (e.g., octreotide )
      • GH receptor antagonists (e.g. pegvisomant)
  • Complications
    • Osteoarthritis
    • Carpal tunnel syndrome
    • Cardiovascular disease (i.e., hypertension, LVH)
    • Endocrine disorders (i.e., hypogonadism, diabetes, hyperprolactinemia)
    • Benign tumors (i.e., uterine myomas, prostatic hypertrophy, colonic polyps)

Other causes

  • Precocious puberty
  • Hyperthyroidism

Genetic disorders

Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome

  • Definition: Congenital disorder of growth with a predisposition to tumor development
  • Epidemiology
    • â¼ 1/15,000 newborns in the US
    • Increased risk of Wilms tumor, hepatoblastoma, neuroblastoma, adrenal tumors
  • Etiology: Genotypic abnormalities of chromosome 11 (â¼ 80% of cases)
  • Clinical features
    • Macrosomia ; , omphalocele (i.e., exomphalos), and macroglossia
    • Earlobe creases and pits , midfacial hypoplasia
    • Organ enlargement (heart, liver, kidney, etc.)
    • Features of neonatal hypoglycemia : irritability, intellectual disability
    • Hemihypertrophy/hemihyperplasia
    • Genitourinary abnormalities
    • Facies: midface hypoplasia and infraorbital creases
    • Cleft palate (rare)
  • Diagnosis
    • â Blood glucose, â serum insulin, IGF-2 (hypoglycemia)
    • Abdominal ultrasound (embryonal tumors)
  • Treatment
    • Frequent feedings to maintain sufficient blood glucose levels (> 60 mg/dl)
    • Resection of embryonal tumors

47,XYY syndrome and 47,XXX syndrome

  • Etiology: excess growth syndrome, which is caused by an additional Y chromosome in males (XYY) and an additional X chromosome in females (XXX)
  • Epidemiology
    • XYY syndrome: 1/1,000â1/2,000
    • XXX syndrome: 1/1,000
  • Clinical features
    • XYY syndrome
      • Tall stature
      • Sometimes severe acne
      • Delayed language development
      • Learning disabilities
      • Behavioral problems
      • Fertility problems (decreased sperm count) possible
    • XXX syndrome
      • Tall stature
      • Delayed motor and language development
      • Learning disabilities
      • Menstrual abnormalities, occasionally fertility problems
  • Diagnosis
    • The majority of triple X women or double Y men are never diagnosed.
    • Chromosome analysis
  • Treatment
    • Only symptomatic treatment is possible
    • Speech therapy
    • Physical, occupational, and educational therapy
    • Reproductive specialist in case of fertility problems

  • Epidemiology: â¼ 1/14,000 newborns
  • Etiology: excess growth syndrome, which manifests either congenitally or within the first 4 years of life. Sotos syndrome is caused by a mutation in the NSD1 gene on chromosome 5.
  • Symptoms
    • Tall stature
    • Macrocephalus
    • Facies
      • High forehead
      • Elongated face
      • Hypertelorism
      • Pointed chin
      • Receding hairline
    • Psychomotor retardation
    • Hypotonia
    • Delays in achieving milestones (e.g., walking, talking, clumsiness)
    • Diagnosis
      • Usually clinical
      • DNA studies (5q35 microdeletions and partial NSD1 deletions in â¼ 10â15% of cases.

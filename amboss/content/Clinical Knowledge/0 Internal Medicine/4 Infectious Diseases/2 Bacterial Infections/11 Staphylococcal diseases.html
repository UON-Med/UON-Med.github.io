Staphylococcal diseases

Abstract

Staphylococci are gram-positive, spherical-shaped bacteria that form clusters and are commonly found on the skin and mucous membranes. Clinically, the most important species include Staphylococcus aureus and Staphylococcus epidermidis, which are categorized according to their coagulase activity. S. aureus is coagulase positive and expresses several virulence factors which support evasion of the host immune response. S. epidermidis is coagulase negative and is usually less virulent, although it can evade the host immune system by forming and subsequently hiding in a biofilm. S. aureus is commonly responsible for many localized infections (e.g., folliculitis, cervical lymphadenopathy) and also severe organ infections in the setting of bacteremia (e.g., endocarditis, osteomyelitis). As a toxin producer, S. aureus can cause food poisoning (see Staphylococcal food poisoning) and, in severe cases, life-threatening diseases such as staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome (SSSS) or toxic shock syndrome (TSS). Methicillin-resistant S. aureus (MRSA), in particular, poses a major threat to both immunocompromised and multimorbid patients and is a considerable challenge to hospital hygiene. S. epidermidis is mostly responsible for foreign body infections caused by, for example, contaminated peripheral lines or prosthetic joints. The treatment of choice is anti-staphylococcal penicillins (e.g., oxacillin, flucloxacillin) or first and second generation cephalosporins.

Definition

Staphylococci are immotile, gram-positive bacteria that have a round shape and are found in clusters.

Epidemiology

  • S. aureus colonizes approx. 37% of the general human population.
    • Three patterns of carriage have been observed: 20% are permanent carriers, 60% are intermittent carriers, 20% are noncarriers
    • MRSA carrier rates and invasive infections have increased during the last decades.

Epidemiological data refers to the US, unless otherwise specified.

Classification

  • Coagulase-positive: Staphylococcus aureus
  • Coagulase-negative
    • Staphylococcus epidermidis

Resistance and virulence of staphylococci

Staphylococcus aureus

The following factors contribute to the resistance and virulence of S. aureus and allow it to evade the host immune response. For MRSA, see methicillin-resistant .

  • Penicillinase (a β-lactamase)
  • Protein A
  • Capsular polysaccharides
  • Enzymes that destroy host tissues
  • Toxins
    • Exotoxins: act as superantigens and can activate large numbers of T cells, resulting in the massive release of cytokines (e.g. toxic shock toxin-1)
    • Cytotoxins: cause inflammation and lysis of target cells

Staphylococcus epidermidis

  • S. epidermidis contains a polysaccharide capsule that allows it to produce a biofilm on the surface of prosthetic material and thereby evade the host's immune system.

Treatment

Staphylococcus aureus

  • Anti-staphylococcal penicillins: oxacillin, flucloxacillin
  • First and second generation cephalosporins
  • In case of penicillin allergy: clindamycin
  • MRSA: drugs of last resort such as vancomycin and linezolid

Coagulase-negative staphylococcus (particularly S. epidermidis)

  • First-line treatment: oxacillin, flucloxacillin
  • In case of penicillin allergy: levofloxacin and rifampicin

Toxic shock syndrome (TSS)

TSS is a systemic inflammatory reaction caused by bacterial exotoxins.

Staphylococcal toxic shock syndrome

  • Etiology
    • ∼ 50% of cases: caused by prolonged use of tampons over several days
    • Postsurgical or after wound infections
  • Pathophysiology: exotoxins (see resistance and virulence of staphylococci)
  • Clinical findings: : if tampon related, symptoms usually appear within five days after menstruation.
    • Widespread exanthem with skin peeling involving palms and soles
    • Myalgias, vomiting, diarrhea
    • Symptoms of shock: hypotension, tachycardia
    • Fever
  • Laboratory tests
    • ↓ Platelets
    • ↑ Bilirubin, ↑ transaminase
  • Treatment
    • IV fluids
    • Remove focus of infection (e.g., drain surgical wounds, remove tampon)
    • Antibiotics
      • First-line antibiotics against MSSA TSS: clindamycin + oxacillin
      • First-line antibiotics against MRSA TSS: clindamycin + vancomycin

Streptococcal toxic shock syndrome

  • Etiology
    • Significantly rarer than staphylococcal shock syndrome
    • Following infection with group A streptococcus (particularly Streptococcus pyogenes), which forms the exotoxins A, B, and C
  • Pathophysiology
    • Exotoxins act as superantigens and can activate large numbers of T cells, resulting in the massive release of cytokines.
  • Clinical findings
    • Soft tissue infection
    • Mostly associated with tenderness and severe pain where minor trauma has occurred
    • Other symptoms similar to Staphylococcal toxic shock syndrome
  • Laboratory findings
    • Leukocytosis with left shift
    • ↑ Creatinine
  • Treatment
    • Supportive therapy: IV fluids
    • Surgical debridement of infectious focus
    • First-line antibiotics: clindamycin + penicillin G

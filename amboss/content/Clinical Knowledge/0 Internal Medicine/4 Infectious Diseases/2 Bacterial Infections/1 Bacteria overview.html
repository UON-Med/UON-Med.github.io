Bacteria overview

Abstract

The nomenclature of bacteria is complex; human pathogenic bacteria are classified according to their form (cocci, bacilli, or coccobacilli) and Gram staining properties (gram‑positive, gram‑negative, and atypica). Further classification is based on metabolic activity (aerobic or anaerobic) and virulence factors (e.g., formation of coagulase or enterotoxins), among other traits. The most important human pathogenic bacteria are discussed in this learning card. An overview of the basics of bacteriology – including diagnosis and genetics – may be found in the learning card on the basics of bacteriology.

Streptococcus gallolyticus

  • Isolated in stool in up to 10% of the population (higher rates in patients with colorectal cancer)

  • Bacteremia
  • Endocarditis

Streptococcus pyogenes (Group A Streptococcus)
  • Humans are the sole host; nasopharynx
  • Facultative anaerobe
  • Bacterial culture: beta hemolysis
  • Lancefield group A
  • Important virulence factors
    • Streptolysin O
    • Streptococcal enzymes (DNase, hyaluronidase, streptokinase )
    • M protein (virulence factor): prevents phagocytosis and inhibits alternative complement activation. Rheumatic fever may occur as a result of cross‑reactivity.
    • Hyaluronic acid capsule: prevents phagocytosis
    • Frequently, protein F
    • Erythrogenic exotoxin A: superantigen that causes toxic shock syndrome
    • Bacitracin sensitive
    • Erythrogenic scarlet fever exotoxins
  • Head and neck: tonsillitis, peritonsillar abscess, otitis media
  • Skin and soft tissue infections: erysipelas, cellulitis, impetigo, necrotizing fasciitis
  • Toxin-mediated disease: scarlet fever , streptococcal toxic shock syndrome (STSS)
  • Complications of primary infection :
    • Acute rheumatic fever
    • Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis
    • Others
    • PANDAS syndrome (pediatric autoimmune neuropsychiatric disorders associated with streptococcal infections)

Streptococcus agalactiae (Group B Streptococcus)
  • Colonizes the human gastrointestinal and genital tracts
  • Facultative anaerobe
  • Bacterial culture: beta hemolysis
    • CAMP factor (used to identify S. agalactiae; enlarges the hemolysis area in culture formed by S. aureus)
  • Lancefield group B
  • Important virulence factors: capsule is possible
  • Bacitracin-resistant
  • Neonatal meningitis, sepsis, and pneumonia
Peptostreptococcus Numerous peptostreptococcus species (e.g., P. anaerobius, P. magnus)

  • Dental infections

Enterococcus

E. faecium and E. faecalis
  • Part of the natural flora of the gastrointestinal tract
  • Facultative anaerobe
  • Bacterial culture: gamma hemolysis, partially also alpha hemolysis
  • Lancefield group D
  • Growth in bile and 6.5% NaCl
  • Urinary tract infection
  • Cholecystitis
  • Endocarditis
  • Intrinsic resistance to cephalosporins
  • Ampicillin
  • Vancomycin in patients with penicillin allergy
  • Vancomycin‑resistant enterococci: linezolid, daptomycin

“B for baby”: Group B streptococci primarily affect neonates.

Clostridia (spore‑forming bacteria)
  • Clostridium difficile
  • Natural gut flora of ∼ 5% of all adults
  • Anaerobe
  • Facultative pathogen
  • Important virulence factor:
    • Toxin A (enterotoxin) and toxin B (cytotoxin)

  • Clostridium perfringens
  • Part of the natural skin and gastrointestinal flora
  • Anaerobe
  • Facultative pathogen
  • Microscopy: club‑shaped bacilli
  • Important virulence factor:
    • Exotoxins;
    • Some strains produce enterotoxins (alpha toxin)
  • Gas gangrene (clostridial myonecrosis)
  • Food poisoning
  • Piperacillin-tazobactam + clindamycin
  • Early surgical debridement is vital.
  • Supportive treatment of food poisoning; no antibiotics required
  • Clostridium tetani
  • Found in soil, worldwide
  • Anaerobe
  • Obligate pathogen
  • Microscopy: drumstick form
  • Important virulence factor: tetanospasmin
  • Tetanus
  • Penicillin G or metronidazole
  • Tetanus prophylaxis may be necessary depending on the vaccination status and wound type (severity, contamination).
  • Clostridium botulinum
  • Found in soil, intestinal tracts of birds and fish, and agricultural products (e.g., vegetables)
  • Anaerobe
  • Obligate pathogen
  • Microscopy: club‑shaped bacilli with flagella
  • Important virulence factor: botulinum toxin
  • Botulism
  • Administration of a botulism antitoxin is the most important treatment measure. Antibiotics are of secondary importance.
Listeria
  • Listeria monocytogenes
  • Most commonly found in unpasteurized milk products
  • Facultative anaerobe
  • Important virulence factor: resistance to cold
  • Listeriosis
  • Ampicillin or penicillin G
Corynebacterium
  • Corynebacterium diphtheriae
  • Humans are the only hosts
  • Facultative anaerobe
  • Microscopy: stainable using the Neisser stain
  • Important virulence factor: exotoxin
  • Diphtheria
  • Erythromycin or penicillin G
  • Administration of a diphtheria antitoxin is an essential part of treatment.
Bacillus
  • Bacillus anthracis
  • Found in soil and mammals
  • Aerobe
  • The only obligate pathogen of the Bacillus genus.
  • Microscopy: appearance is similar to that of a bamboo stick
  • Important virulence factor: spore formation; exotoxins
  • Anthrax
  • Ciprofloxacin (or Penicillin G) plus clindamycin (or linezolid)
    • Plus meropenem in the case of meningitis
  • Anthrax antitoxin
  • Bacillus cereus
  • Proliferate quickly in contaminated food; rice that is kept warm and/or reheated is a particularly good breeding ground.
  • Aerobe
  • Important virulence factor: enterotoxin
  • Food poisoning (see bacillus cereus infection)
  • There is no specific treatment against the enterotoxin available.
  • Antibiotic treatment is not indicated.

  • Component of the gut flora in birds. Transmission often occurs via contaminated poultry.

  • Microaerophile
  • Campylobacter enterocolitis
  • Reactive arthritis
  • Guillain‑Barré syndrome
  • Macrolides (e.g., erythromycin)
Pseudomonas
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • Common in the environment; grows in water and humid conditions, e.g., hot tubs, contaminated contact lens solution
  • Lophotrichous flagella
  • Aerobic
  • Special macroscopic characteristics
    • A typical blue‑green pus may form in infection
    • Sweet odor, if grown in culture
  • Infections caused by Pseudomonas aeruginosa
    • Nosocomial urinary tract infections
    • Nosocomial pneumonia
    • Nosocomial burn wound infections
    • Pneumonia in association with cystic fibrosis
    • Endocarditis in intravenous drug abuse
  • Piperacillin or ceftazidime
  • Ciprofloxacin in severe infections
  • Intrinsic and acquired resistances are common; some strains are multidrug-resistant.
Burkholderia
  • Burkholderia cepacia
  • Aquatic environments
  • Aerobic
  • Pneumonia in association with cystic fibrosis
  • Cotrimoxazole, meropenem, ceftazidime, or quinolone
Vibrio
  • Vibrio cholerae
  • Humans and contaminated water
  • Straight or curved, polar flagella
  • Facultative anaerobe
  • Produces cholera toxin (enterotoxin) consisting of two subunits:
    • The B unit binds to the intestinal epithelium.
    • The toxic A unit is cleaved and channeled into the cell.
  • Cholera
  • Fluoroquinolones (e.g., ciprofloxacin)
Fusobacteriaceae
  • Numerous
  • Humans; oral cavity
  • Anaerobic
  • In some cases, formation of a capsule
  • Injury to the skin or mucous membranes can lead to the formation of foul‑smelling abscess or even peritonitis and sepsis
  • Primary resistance against aminoglycosides
  • Metronidazole, clindamycin, or penicillin
Bacteroides
  • Numerous
  • Account for 90% of all fecal flora
  • Responsible for the typical oder of stool
Bartonella

  • Bartonella quintana
  • Humans (the louse is usually the vector)
  • Facultative intracellular
  • Microaerophile
  • Trench fever
  • Doxycycline + gentamicin
  • Bartonella bacilliformis

  • Facultative intracellular
  • Flagellate
  • Bartonellosis (Carrión's disease)
    • Oroya fever (acute phase)

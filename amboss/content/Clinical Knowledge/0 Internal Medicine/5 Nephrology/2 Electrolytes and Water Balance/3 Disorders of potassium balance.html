Disorders of potassium balance

Abstract

Content currently under development

Potassium disorders include hyperkalemia (high serum potassium) and hypokalemia (low serum potassium). The most common cause of hyperkalemia is decreased kidney function. It may also be the result of drugs (e.g., potassium-sparing diuretics, angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors, nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), and digoxin) or endocrinological disturbances (e.g., hypoaldosteronism, hypocortisolism). In contrast, a low serum potassium level may be the result of gastrointestinal losses (e.g., due to vomiting, diarrhea), and drugs (e.g., non-potassium-sparing diuretics, laxatives). Reviewing the patient's medication list and assessing the patient for aldosterone and cortisol disturbances are essential to determine the cause of potassium disorders. Acute changes in serum potassium are very dangerous as they influence the resting membrane potential, and thus the electrical excitability of cells. These changes can lead to malignant cardiac arrhythmias. The management of hypo- and hyperkalemia includes dietary changes, medications, and (only in hyperkalemia) dialysis. Until the potassium serum concentration is corrected, it should be monitored closely.

Definition

Content currently under development

  • Hypokalemia: serum potassium level < 3.5 mEq/L
  • Hyperkalemia: serum potassium level > 5 mEq/L

Etiology

Content currently under development

Hypokalemia

  • Potassium loss
    • Renal loss
      • Endocrine causes: hyperaldosteronism, hypercortisolism
      • Drugs: diuretics, glucocorticoids, licorice (aldosterone-like action)
      • Hypomagnesemia
      • Type I and II renal tubular acidosis
    • Gastrointestinal loss: vomiting, diarrhea, laxatives (Watch out in patients receiving bowel preparation prior to medical procedures!)
  • Intracellular shift
    • Alkalosis
    • Insulin therapy

"Potassium follows glucose into the cells!": (i)nsulin → (i)ntracellular K+

Hyperkalemia

  • Potassium excess
    • Reduced excretion: acute and chronic kidney disease
    • Endocrine causes: hypocortisolism, hypoaldosteronism
    • Drugs: potassium-sparing diuretics, ACE inhibitors, angiotensin receptor blockers, NSAIDs, and trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole
    • GI absorption: increased intake (e.g., fresh fruits)
    • Type IV renal tubular acidosis
    • Release from cells: myolysis, tumor lysis, hemolysis
  • Extracellular shift
    • Acidosis
    • Insulin deficiency
    • Drugs: succinylcholine , digoxin
  • Pseudohyperkalemia: release of potassium from red blood cell lysis
    • Blood drawn from the side of IV infusion or a central line without previous flushing
    • Prolonged use of a tourniquet

Errors in the technique of blood drawing may lead to red blood cell lysis and a falsely elevated serum potassium concentration (pseudohyperkalemia)!

Pathophysiology

Content currently under development

Important causes of potassium disorders

  • Blood pH (The following mechanisms work in the reverse direction as well)
    • Alkalosis; → ↓ extracellular H+ → stimulation of the Na+/H+ antiporter (transfers H+ out of the cells in exchange for Na+) → ↑ intracellular Na+ → ↑ sodium gradient stimulates the Na+/K+-ATPase (transfers K+ into the cells in exchange for Na+) → ↓ extracellular K+ concentration → hypokalemia
    • Acidosis ; → ↑ extracellular H+ → inhibition of the Na+/H+ antiporter → ↓ intracellular Na+ → ↓ sodium gradient inhibits the Na+/K+-ATPase → ↑ extracellular K+ concentration → hyperkalemia
    • Hypokalemia; → ↓ extracellular K+ concentration → ↓ potassium gradient inhibits the Na+/K+-ATPase → ↓ extracellular Na+ → ↓ sodium gradient inhibits the Na+/H+ antiporter → ↓ extracellular H+ → alkalosis
    • Hyperkalemia ; ↑ extracellular K+ concentration → ↑ potassium gradient stimulates the Na+/K+-ATPase → ↑ extracellular Na+ → ↑ sodium gradient stimulates the Na+/H+ antiporter → ↑ extracellular H+ → acidosis
    • Exception: In renal tubular acidosis findings include hypokalemia and metabolic acidosis.
  • Aldosterone
  • Insulin

K+ acts like H+ → Hyperkalemia leads to acidosis and vice versa! Hypokalemia leads to alkalosis and vice versa!

Effects of potassium disorders

  • Important factor in maintaining the resting membrane potential.
    • ↑ Extracellular K+ concentration → ↓ resting membrane potential (less negative than -90 mV) → ↑ excitability
    • ↓ Extracellular K+ concentration → ↑ resting membrane potential (more negative than -90 mV) → ↓ excitability

Particularly acute extracellular changes in concentration influence excitability! Chronic changes lead to intracellular compensation!

Clinical features

Content currently under development

Hypokalemia

  • Cardiac arrhythmias (e.g., premature atrial and ventricular complexes, ventricular fibrillation)
  • Muscle weakness, paralysis
  • ↓ Deep tendon reflexes
  • Nausea, vomiting, constipation
  • Fatigue
  • Polyuria
  • In patients treated with digoxin: symptoms of digoxin toxicity

Hyperkalemia

  • Cardiac arrhythmias (e.g., atrioventricular block, ventricular fibrillation)
  • Muscle weakness, paralysis
  • ↓ Deep tendon reflexes
  • Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea

Both electrolyte disorders are potential causes of cardiac arrhythmia and may lead to ventricular fibrillation!

Diagnostics

Content currently under development

Laboratory evaluation

  • Serum potassium levels (always confirm abnormal serum potassium levels with a repeat blood draw)
  • Hypokalemia: urinary potassium level
    • > 20 mEq/L: renal loss
    • < 20 mEq/L: extrarenal loss
  • Hyperkalemia:
    • Creatinine, GFR to assess renal function
    • In normal renal function: aldosterone level to rule out Addison's disease
  • Arterial blood gas (ABG): influence of pH on the potassium homeostasis
    • Metabolic alkalosis: hypokalemia
    • Metabolic acidosis: hyperkalemia

ECG changes

  • Hypokalemia
    • T-wave flattening
    • ST depression
    • Presence of U waves, possibly TU fusion, premature atrial and ventricular complexes
  • Hyperkalemia
    • Tall, peaked T waves
    • P-wave flattening, lengthening of the PR interval
    • Shortened QT interval
    • Lengthening of the QRS interval

"No pot, no tea!" → A lack of potassium may result in a flattened T wave

Treatment

Content currently under development

Patients require close monitoring (repeat serum potassium levels, EGCs) until the potassium concentration has been corrected

Hypokalemia

  • Treat the underlying cause or adjust an existing treatment ; correct possible hypomagnesemia
  • Repletion
    • Intake of high-potassium foods: such as fresh fruits (esp. bananas), dried fruits and legumes, vegetables (esp. green leafy vegetables), nuts, seeds, bran products, milk, and dairy products
    • Potassium chloride (KCl)
      • Preferred route of administration: oral
      • In severe hypokalemia (< 2.5 mEq/L): IV

IV potassium may cause vein damage and lead to cardiac arrhythmias. Therefore, it should always be administered slowly (max. rate of 22 mEq/h)!

Hyperkalemia

  • Potassium level ≤ 6.5 mEq/L and no signs of cardiotoxicity: decrease intake/absorption (slow-acting option)
    • Discontinue drugs that increase serum potassium
    • Avoid high-potassium foods
    • Cation-exchange resins (e.g., sodium polystyrene sulfonate): bind potassium in the gut via the exchange of Na+ for K+
      • Adverse effect (rare): intestinal necrosis
  • Potassium level > 6.5 mEq/L or cardiotoxicity: IV therapy for cardioprotection and to induce elimination/intracellular shift (rapid-acting option)
    • Calcium gluconate: should be administered first!
    • Insulin and glucose
    • Sodium bicarbonate: in acidemic patients
    • Beta-2-adrenergic agonists
    • Forced diuresis (loop diuretics with normal saline (NS) solution)
  • Renal failure or ineffective initial treatment
    • Hemodialysis: most effective and definitive treatment option

Serum potassium levels < 2.5 mEq/L and > 6.5 mEq/L are a medical emergency and need to be treated immediately!

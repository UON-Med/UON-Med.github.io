Hemostasis and bleeding disorders

Abstract

Bleeding disorders are characterized by defects in hemostasis that lead to an increased susceptibility to bleeding (also known as hemorrhagic diathesis). They are caused either by platelet disorders (primary hemostasis defect), coagulation defects (secondary hemostasis defect), or, in some cases, a combination of both. Coagulation defects may be general or further divided into either intrinsic or extrinsic defects according to the specific pathway of the coagulation cascade that is affected. Bleeding disorders may be inherited or acquired. Although clinical features of inherited and acquired disorders may overlap, mucocutaneous bleeding (e.g., epistaxis, petechiae, gastrointestinal bleeding) is associated more with platelet disorders, while bleeding into potential spaces (e.g., hemarthrosis, muscular bleeding) is more characteristic of coagulation defects. A basic understanding of hemostasis is necessary for properly interpreting laboratory studies in order to accurately diagnose the disorder and ensure appropriate treatment.

Classification

Hemostasis is the processes involved in stopping hemorrhages, while hemorrhagic diathesis implies an abnormal and increased susceptibility to bleeding.

Disorders of primary hemostasis

  • Platelet disorders
  • Platelet deficiency
  • Disorders affecting the vessel wall
    • Vascular hemorrhagic diathesis (e.g., Henoch-Schönlein purpura)
    • Thrombotic microangiopathy (e.g., hemolytic-uremic syndrome, HUS)

Disorders of secondary hemostasis

  • Disorders of the coagulation cascade
    • Intrinsic pathway: : coagulation factors deficiency: hemophilia A (factor VIII); or hemophilia B (factor IX), autoantibodies (e.g., systemic lupus erythematosus, or SLE)
    • Extrinsic pathway: : factor VII deficiency
    • Vitamin K-associated factor disorders:
      • Deficiency of vitamin K-dependent factors: deficiency of coagulation factors II, VII, IX, and X
      • Vitamin K antagonist therapy: warfarin therapy
      • Vitamin K deficiency: : e.g., malabsorption syndrome, depletion of gut flora (e.g., following antibiotic administration), vitamin K-deficient bleeding of the newborn
      • Impaired hepatic production: : e.g., liver cirrhosis
      • Autoantibodies (e.g., SLE)

Fibrinolytic disorders

  • Hyperfibrinolysis: pathological fibrinolysis, which leads to bleeding (caused by, e.g., congenital or metabolic causes, trauma, decreased hepatic clearance caused by liver dysfunction, sepsis, etc)
    • Mechanism: Excessive plasmin activity causes increased fibrin degradation → Blood clots become unstable or dissolve shortly after forming.
    • Can occur in:
      • Surgery in tPA-rich organs such as the prostate and uterus
      • Prostate carcinoma (paraneoplastic)
      • Peripartum complications
      • Disseminated intravascular coagulation (secondary to liver dysfunction, sepsis etc.)
      • Coagulation lysis therapy following a stroke

Other important types

Von Willebrand disease

  • Definition: : a disorder of primary hemostasis characterized by either a deficiency or disorder of vWF
  • Epidemiology: most common congenital hemostatic disorder
  • Clinical findings
    • Mostly asymptomatic
    • Symptomatic patients:
      • Ecchymosis (esp. mucosal hemorrhages) → from reduced half-life of factor VIII
      • Petechial hemorrhages in severe cases → caused by impaired platelet aggregation
      • Other symptoms include epistaxis, gingival bleeding, menorrhagia; (∼ 50% of women with vWD), GI bleeding, and excessive bleeding during surgical procedures.
  • Diagnosis
    • Prolonged bleeding time and sometimes prolonged activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT)
    • ↓ Factor VIII levels
    • vWF-specific measurements
      • Quantitative assesment by vWF antigen assay: ↓ factor levels
      • Qualitative assesment by a collagen or glycoprotein Ib binding assay, or a a ristocetin cofactor activity assay: ↓ decreased binding, or ristocetin cofactor level < 30 IU/dL is considered definitive for vWD
  • Therapy: only if symptoms occur
    • Recombinant von Willebrand factor (rVWF) concentrate
    • Desmopressin
    • Other treatment options: factor VIII concentrates ; oral contraceptives in cases with menorrhagia; antifibrinolytic drugs (i.e., aminocaproic acid, tranexamic acid)
    • Avoid aspirin, NSAIDS, and intramuscular injections

Platelet aggregation inhibitors are contraindicated because of the increased risk of bleeding!

Disseminated intravascular coagulation

  • Also known as consumption coagulopathy; , it is a condition in which the procoagulant pathways are abnormally active, resulting in the formation of blood clots in the small blood vessels throughout the body.
  • For further information, see disseminated intravascular coagulation in the learning card on shock.

In disorders of primary hemostasis, platelet aggregation is impaired, whereas disorders of secondary hemostasis are caused by impairment of the coagulation cascade!!References:[1][2][3][4][5][6][7][8]

Pathophysiology

Activation of hemostasis

  • Primary hemostasis: : hemostasis achieved via platelet adhesion and aggregation at the site of endothelial injury
    1. Initiation: : An endothelial injury results in transient vasoconstriction. The subendothelial collagen is subsequently exposed, which causes vWF to bind to it. This initiates the primary hemostatic process, the end result of which is the formation of a platelet plug at this site. The platelets undergo the following steps to facilitate this process:
    2. Adhesion: : vWF and platelet GpIb receptors mediate the adhesion of platelets to the injured endothelium by forming pseudopodia; phospholipid is expressed on cellular membranes.
    3. Activation: release of adenine diphosphate (ADP), thromboxane, calcium, and platelet activating factor (PAF), which assist in platelet aggregation, vasoconstriction and degranulation
    4. Aggregation: mediated by GpIIb/IIIa-receptor and fibrinogen → formation of a white thrombus composed of platelets and fibrin
  • Secondary hemostasis: hemostasis achieved via the interaction of plasma coagulation factors (coagulation cascade)
  1. Injury to endothelium → activation of the extrinsic pathway (hemostasis)
    • Tissue factor ; (factor III), which is present under the endothelium on fibroblasts, binds to and thus activates factor VII.
    • Factor VIIa; and tissue factor ; form a complex (TF-FVIIa)* that activates factor X
    • Factor Xa and factor Va; form a complex* that activates thrombin.
  2. Additionally, activation of the intrinsic pathway (hemostasis), especially through thrombin
    • Thrombin activates factors XI and VIII.
      • Factor XIa activates factor IX.
      • Factors IXa and VIIIa; form a complex* that activates factor X.
    • This causes a positive feedback loop of factor X and thrombin activation via the intrinsic pathway
  3. The common pathway of the extrinsic and intrinsic pathways then follows:
    • Factor Xa and factor Va form a complex* that cleaves prothrombin to thrombin (= factor II).
    • Thrombin cleaves ; fibrinogen (factor I) into soluble ; fibrin (factor 1a) monomers.
    • Cross links of the fibrin network are stabilized by factor XIIIa → formation of a fibrin network → fibrin closely binds to the platelet plug, forming a stable fibrin clot (secondary or red thrombus)

(*) The formation of complexes marked with an asterisk is mediated by Ca2+ on the (negative) surface of the fibrocytes/active platelets.

The coagulation cascade requires the presence of calcium ions (factor IV)!

Inhibition of hemostasis

  • Extrinsic pathway: tissue factor pathway inhibitor, which inhibits tissue factor
  • Intrinsic pathway
    • Protein S: cofactor of protein C
    • Protein C: : forms the activated protein C; complex together with its cofactor protein S; (APC complex; ) → inhibition of factors Va and VIIIa; → inhibition of coagulation cascade
      • Produced in the liver; synthesis requires vitamin K
      • Shorter half-life than the other coagulation factors dependent on vitamin K
      • Clinical relevance: APC resistance; treatment with vitamin K antagonists
    • Antithrombin
      • One of the most important physiological inhibitors of hemostasis. Degrades thrombin and other coagulation factors (factor IXa and Xa) and activates tissue plasminogen activator (tPA).
      • Clinical relevance: The efficacy of antithrombin may be affected by various factors.
    • Others
      • Protein Z (factor X inhibitor)
      • Heparin-like glycosaminoglycans (boosts antithrombin)
      • Heparin cofactor II (requires heparin for activation)
      •
  • Nonspecific inhibitors of hemostasis: protease inhibitors found in plasma (e.g., alpha-1-antitrypsin, alpha-2-macroglobulin)

Diseases that affect the inhibitors of the coagulation cascade may lead to hypercoagulability!

  • For further information, see risk factors for deep vein thrombosis.

Fibrinolysis

Since the fibrinolytic system continuously cleaves and degrades fibrin, hemostasis and fibrinolysis are processes that occur simultaneously in the circulatory system.

  • Physiological fibrinolysis
    • Tissue injury → release of factor XII; , urokinase and tissue plasminogen activator (tPA)
    • → Breakdown of plasminogen, leading to the formation of active plasmin → breakdown of fibrin by activated plasmin
    • → Release of degradation products, including D-dimers
  • Regulation: Fibrinolysis and tPA activity are reduced if tPA binds to plasminogen activator inhibitor (PAI).

Diagnostics

Coagulation studies

Clinicians can use several tests to assess the functional adequacy of the different steps of hemostasis.

Diagnosing defects of primary hemostasis

  • Abnormal platelet count
  • Abnormal bleeding time
    • Time it takes for hemostasis to occur after lancet puncture
    • Can be used to assess both quantitative and qualitative platelet pathologies
    • Reference value: depending on the testing protocol, about 2–7 minutes

Diagnosing defects of secondary hemostasis

Additional diagnostic measures

  • Rumpel-Leede test: : detect platelet aggregation defects (primary hemostasis)
    • Procedure: Apply a blood pressure cuff to the upper arm → pressure should be around 90 mm Hg → remove cuff after 5 minutes → no petechial bleeding should occur
      • Positive results: more than 10 areas with petechial bleeding distal to the cuff
      • Interpretation: yields diagnostic clues for platelet disorders, platelet deficiency, or vascular hemorrhagic diathesis (Osler-Weber-Rendu disease, Henoch-Schönlein purpura); also positive in scarlet fever and Dengue fever
  • Anti-factor Xa: Measuring anti-factor Xa activity may be helpful in the following circumstances:
    • Therapy with low molecular weight heparin (LMWH) or fondaparinux in patients with impaired renal function
    • Therapy with unfractionated heparin if aPTT is inconclusive
  • D-dimers: correlate with activity of coagulation and fibrinolysis
    • Clinical relevance: rule out venous thrombosis, pulmonary embolism, and disseminated intravascular coagulation
  • If hyperfibrinolysis is suspected
    • Clot observation test: simple method of point-of-care testing to assess coagulation, especially if hyperfibrinolysis is suspected
      • Procedure: A small amount of the patient's blood is collected in a test tube and examined macroscopically after a few minutes.
        1. Stable thrombus → normal coagulation
        2. No coagulation → incoagulability
        3. Development of an unstable thrombus that dissolves again shortly after forming → hyperfibrinolysis
    • Thromboelastogram full blood assay that assesses the formation, stability, and dissolution of a thrombus alongside prothrombin time and aPTT

Treatment

Disorders of primary hemostasis

  • Platelet deficiency, platelet disorders, platelet coagulation: Treat the underlying condition and control symptoms (see thrombocytopenia).
  • Disorders affecting the vessel walls
    • Henoch-Schönlein purpura: supportive care (see HSP for details)
    • Thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura: prompt plasma exchange therapy (see TTP for details)

Disorders of secondary hemostasis

  • Disorders of the intrinsic pathway
    • Hemophilia A; (factor VIII deficiency) and hemophilia B; (factor IX deficiency): substitution of clotting factors
    • For systemic lupus erythematosus: autoantibodies directed against individual coagulation factors
  • Disorders of the extrinsic pathway
    • Deficiency of vitamin K-dependent coagulation factors
      • Intramuscular injection vitamin K
      • Prothrombin complex concentrate; (PCC), fresh frozen plasma (FFP)
    • Cirrhosis: Treat the underlying condition (see cirrhosis).

Other

  • Hyperfibrinolysis: tranexamic acid
  • Disseminated intravascular coagulation: Treat the underlying condition (see DIC).
  • See von Willebrand disease.

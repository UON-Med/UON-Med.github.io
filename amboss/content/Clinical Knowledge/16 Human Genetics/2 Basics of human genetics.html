Basics of human genetics

Abstract

Human genetics is the study of the human genome and how genes are transmitted through generations. The human genome consists of 23 pairs of chromosomes (22 pairs of homologous chromosomes and one pair of sex chromosomes), each containing genes that code for proteins within the cell. On all homologous chromosome pairs, there are two forms of the same gene known as alleles, which are passed on from parent to offspring. Heritable diseases are also passed down from parent to offspring via different patterns of inheritance, such as autosomal dominant, autosomal recessive, X-linked, and mitochondrial. These diseases often result from alterations within an individual's genes called mutations. Although some mutations are benign, many cause cellular dysfunction that manifests as disease. Diagnostic studies for chromosome and gene analysis include karyotyping, fluorescence in situ hybridization, sequencing, and polymerase chain reaction. Understanding the mechanisms underlying transmission of genetic material is vital for understanding and treating diseases that have a genetic component.

Basic concepts of genetics

Chromosomes

  • Each human cell contains 23 pairs of chromosomes; , 46 in total:
    • 22 pairs of autosomes, each containing two homologous (similar) chromosomes
    • 1 allosome (sex chromosome) pair, which consists of either two X chromosomes (♀) or one X and one Y chromosome (♂)
    • A chromosome pair contains one chromosome from each parent.
  • Depending on the cell cycle phase, a chromosome contains one or two chromatids (DNA double helix with associated packaging proteins)
    • During cell division, the centromere divides the chromatids into a short p arm and a long q arm.
    • The genetic information of the two chromatids of a chromosome is identical (with the exception of individual mutations that may occur during cell division).

Genes

  • A gene ; is a section of the information-containing DNA.
    • In all homologous chromosome pairs, there are two copies of the same gene (one on each maternal and one on each paternal chromosome). These copies are called alleles.
    • If the two alleles of a gene are identical, these are known as homozygotes (homozygous expression of a characteristic).
    • If the two alleles of a gene are non-identical, these are known as heterozygotes (heterozygous expression of a characteristic).

Genetic disease

  • Penetrance: indicates how likely a disease is to phenotypically manifest in a person carrying the altered gene
  • Expressivity: refers to how severe a disease will be once it has manifested
  • Compound heterozygosity: If the same gene on both chromosomes is altered (due to differently mutated alleles) a loss-of-function of the affected gene may occur despite the mutation not being homozygous.

Diagnostic procedures

  • Karyogram: used to visualize and analyze the full set of chromosomes under light microscopy
    • Karyotype: evaluation of the karyogram according to the number and structure of the chromosomes
      • The total number of chromosomes is stated, followed by the type of sex chromosome present; anomalies, if present, are mentioned last.
      • Example: (46, XX) for a normal female karyotype; (47, XY+21) for a male with trisomy 21; (45, X0) for a female with Turner syndrome
  • Fluorescence in situ hybridization(FISH): used to simplify the assessment of a karyogram or to provide evidence of structural chromosomal aberrations in tissue samples
    • Example: detection of microdeletions; (2–3×106 base pairs) such as in DiGeorge syndrome; evidence of a Philadelphia chromosome translocation t(9;22).
  • DNA microarray: (or array comparative genome hybridization/CGH): for the detection of microdeletions and duplications
  • Sequencing: determination of the exact sequence of base pairs of a gene; e.g., to demonstrate an unknown mutation on a disease-causing gene
  • Polymerase chain reaction: (PCR): method of amplifying fragments of DNA

Types of mutations

Mutations according to the affected cell population

Based on the affected cells, mutations can be differentiated as follows:

  • Germline mutation: (gametic mutation): mutations that are passed on to offspring via the ova or sperm
  • Somatic mutation: : do not occur in the germline and therefore cannot be passed on to offspring via the ova or sperm; almost all malignancies are preceded by a somatic mutation
  • Mosaic: mutation affecting only one population of the body's cells
    • In chromosomal mosaicism, cell populations with different karyotypes are present in one organism.
    • Example: In Turner syndrome; , gonosomal mosaicism is frequently present (several cells with X0; , several cells with XX).
  • Chromosomal instability: caused by mutations in genes that are responsible for DNA repair
    • Results in an increase in chromosomal translations, inversions, and deletions. Without repair genes, errors in cell division are never fixed and are passed down through the cell line.
    • Occurrence: Fanconi anemia, ataxia-telangiectasia

Chromosomal aberrations

Chromosomal aberrations are changes in the genome, which are mostly detectable in a karyogram.

  • Numerical chromosomal aberrations: altered number of chromosomes
    • Aneuploid: Individual chromosomes are multiplied or absent.
      • Trisomies: trisomy 21, trisomy 13, trisomy 18, trisomy 8, trisomy 9
      • Monosomies: Turner syndrome (45, X0)
      • Polysomies of the gonosomes: Klinefelter syndrome (47, XXY), XYY syndrome, triple X syndrome
    • Polyploid: The entire chromosome set is multiplied, e.g., triploidy (3 x 23 = 69,XXY/XXX).
  • Structural chromosomal aberrations: altered chromosome structure with an identical number of chromosomes
    • Detection primarily via FISH
    • Deletion: loss of a chromosome segment, e.g., Cri-du-Chat syndrome (46,XX/XY del(5))
    • Duplication: duplication of a chromosome segment
    • Inversion: inversion of a chromosome segment (e.g., 46,XY,inv(3)(p23q27))
    • Chromosomal translocation: relocation of one chromosome segment onto another (nonhomologous) chromosome
      • Balanced translocation: sum of the genetic material remains identical → no phenotypical alterations
        • Offspring have an increased risk of an unbalanced translocation.
        • E.g., balanced Robertson translocation (45,XY/XX rob(14;21))
      • Unbalanced translocation: The sum of the genetic material is altered.
        • Deletion or duplication of the chromosome segments
        • Normal number of chromosomes, may have altered phenotype
  • Uniparental disomy
    • Offspring has two copies of one chromosome from a single parent and no copies from the other parent.
      • Heterodisomy: two different homologous chromosomes; from one parent passed to offspring → error in meiosis I
      • Isodisomy: identical homologous chromosomes; from one parent passed to offspring → error in meiosis II
    • Should be considered if individual presents with an autosomal recessive condition when only one parent is a carrier
    • Usually results in normal phenotype
    • Uncommon cause of Angelman syndrome and Prader-Willi syndrome (see “Imprinting” below)

Uniparental disomy cannot be detected via karyotyping because the number of chromosomes is normal with no loss of genetic material!

Gene mutations

  • Various types of gene mutations include:
    • Point mutation: alteration of a single DNA base pair
    • Deletion: loss of one or several base pairs
    • Insertion: addition of one or several base pairs
    • Substitution: One or several base pairs are replaced by one or several different base pairs.
    • Trinucleotide repeat expansion: increased repetition of base triplets: that leads to faulty protein synthesis or folding (e.g., fragile X syndrome, Huntington's disease, myotonic dystrophy)
  • Gene mutations can be differentiated depending on the outcome:
    • Frameshift mutation: shift in the reading frame caused by insertion or deletion of a number of nucleotides not divisible by 3, which leads to modified amino acid coding in the gene segments downstream
    • Silent mutation: altered codon, which codes for the identical amino acids
    • Nonsense mutation: formation of a stop codon, which leads to alterations in the splicing process and early termination of translation
    • Missense mutation: altered codon, which codes for a different amino acid

Nonsense and frameshift mutations usually change the fundamental structure of the coded protein, so they typically lead to more severe disease manifestations than missense and silent mutations!

Regulation of gene expression

Imprinting

  • Definition: : a mechanism of gene regulation in which a gene is silenced on one chromosome and expressed on the other
  • Mechanism: : humans usually inherit two working copies of each gene, expressed by both maternal and paternal chromosomes; → some genes are epigenetically silenced via methylation on one chromosome → depending on the gene, either the maternal or paternal chromosome is inactivated
  • Examples: Angelman syndrome, Prader-Willi syndrome

Multifactorial inheritance disorders

  • Definition: disorders which are influenced by various factors; (e.g., genetics; , external influences like lifestyle; and environment)
  • Index case: first person within a family to manifest with the disease
  • Carter effect: risk of recurrence within a family is higher if the sex of the index case is empirically less frequently affected

Pedigree analysis

Autosomal dominant inheritance

  • Leads to disease even if only one allele is altered
  • Usually due to mutations in structural genes
  • If one parent is affected, every child has a 50% risk of inheriting the altered allele and thereby the disease.
  • Examples: polycystic kidney disease, achondroplasia, Huntington disease, Marfan syndrome, Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, myotonic dystrophy

An autosomal dominant disease with complete penetrance will always manifest in each generation!


Autosomal recessive inheritance

  • Disease occurs only if both alleles are altered.
    • Inheritance either via homozygosity for the altered allele or compound heterozygosity
    • Examples: cystic fibrosis, phenylketonuria, autosomal recessive polycystic kidney disease
  • Heterozygote (healthy) carriers of a recessive disease are known as carriers and show no phenotypic evidence of the disease.
  • If both parents are heterozygote carriers, the offspring have a 25% probability of inheriting the disease, a 50% probability of becoming a disease carrier, and a 25% probability of being unaffected (see table below).

X-linked recessive inheritance

  • The allele responsible for the disease is located on the X chromosome.
  • Women are usually carriers; and, in rare cases, only affected if both X chromosomes; carry the altered allele!
    • Children of a carrier have a 50% probability of inheriting the altered X chromosome.
      • Sons develop the disease, daughters are carriers
      • The probability that a carrier and healthy father conceive a phenotypically diseased child is 25%.
  • Men, whose only X chromosome carries the altered allele, always develop the disease!
    • All daughters inherit the altered X chromosome → carriers
    • All sons inherit the healthy Y chromosome → do not carry any altered allele and are healthy
  • Clinical examples: color blindness, hemophilia A/B, G6PD deficiency, Duchenne muscular dystrophy, Becker muscular dystrophy

In X-linked recessive inheritance, all male offspring of the affected father (and a healthy mother) are healthy, do not carry the altered allele, and therefore cannot pass on the altered allele!

X-linked dominant inheritance

  • The allele responsible for the disease is located on the X chromosome.
  • Both men and women are affected.
  • Affected mothers have a 50% risk of passing the altered allele on to their offspring (regardless of gender).
  • Affected fathers pass the altered allele to all daughters and no sons.
  • Clinical examples: Rett syndrome, Alport syndrome, hypophosphatemic rickets

Mitochondrial inheritance

  • Mitochondrial DNA is maternally inherited; → diseases caused by mutations in mitochondrial DNA only passed down from mother to offspring
  • Any offspring of an affected mother may show signs of the disease.
  • Heteroplasmy
    • Each mitochondrion has multiple strands of DNA, and each cell has many mitochondria. Normally, all of the DNA within these mitochondria is the same. If a mutation arises, it tends to only occur in a select population of mitochondrial DNA strands. This heterogeneity among mutated and normal DNA is known as heteroplasmy.
    • Disease severity often correlates to the proportion of mutated DNA strands.
  • Clinical examples: mitochondrial myopathies (e.g., MELAS syndrome), exercise intolerance

Heterozygote frequency

  • Definition: proportion of heterozygote carriers of an altered allele in the population; used to estimate a child's risk of a recessive inherited disorder if the genotype of the parents is unknown
  • The heterozygote frequency is calculated in accordance with the Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium: (p+q)2 = p2 + 2pq + q2 = 1 (100%)
    • q = probability of carrying an altered allele; p = probability of carrying an unaltered allele
    • q2 = probability of carrying two altered alleles; (= homozygote for the altered allele) p2 = probability of carrying two unaltered alleles (= homozygote for the unaltered allele)
    • 2pq = heterozygote frequency (= probability of carrying both an altered and an unaltered allele)
  • Example: cystic fibrosis with an incidence of ∼ 1:2,500
    1. As cystic fibrosis is a recessive disorder, only homozygote carriers develop the disease. The incidence corresponds to the homozygote frequency q2.
    2. Calculate q: q2 = 1/2500 → q = 1/50 = 2%
    3. Calculate p: (p + q)2 = 1 → p + q = 1 → p = 1 - 1/50 = 0.98 (98%) ≈ 1
    4. Calculate 2pq: 2pq = 2×1×1/50 = 1/25 heterozygote frequency
